**Definition 6.1.** A circle is a set of points in a plane having the same distance from a fixed point.

- The fixed point, O is called the **center** of the circle.
- The distance between the center of the circle and any point of the circle, r is called the **radius** (plural: radii) of the circle.

**Note:** A capital letter is used to designate the center of the circle. If the center of a circle is designated by the letter O, the circle is referred to as circle O.

Several basic terms with which you should be familiar with are associated with circles. These terms are defined below and illustrated in Figure 6.2.

**Definition 6.2**

a) A **chord** of a circle is a line segment joining two points of the circle. Thus, in the figure, $\overline{AB}$ is a chord.  
b) A **diameter** of a circle is a chord through the center. Thus, $\overline{CD}$ is a diameter of circle O.  
c) A **secant** of a circle is a line that intersects the circle at two points. Thus, $\overleftrightarrow{EF}$ is a secant.  
d) A **tangent** of a circle is a line that touches the circle at one and only one point. Thus, $\overleftrightarrow{GH}$ is a tangent to the circle at P. The point P is called the **point of tangency** or point of contact.  
e) The distance around a circle is called the **circumference** of the circle.

**Figure 6.2:** A circle with center O showing a chord AB, diameter CD, secant EF, and tangent GH at point of tangency P.

**Note:**
- All radii of a circle are congruent.
- A diameter of a circle consists of two radii that lie on the same straight line.

**Example 6.1:** In circle O, radius $\overline{OA} = 3x - 10$ and radius $\overline{OB} = x + 2$. Find the length of a diameter of circle O.

**Solution:** Since all radii of a circle are congruent,
- $OA = OB$
- $3x - 10 = x + 2$
- $3x = x + 12$
- $x = 6$
- $OA = OB = x + 2 = 6 + 2 = 8$

Therefore, the length of a diameter of circle O is $2 \times 8 = 16$.

### Arcs and its Type

**Figure 6.4:** Circle O showing points A and B creating arcs.

**Definition 6.3:** An arc of a circle is a part of the circumference of a circle. The symbol for an arc is $\widehat{}$. Thus $\widehat{AB}$ stands for arc AB.

In figure 6.5, A, B, and C are points on circle O and $\angle AOB$ intersects the circle at two distinct points, A and B, separating the circle into two arcs.

1. If $m\angle AOB \leq 180^\circ$, points A and B and the points of the circle in the interior of $\angle AOB$ make up **minor arc AB**, written as $\widehat{AB}$.  
2. Points A and B and the points of the circle not in the interior of $\angle AOB$ make up **major arc AB**. Usually a major arc is named by three points: The two end points and any other point on the major arc. Thus, the major arc with end points A and B is written as $\widehat{ACB}$.  
3. A **semi-circle** is an arc of a circle whose end points are the end points of a diameter of the circle; its measure is $180^\circ$.

**Figure 6.5:** Circle O showing minor arc AB, major arc ACB, and central angle AOB.

**Note:** A chord of a circle subtends the arcs which it cut off on the circle. The arc of a circle cut off by a chord is subtended by the chord. In figure 6.6, arc $\widehat{ADB}$ is subtended by chord $\overline{AB}$.

**Definition 6.4:** A **sector** of a circle is the part of a circle bounded by two radii and their intercepted arc. In the figure, region AOB is called a **minor sector** and region ADB is called a **major sector**.

**Definition 6.5:** A **segment** of a circle is the part of a circle bounded by a chord and its subtended arc. Regions ABC and ADB are called segments. Region ABC is the **minor segment** and region ADB is called the **major segment**.

**Figure 6.7:** Circle O showing sector AOB and segment ABC.

**Competencies:** At the end of this sub-topic, students should:

We now extend the discussion to angles whose vertices lie at the center called **central angle** and whose vertices lie on the circle called **inscribed angle**.

**Definition 6.6.** A **central angle** is an angle whose vertex is the center of the circle and whose sides are radii of the circle. In figure 6.12, $\angle AOB$ is a central angle. The reflex angle AOB is called the **reflex central angle**.

**Figure 6.12:** Circle O showing central angle AOB.

**Note:** From figure 6.12, arc $\widehat{ACB}$ is said to be intercepted by $\angle AOB$ and $\angle AOB$ is said to be subtended by arc $\widehat{ACB}$. A central angle is measured by its intercepted arc. That is, the measure of an arc of a circle may also be related to an angle whose vertex is the center of the circle and whose sides intercept arcs of the circle. In figure 6.13, $\angle AOB$ is called a central angle. The minor arc that lies in the interior of the angle is defined to have the same measure as its central angle, $\angle AOB$.

The measure of the central angle $\angle AOB$ is equal to the measure of the intercepted arc $\widehat{ACB}$. That is, $m\angle AOB = m\widehat{ACB}$.

**Figure 6.13:** Circle O showing relationship between central angle AOB and intercepted arc ACB.

**Example 6.2:** For the following figure, if $m\angle AOB = 67^\circ$ and $\overline{CD}$ is a diameter of circle O, then find:
   a) $m\widehat{AB}$
   b) $m\widehat{AC}$
   c) $m\widehat{ADB}$
   d) $m\widehat{BDC}$

**Figure 6.14:** Circle O with points A, B, C, D and central angle AOB = 67°.

**Solution:**
a) $m\widehat{AB} = m\angle AOB = 67^\circ$  
b) $m\widehat{AC} = m\angle AOC = 180^\circ - 67^\circ = 113^\circ$  
c) $m\widehat{ADB} = 360^\circ - m\widehat{AB} = 360^\circ - 67^\circ = 293^\circ$  
d) $m\widehat{BDC} = 180^\circ$

Let us now consider the situation in which the vertex of the angle is a point on the circle and the sides of the angle are chords of the circle.

**Definition 6.7.** An **inscribed angle** is an angle with its vertex on the circle and whose sides contain chords of the circle. In the figure, $\angle ABC$ is an inscribed angle for circle O.

**Figure 6.15:** Circle O showing inscribed angle ABC and intercepted arc AC.

**Note:** The arc formed by the intersection of the two sides of the angle and the circle is called an **intercepted arc**. From figure 6.15, $\angle ABC$ is an inscribed angle and arc $\widehat{AC}$ is an intercepted arc. We say that $\angle ABC$ is inscribed in the arc ABC and $\angle ABC$ is subtended by arc $\widehat{APC}$.

You know the measure of an arc is equal to the measure of the corresponding central angle, then how do the measures of inscribed angle ABC and its intercepted arc AC compare?

**Theorem 6.1.** The measure of an inscribed angle is one-half the measure of its intercepted arc.

In the figure, $m\angle ABC = \frac{1}{2} m\widehat{AC}$.

**Figure 6.16:** Circle O illustrating Theorem 6.1 with inscribed angle ABC and intercepted arc AC.

**Example 6.3:** Let $\overline{AC}$ be the diameter of circle O and $m\angle AOB = 112^\circ$ as shown in the figure. Then find $m\angle OAB$.

**Figure 6.17:** Circle O with diameter AC, central angle AOB = 112°, find angle OAB.

**Solution:**
- $m\widehat{AB} = m\angle AOB = 112^\circ$
- $m\widehat{BC} = m\widehat{ABC} - m\widehat{AB} = 180^\circ - 112^\circ = 68^\circ$
- $m\angle OAB = \frac{1}{2} m\widehat{BC} = \frac{1}{2} \times 68^\circ = 34^\circ$

**Example 6.4:** If $m\angle ABC = 75^\circ$ and $m\widehat{AD} = 60^\circ$ as shown in the figure, then find $m\widehat{DC}$.

**Figure 6.18:** Circle with inscribed angle ABC = 75° and arc AD = 60°, find arc DC.

**Solution:**
- $m\angle ABC = \frac{1}{2} m\widehat{ADC}$
- $75^\circ = \frac{1}{2} m\widehat{ADC}$
- $m\widehat{ADC} = 150^\circ$
- But $m\widehat{DC} = m\widehat{ADC} - m\widehat{AD} = 150^\circ - 60^\circ = 90^\circ$

**Example 6.5:** $\triangle ABC$ whose vertices lie on a circle so that its sides divide the circle into arcs whose measures have the ratio of 2:3:7. Find the measure of the smallest angle of the triangle.

**Figure 6.19:** Circle O with triangle ABC dividing circle into arcs with ratio 2:3:7.

**Solution:**
First, determine the measures of the arcs of the circle.
- $m\widehat{AB} + m\widehat{BC} + m\widehat{AC} = 360^\circ$
- $2x + 7x + 3x = 360^\circ$
- $12x = 360^\circ$
- $x = 30^\circ$

The measures of the arcs of the circle are:
- $m\widehat{AB} = 2x = 60^\circ$
- $m\widehat{BC} = 7x = 210^\circ$
- $m\widehat{AC} = 3x = 90^\circ$

Each angle of the triangle is an inscribed angle. The smallest angle lies opposite the arc having the smallest measure.
- Hence, $m\angle C = \frac{1}{2} m\widehat{AB} = \frac{1}{2} \times 60^\circ = 30^\circ$

**Theorem 6.2.** In a circle, inscribed angles subtended by the same arc are congruent.

That is $m\angle ABE = m\angle ACE$.

**Figure 6.20:** Circle O showing inscribed angles ABE and ACE subtended by the same arc AE.

**Example 6.6:** In figure 6.20, if $m\angle ACE = 65^\circ$, then find $m\angle ABE$.

**Solution:**
Since $\angle ACE$ is an inscribed angle and its measure is one-half of $\widehat{AE}$, then $m\widehat{AE} = 2 \times 65^\circ = 130^\circ$.

Similarly, $\angle ABE$ is an inscribed angle and its measure is one-half of $\widehat{AE}$, then $m\angle ABE = \frac{1}{2} \times 130^\circ = 65^\circ$.

Or by the above theorem, $\angle ACE$ and $\angle ABE$ intercept the same arc, then $m\angle ABE = m\angle ACE = 65^\circ$.

### 6.1.3 Angles formed by two intersecting chords

**Competencies:** At the end of this sub-topic, students should:
- Describe the angle formed by two intersecting chords.
- Solve problems related to angle formed by two intersecting chords inside a circle.

You know that the measure of an angle inscribed in a circle is one-half of the measure of the intercepted arc. In this sub-topic you will learn about the measure of an angle formed by two intersecting chords within a circle.

#### Activity 6.4

In figure 6.24, if $m\widehat{AD} = 72^\circ$ and $m\widehat{BC} = 48^\circ$, then find the value of $\angle APD$.

**Figure 6.24:** Circle O with intersecting chords AC and BD intersecting at P, with arcs AD = 72° and BC = 48°.

**Theorem 6.3.** The measure of an angle formed by two chords intersecting inside a circle is one-half the sum of the measures of the arcs intercepted by the angle and its vertical angle.

$m\angle APD = \frac{1}{2}(m\widehat{AD} + m\widehat{BC})$

**Figure 6.25:** Circle O with intersecting chords AB and CD intersecting at P, showing vertical angles APD and BPC.

**Figure 6.26:** Circle O with center O and intersecting chords.

**Example 6.7:** In figure 6.26 shown, O is the center, $m\angle ABD = 30^\circ$ and $m\widehat{DC} = 60^\circ$. Then find:
   a) $m\angle CPB$
   b) $m\angle BDK$
   c) $m\angle AQK$

**Solution:**
- $m\widehat{AD} = 2 \times m\angle ABD = 2 \times 30^\circ = 60^\circ$
- Since $\overline{AB}$ is a diameter, $m\widehat{AD} + m\widehat{DC} + m\widehat{CB} = 180^\circ$
- $m\widehat{CB} = 180^\circ - 120^\circ = 60^\circ$

a) $m\angle CPB = \frac{1}{2}(m\widehat{BC} + m\widehat{AD}) = \frac{1}{2}(60^\circ + 60^\circ) = 60^\circ$  
b) Since $\overline{BD}$ is a diameter, $m\widehat{DC} + m\widehat{CB} + m\widehat{BK} = 180^\circ$  
   $m\widehat{BK} = 180^\circ - 120^\circ = 60^\circ$  
   $m\angle BDK = \frac{1}{2} m\widehat{BK} = \frac{1}{2}(60^\circ) = 30^\circ$  
c) $m\widehat{AK} = 180^\circ - 60^\circ = 120^\circ$  
   $m\angle AQK = \frac{1}{2}(m\widehat{DC} + m\widehat{AK}) = \frac{1}{2}(60^\circ + 120^\circ) = 90^\circ$

**Example 6.8:** An angle formed by two chords intersecting within a circle is $54^\circ$, and one of the intercepted arcs measures $40^\circ$. Find the measures of the other intercepted arc.

**Solution:**
Consider figure 6.26.
- $m\angle BPC = \frac{1}{2}m\widehat{BC} + \frac{1}{2}m\widehat{AD}$
- $54^\circ = \frac{1}{2}(40^\circ) + \frac{1}{2}m\widehat{AD}$
- $108^\circ = 40^\circ + m\widehat{AD}$
- $m\widehat{AD} = 68^\circ$

**Example 6.9:** Prove that the measure of an angle formed by two chords intersecting in the interior of a circle is equal to one-half the sum of the measures of the two intercepted arcs.

**Proof:** Consider circle O with chords $\overline{AC}$ and $\overline{BD}$ intersect at point P. (refer to figure 6.26) $\angle APD$ is an exterior angle of $\triangle APB$.

Hence $m\angle DPB = m\angle A + m\angle B$ [sum of two remote interior angles]
- $= \frac{1}{2}m\widehat{BC} + \frac{1}{2}m\widehat{AD}$
- $= \frac{1}{2}(m\widehat{BC} + m\widehat{AD})$

## 6.2 Applications of Circle

**Competency:** At the end of this sub-topic, students should:
- Solve the application problems.

Circles are present in real life, both in the nature world such as Satellite's orbit around the earth, Mushrooms with domed caps and in manmade creations such as camera lenses, coins, cups, rings, wheel, roundabout, a sniper target grid etc. In this section we will discuss some real life applications of circles using examples.

**Example 6.10:** Which part of a circle can be found in a do not smoke sign when the line crosses the image of cigarette?

**Solution:** Diameter.

**Example 6.11:** Dawit observes that the time is 5:00 hrs. What is the angle between the hands of the clock?

**Solution:**
The small hand on a clock shows the hours on the face of a clock. So, if the hour hand lies on 6.00 hrs, then the angle between the hands is $180^\circ$.

$6 \text{ hr} = 180^\circ$  
$1 \text{ hr} = x$  
$x = \frac{1 \text{ hr} \times 180^\circ}{6 \text{ hr}} = 30^\circ$

Thus, 1 hr measures $30^\circ$.

Therefore, a 5 hr hand measures $5 \times 30^\circ = 150^\circ$.

**Example 6.12:** When you go to the movies, you want to be close to the movie screen, but you don't want to have to move your eyes too much to see the edges of the picture. If E and G are the ends of the screen and F is a viewing position, $m\angle EFG$ is called your viewing angle. You decide that the middle of the sixth row has best viewing angle.

If someone is sitting there, where else can you sit to have the same viewing angle?

**Solution:** Draw a circle that is determined by the end points of the screen and the sixth row center seat. Any other location on the circle will have the same viewing angle.

**Figure (conceptual):** A cinema screen with endpoints E and G, and multiple seating positions on a circular arc all having the same viewing angle.

## Summary for Unit 6

1. A circle is the set of all points in a plane having the same distance from a fixed point. The fixed point is called the center and the fixed distance is the radius of the circle.
2. A chord is a line segment whose end points lie on the circle.
3. A diameter is a chord that passes through the center of the circle.
4. A tangent line is a line that intersects a circle in exactly one point. The point of contact is called the point of tangency.
5. A secant line is a line that intersects a circle in two different points.
6. Every secant line includes a chord of the circle.
7. An arc is a curved portion of a circle.
8. A chord determines an arc of a circle. So, we say that the chord intercepts the arc.
9. Arcs are classified in the following ways:
   a) Semi-circle: an arc whose end points are also end points of a diameter of a circle.
   b) Minor arc: the part of a circle less than a semi-circle.
   c) Major arc: the part of a circle greater than a semi-circle.
10. A sector of a circle is the region bounded by two radii and an arc of the circle.
11. A segment of a circle is the region bounded by a chord and the arc of the circle.
12. A central angle is an angle whose vertex is at the center of the circle.
13. An inscribed angle is an angle whose vertex is on a circle and whose sides contain chords of the circle.
14. To prove a chord is a diameter show that the chord divides the circle into congruent (equal) parts.
15. The measure of an inscribed angle is equal to one-half of the measure of its intercepted arc.
16. The measure of an angle formed by two chords intersecting inside a circle is one-half of the sum of the measure of the arcs subtending the angle and its vertical opposite angle.
