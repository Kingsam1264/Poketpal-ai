## What are integumentary systems?

The integumentary systems are the organs of the body that form a physical barrier between the external environment and the internal body part that they serve to protect and maintain. The integumentary system includes the skin, associated glands, hair, and nails.

### 4.1.1 Components of Integumentary System

The integumentary systems are human body systems used to perform different types of functions such as protection and excretion. These systems have components like skin, hair, nail, and glands.

#### 1. Skin

The skin is the outer layer of the body that covers the internal parts of the body. Human skin has three major layers:

**A) Upper layer (Epidermis):**
This layer is the outermost layer of the skin, made up of dead cells. These reduce excess water loss, protect from light rays (UV light), and prevent entry of disease-causing microorganisms.

**B) Middle layer (Dermis):**
This layer contains blood vessels (responsible for thermoregulation), lymph vessels, sweat glands, sensory receptors, and hair follicles.

**C) Lower layer (Hypodermis):**
This layer contains fatty tissue used to store energy and acts as an insulation layer, protecting you from loss of excess heat.

#### 2. Hair

Hair comes from follicles, which are simple organs made up of cells called epithelial cells. Hair is made up of different structures and layers. The outer layer of hair is the shaft, made up of dead cells that turned into keratin and binding materials. The hair shaft is formed from three layers:

**A) Medulla:** Deepest layer of hair shaft, only seen in large and thick hairs.
**B) Cortex:** Middle layer of hair shaft which provides strength, color, and texture of hair fiber.
**C) Cuticle:** Outer layer of hair shaft is thin and colorless. It serves as protection of the cortex. These hairs help protect the skin, regulate body temperature, and aid in evaporation and perspiration.

**Figure 4.2: Layers of hair shaft**

#### 3. Nail

It is the hard covering at the end of the finger or toe. Like other body parts, it consists of several parts including:
- The nail plate: The part of the nail that is visible
- The nail bed: The skin that lies beneath the nail plate
- The cuticle: The thin line of tissue located at the base of the nail and overlaps the nail plate
- The nail folds: The folds of skin located on the sides of the nail plate
- The lunula: The white-colored half-moon-shaped area
- The matrix: Part of the nail that is not visible, located underneath the cuticle; this area is responsible for the growth of the nail

**Figure 4.3: Structures of human nail**  
The image illustrates the anatomy of a human fingernail, labeling the distal edge, lateral nail fold, cuticle, nail plate, nail bed, lunula, and matrix.

#### 4. Gland

A gland is an organ that makes one or more substances such as hormones, digestive juice, sweat, tears, saliva, or milk. Integumentary systems have four types of exocrine glands that secrete substances outside the cell and body:

- **Sudoriferous glands:** Sweat glands that are hollow, cylindrical structures under the skin that excrete sweat through small openings at the skin surface.
- **Sebaceous glands:** Very small tubular-shaped glands in the dermis used to release oil into the hair follicle to help lubricate and protect the hair shaft, keeping it from becoming hard and brittle.
- **Ceruminous glands:** Located in the ear canal and produce ear wax (medically called cerumen) for protection.
- **Mammary glands:** In females, these glands function to produce breast milk after giving birth.

**Key term:** Exocrine gland - A gland that releases its secretion through ducts or openings.

## 4.1.2 Functions of Integumentary Systems

The skin is a remarkably complex organ which carries out a number of important functions in your body. Some of these functions include serving as an enclosing barrier and providing environmental protection, regulating temperature, producing pigment and vitamin D, sensory perception, and homeostasis. Besides this, the skin also contains many sense organs for touch, temperature, pressure, and pain.

The primary function of human hair is to insulate the human body. It does this in two ways. First, it serves as a physical barrier between external cold air and the skin. Second, it traps warm air between the skin and the hair, keeping the body warmer. The nail is used to protect the upper end of each finger and toe from injury and also provides sensation with the sense of touch. Glands in the body are used to secrete different chemicals. Some types of glands are used to excrete wastes, cool down the body, release oil into the hair follicle, produce ear wax for protection, and produce breast milk for feeding infants.

## 4.1.3 Major Skin Diseases and Disorders

Skin diseases are a broad range of conditions affecting the skin, including diseases caused by bacterial infections, viral infections, fungal infections, allergic reactions, skin cancers, and others.

Skin disorders vary greatly in symptoms and severity. They can be temporary or permanent, and may be painless or painful. While most skin disorders are minor, others can indicate a more serious issue. There are many different types of skin disorders. Here is a list:

### • Acne:
Commonly located on the face, neck, shoulders, chest, and upper back. May leave scars or darken the skin if untreated. Its symptoms are tips, large, solid, painful lumps under the skin. Caused when the pores of your skin become blocked with oil, dead skin, or bacteria.

### • Rosacea:
The skin condition rosacea is most commonly associated with redness. However, there are four subtypes that cause other symptoms as well.

### • Eczema:
These are a range of persistent skin conditions that include dryness and recurring skin rashes characterized by redness, skin swelling, itching and dryness, crusting, cracking, or bleeding.

### • Hives:
Hives are itchy welts that are raised up from the normal layer of the skin. It is frequently caused by allergic reactions in the body but can also be caused by outside factors such as stress, illnesses, or tight clothing.

**Figure 4.7: Hives**  
The image shows raised, itchy welts on skin typical of hives.

### • Warts:
Typically occur on humans' hands or feet but often in other locations. Caused by viral infections, especially by human papilloma virus (HPV). They often go away on their own, though unsightly warts can be treated with liquid nitrogen or medicated creams.

### • Cold sore:
A cold sore is a red, fluid-filled blister usually found near the mouth. The sore itself is painful or delicate. Other symptoms include itching or burning sensations on the site before the sore is visible. Cold sores are caused by the herpes simplex virus, and there is no known cure for the virus.

### • Carbuncle:
When Staphylococcus aureus bacteria make their way into the hair follicles and cause an infection, a carbuncle is produced. This is a red, irritated lump underneath the skin. Carbuncles can be nearly any size, and the lump quickly fills with pus and becomes swollen. Other symptoms include tiredness, itching at the site of the lump, and fever. Carbuncles are treated with antibiotics and antibacterial soaps.

### • Blister:
A blister is a bubble of fluid under the skin. The clear, watery liquid inside a blister is called serum. If the blister remains unopened, serum can provide natural protection for the skin beneath it. Blisters heal by:
1. Wash hands and blister with soap and warm water
2. Swab blister with iodine
3. Sterilize clean, sharp needle with rubbing alcohol

### • Actinic keratosis:
An actinic keratosis is a rough, scaly patch on the skin that develops from years of sun exposure. It's often found on the face, lips, ears, forearms, scalp, neck, or back of the hand.

### • Latex allergy:
Latex allergy is a reaction to certain proteins found in natural rubber latex, a product made from the rubber tree. Latex allergy may cause itchy skin and hives or even anaphylaxis, a potentially life-threatening condition that can cause throat swelling and severe difficulty breathing.

### • Chickenpox:
Chickenpox consists of an itchy, red rash that breaks out on the face, scalp, chest, back and, to a lesser extent, arms and legs. The spots quickly fill with a clear fluid, rupture and then turn crusty. Chickenpox is an infection caused by the varicella-zoster virus.

## What are muscular systems?

Muscles are tissues composed of cells called muscle fibers. Muscles are used to perform different types of body movements like pumping blood, stability, posture, digestion, circulation, breathing air in and out, etc. Muscular systems are the collection of these muscle tissues throughout the body.

**Figure 4.15: Muscular structures of human**  
The image shows the major muscle groups of the human body, including muscles of the arms, legs, torso, and face.

## 4.2.1 Components of Muscular System

There are three distinct components of muscular systems: skeletal muscles, cardiac or heart muscles, and smooth (non-striated) muscles.

### Skeletal muscle
Skeletal muscles are the only muscles that can be consciously or voluntarily controlled. Contracting these muscles causes movement of bones. Examples include chewing, talking, facial expressions, and drinking. They are found attached to the ends of bones.

### Smooth muscle
Smooth muscle lines the inside of vital organs, blood vessels, eyes, airways, and organs such as the stomach, digestive tract, reproductive tract, urinary tracts, and others. Smooth muscle acts involuntarily and cannot be consciously controlled.

### Cardiac muscle
Located only in the heart, cardiac muscle pumps blood around the body by involuntary action. They are auto-rhythmic, meaning they are capable of contracting spontaneously without hormonal stimulation. Cardiac muscle stimulates its own contractions that form our heartbeat.

**Key term:** Auto-rhythmic - Capable of contracting spontaneously without hormonal stimulation.

**Did you know?** The muscles account for around 40 percent of a person's weight, with the largest muscle in the body being the gluteus maximus in the buttocks.

## 4.2.2 Function of Muscular Systems

The muscular system consists of various types of muscle that each plays a crucial role in the function of the body. Muscles allow a person to move, speak, and chew. They control heartbeat, breathing, and digestion. All muscle cells share different properties for functions. Some of these properties are:

- **Contractility:** The ability of muscle cells to forcefully shorten. When you contract or flex, the angle of your joint decreases as your elbow contracts or shortens. Muscles can only pull but never push.
- **Extensibility:** The power of the muscle to be stretched. In the human body, the extensibility of muscle is very high in adults. But when age increases, extensibility reduces, which is called spasticity.
- **Excitability:** The ability of muscle to respond to stimuli that come from motor neurons and hormones.
- **Elasticity:** The quality or state of being elastic and the capability of a strained body to recover its size and shape after deformation.

## 4.2.3 Major Muscle Disease and Disorders

Muscle disorders are diseases that affect the human muscle system, with main manifestation being skeletal muscle weakness. They can be caused by different factors and make the muscle weak, reducing its strength. There are many disorders, but some are:

### • Muscular dystrophy
A group of inherited diseases characterized by weakness and wasting away of muscle tissue, with or without the breakdown of nerve tissue. Its different types involve an eventual loss of strength, increasing disability, and possible death.

### • Muscle strains
A muscle strain is an injury to a muscle or a tendon (the fibrous tissue that connects muscles to bones). Sometimes called pulled muscles, strains commonly occur in the lower back and in the muscles at the back of the thigh.

The skeletal system interacts with other systems by holding up your body and giving it support. The muscular system connects bones to bones, and the nervous system controls our movements.

## 4.3.1 Structural Components of Skeletal System

The skeletal system consists of bones and connective tissue, including cartilage, tendons, and ligaments. It's also called the musculoskeletal system.

### A. Bone
Bone is mineralized connective tissue that contains collagen and minerals like calcium phosphate and mineral crystals. Because of calcium phosphate, bones are firm, compact or spongy, and provide protection of body organs. They are made up of living cells and are called living organs. An adult human skeleton contains 206 bones.

#### Types of bone

Bones are a major component of the skeletal system. Based on their shape, bones can be divided into four major groups:

1. **Long bones:** Hard and compact, tubular, filled with yellow bone marrow. Examples: Upper and lower limbs, arm, leg, finger, and thigh.

2. **Short bones:** Compact bones with chambers or partitions but without marrow. Examples: Wrist, finger, toes, and ankle.

3. **Flat bones:** Thin, flat, compact, and typically curved. They have no cavity or hollow. Examples: Ribs, sternum, cranial bones, and scapula.

**Figure 4.20: Flat bone**

4. **Irregular bones:** Typically thin membranes which have nerves and blood vessels that nourish the bone. On their surface there are small holes for entry and exit of materials into and out of bone. Examples: Hip bones, facial bones, and vertebrae.

### B. Cartilage
Cartilage provides flexible and elastic structural support for certain structures in adult humans, including the nose, trachea, and joints.

### C. Tendon
A tendon is a fibrous band of connective tissue that is bonded to bone and connects muscle to bone.

### D. Ligament
A ligament is a stretchy and flexible band of tissue which holds bones together at joints. It is firm, rubbery tissue.

### E. Joints
A joint is a site where two or more bones or other skeletal components are joined together. It has two main jobs: keeping bones apart and holding bones in place as they move or rotate.

#### Types of joints

There are two different types of joints: immovable and movable joints.

1. **Immovable joints:** Fixed in place and do not move.
2. **Moving joints:** Permit movement up and down or twist and bend in some directions. Grouped into:
   - **Hinge joint:** Looks like a hinge on a door which permits front and back movements in a single direction. Examples: elbow, knee, finger, and toe joints.
   - **Ball and socket joint:** Made from the round end of one bone fitting into a cup-shaped socket in another. These permit movement in every direction. Examples: shoulder and hip joints.

## 4.3.2 Functions of Skeletal System

Human skeleton extends throughout the body like other vertebrates which have a spine for supporting the body, skull protecting the brain, and ribs protecting the heart and lungs. Functions of skeletal systems include:

- **Movement:** The skeleton supports body weight to help you stand and move. Joints, connective tissue, and muscles work together to make body parts mobile.
- **Produces blood cells:** Bone contains bone marrow, which is the site of blood cell formation (white blood cells and red blood cells) in normal adults.
- **Protection and support:** Skeletal systems mainly protect and support the body, giving it form and structure.
- **Storage:** Stores different types of minerals like mineral salts, calcium, fats in bone marrow, and calcium phosphate needed for blood clotting, nerve function, and muscle contraction.

## 4.3.3 Major Diseases of Skeletal System

There are a number of diseases that can affect the human skeletal system:

- **Leukemia:** Cancer of the body's blood-forming tissues, including bone marrow and the lymphatic system.
- **Osteopenia, osteitis deformans, and osteomalacia:** Similar to osteoporosis, these are other types of bone loss.
- **Osteoporosis:** Literally means 'porous bone'. It is a condition where bones become thin and lose strength as they become less dense and their quality is reduced.
- **Osteoarthritis:** Involves degradation of joints. Symptoms include joint pain, tenderness, stiffness, locking, and sometimes an effusion.
- **Fracture:** A break in the continuity of a bone, which may be partial or complete.

## What does it mean digestions?

Digestion is the process in which larger, complex, hard, and insoluble food substances are changed into smaller, simpler, easier, and soluble forms by the action of digestive organs. Such food molecules can be easily used by body cells. The activity of digestion involves different digestive enzymes and digestive juices.

## 4.4.1 Structural Components of Digestive System

The digestive system is an organ system made up of the alimentary canal, which includes several organs: mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus. It also has associated organs such as salivary glands, liver, and pancreas.

The two types of digestion are physical and chemical digestion. Physical digestion includes cutting and grinding food molecules in the mouth by teeth.

## 4.4.2 Functions of Digestive System

The major function of the digestive system is the breakdown of food molecules into smaller, usable forms. The muscular contraction and relaxation of the wall of the alimentary canal produce movements of digested food along in one direction.

### Mouth
It is the first anterior opening part of the gut which contains teeth. In the mouth, both types of digestion occur: physical (mechanical) and chemical digestion.

### Teeth
Teeth of humans and other animals are hard structures that grow from jaw bone. They are used to bite and chew food. Each tooth consists of crown, neck, and root.

The true human teeth are made up of three layers:
1. **Enamel:** The outer layer, white and the hardest substance in the body.
2. **Dentine:** The living layer under the tooth enamel.
3. **Pulp cavity:** The central part of the tooth. It contains living tissue with blood vessels and nerves which supply the living tooth with nutrients.

**Figure 4.34: Human teeth structure**  
The image shows a cross-section of a human tooth labeling enamel, dentine, pulp cavity, root canal, cement, and blood vessels.

Mammals have two sets of teeth in their life. In humans, the first set has 20 teeth called milk teeth. These appear first but loosen and fall out when a child is about 6 years old and are replaced by the second or permanent teeth, which are not replaced when once decayed or lost. The permanent teeth set has 28 teeth. When a person is around 20-25 years old, four additional back teeth grow called wisdom teeth. This development and arrangement of teeth is known as dentition.

#### Types of teeth
Humans have four types of teeth:

1. **Incisors (I):** Chisel-shaped frontal teeth with sharp edges. There are four on each jaw, used for biting, cutting, and grinding food.
2. **Canines (C):** Long, sharp, pointed teeth located beside incisors. There are two on each jaw, used to tear and pierce food. They are prominent in predator organisms like lions.
3. **Premolars (P):** Located behind canines with relatively flat edges. There are four in each jaw, used for chewing, crushing, or grinding food.
4. **Molars (M):** Wider and stronger ridges with depressions. There are six in each jaw. Their function is similar to premolars.

**Figure 4.35: Types of human teeth**  
The image shows the arrangement of teeth in upper and lower jaws, including central incisor, lateral incisor, canine, first premolar, second premolar, first molar, second molar, and wisdom tooth/third molar.

**Dental formula** represents the number, type, and arrangement of teeth. It shows the number and type of teeth in one half of the upper and lower jaws. Teeth on the upper jaw are listed above those on the lower jaw, listed as: incisors (I), canines (C), premolars (P), and molars (M). Therefore, the dental formula of adult humans is: I=2/2, C=1/1, P=2/2, M=3/3.

The total number of teeth in a given mammal can be calculated by adding the number of teeth in upper and lower jaws, then multiplying each jaw by two, and finally summing them up.

### Stomach
The stomach is a muscular bag that produces enzymes like pepsin for protein digestion. It also produces hydrochloric acid.

### Small intestine
The first section of the small intestine, called the duodenum, is where food joins with two liquids: bile and pancreatic juice.

Bile is made by liver cells and stored in the gall bladder until needed. Bile has two important functions:
1. Neutralize acid from the stomach and make the semi-digested food alkaline.
2. Emulsify fats in the food, which means breaking down fat into smaller droplets.

The small intestine has many finger-like projections called villi for absorption of digested food into blood.

### Where do the digestion end products go next?
Digestion end products of protein are amino acids, and fats are broken into fatty acids and glycerol, entering the small intestine to be stored for short periods and absorbed into blood.

### Large intestine
It is the part of the alimentary canal between the small intestine and anus. Its main function is absorption of water and formation of feces. Undigested food substances unusable by cells are removed through the anus, which is the last part of the gut. This process is called defecation.

**Key term:** Defecation - Removal of wastes from body parts.

## 4.4.3 Major Diseases of Digestive System

The organs of the digestive system can be affected by disease-causing pathogens. Some diseases include:

### Constipation
If feces stay too long in the large intestine, too much water absorption takes place, causing constipation. The most common causes are lack of fiber food (roughage) and not drinking enough water.

- Drinking sufficient amount of water

**Key term:** Roughage - Fibrous indigestible material in vegetable foodstuffs which aids passage of food and waste products through the gut.

### Diarrhea
Also called watery feces. Caused by infection of the gut, making the gut contract more strongly and rapidly than usual. Can cause dehydration.

**Prevention:**
- Treat patients by giving enough water and salt to replace lost fluid
- Stool examination and early treatment with proper medicines

Swollen veins in the lower rectum and anus. Internal hemorrhoids are usually painless but tend to bleed. External hemorrhoids may cause pain. Also called piles.

- Eat high-fiber foods: more fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Drink plenty of fluids
- Do regular exercise

Inflammation, irritation, or erosion of the stomach lining. Can occur suddenly (acute) or gradually (chronic).

**Prevention:**
- Avoid known trigger foods
- Quit smoking
- Manage and reduce stress
- Avoid alcohol
- Maintain healthy weight
- Avoid abuse of over-the-counter pain medications

- Acute: Very serious, strong, sensitive, or dangerous

### Peptic ulcer disease
A condition where painful sores or ulcers develop in the stomach lining or first part of small intestine. Normally, a thick layer of mucus protects the stomach lining from digestive juices.

**Prevention:**
- Avoid tobacco products and alcohol
- Don't ignore ulcer symptoms
- Protect yourself from infections by washing hands regularly and consuming thoroughly cooked foods

# 4.5 Respiratory System

At the end of this section, you will be able to:

- Describe the main functions of human respiratory system

## What are respirations?

In single-celled and small living organisms like amoeba and paramecium, oxygen diffuses from air or water into the cell, and carbon dioxide diffuses out. But in larger organisms like humans, simple diffusion is not enough. Breathing is the process of bringing oxygen into the organism's body (inhalation) and removing waste carbon dioxide (exhalation).

**Key terms:**
- Inhalation: Taking oxygen into the body
- Exhalation: Removal of carbon dioxide out of the body

## 4.5.1 Structural Components of Respiratory System

Human respiratory systems have well-developed structures for breathing air. They include nose with large surface area, good blood supply, lots of hair, and mucus. Each structure has specific functions:

- **The hair:** Filters out dust particles and pathogens from inhaled air
- **The large surface area:** Moistens the air and increases humidity
- **The mucus:** Filters inhaled air and collects bacteria and dust
- **Good blood supply:** Helps warm the air we inhale

Organs of breathing include: nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli, and lungs. The trachea has series of incomplete rings of cartilage (C-shaped) that support it and hold it open. They are incomplete so you can swallow food.

**Key term:** Cellular respiration - Combination of food and oxygen inside the body.

**Figure 4.36: Human respiratory systems**  
The image shows respiratory organs: frontal sinus, nasal cavity, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli, and lungs, with the diaphragm at the base.

## 4.5.2 Functions of Respiratory System

Respiration is the process by which the body gets and uses oxygen and releases carbon dioxide and water. Respiration is divided into two parts:
1. **Breathing:** Involves inhaling and exhaling
2. **Cellular respiration:** Involves chemical reactions or burning of food by oxygen that releases energy

### Organ functions:
- **Pharynx:** Membrane-lined cavity behind nose and mouth connecting them to esophagus
- **Larynx:** Hollow muscular organ forming an air passage to lungs and holding vocal cords
- **Trachea:** Large membranous tube reinforced by rings of cartilage, extending from larynx to bronchial tubes
- **Bronchus:** Major air passages of lungs that diverge from windpipe
- **Bronchioles:** Smaller tubes branching from each bronchus in the lung
- **Alveoli:** Tiny air sacs in lungs where exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide takes place
- **Lung:** Provides place where oxygen can reach blood and carbon dioxide be removed. Lung shape is controlled by relaxation and contraction of diaphragm and intercostal muscles.

## 4.5.3 Major Diseases of Respiratory System

Respiratory diseases range from mild and self-limiting (common cold, influenza, pharyngitis) to life-threatening (bacterial pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, tuberculosis, acute asthma, lung cancer, severe acute respiratory syndromes).

### Asthma
Chronic lung disease marked by recurring episodes of airway obstruction manifested by labored breathing, accompanied by wheezing, coughing, and chest constriction, triggered by hyper-reactivity to various stimuli.

### Sinusitis
Painful swelling of tissues inside the sinus or nose due to infections, allergies, or autoimmune problems. Inflammation of the mucous membrane lining the paranasal sinuses.

### Influenza
Viral infection attacking the respiratory system (nose, throat, lungs). Commonly called flu, but different from stomach "flu" viruses that cause diarrhea and vomiting.

### Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
Group of diseases causing airflow blockage and breathing problems, including emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Mainly caused by smoking.

### Bronchitis
Inflammation of the lining of bronchial tubes, which carry air to and from the lungs.

# 4.6 Circulatory System

At the end of this section, you will be able to:
- Distinguish the major structural components of human circulatory system
- Describe the main functions of human circulatory system
- Identify and discuss the main diseases or disorders associated with human circulatory system

## What is a circulatory system?

Circulatory system is the system for transporting substances in the body of an organism. Mainly used to transport air, food, minerals, vitamins, and other liquid materials and solutions between cells. They also transport waste materials for removal from the body.

## 4.6.1 Components of Circulatory System

Blood flows from the heart to other body parts through blood vessels. The contraction and relaxation of heart muscle pump blood to different body parts. In humans, there are two types of blood circulation called double circulation:

1. **Systemic circulation:** Blood flows between heart and other body parts
2. **Pulmonary circulation:** Blood flows between heart and lungs only

**Figure 4.37: Double circulatory systems**  
The image shows blood flow patterns: Pulmonary circulation (between heart and lungs) and Systemic circulation (between heart and body tissues).

## 4.6.2 Functions of Circulatory System

Blood circulation systems consist of three elements: heart, blood vessels, and blood. In circulation, the left side of the heart always pumps oxygenated blood while the right side receives deoxygenated blood.

### A. Heart
It is the muscular blood-pumping organ made of involuntary cardiac muscle. The heart has four chambers:

1. **Right atrium:** Upper part receiving deoxygenated blood from body to pump to lungs. Has thin wall.
2. **Left atrium:** Upper part receiving oxygenated blood from lungs to pump to left ventricle.
3. **Right ventricle:** Lower chamber pumping deoxygenated blood into the lungs.
4. **Left ventricle:** Pumps blood at long distances throughout the body, so it has thicker, more muscular walls.

Inside the heart, oxygenated and deoxygenated blood cannot mix because chambers are separated by valves. Valves prevent backflow of blood.

**Figure 4.38: Parts of human circulatory systems**  
The image shows heart anatomy: superior vena cava, aorta, pulmonary arteries and veins, left and right atria, left and right ventricles, mitral valve, tricuspid valve, aortic valve, and pulmonary valve.

**Blood flow pathways:**
- Oxygenated blood: Lungs → pulmonary vein → left atrium → left ventricle → aorta → body tissues
- Deoxygenated blood: Body → vena cava → right atrium → right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs

### B. Blood Vessels
Pathways through which transportation of substances occurs. Three major groups:

1. **Artery:** Carries blood away from heart to different body parts. Have thick, elastic walls. Except pulmonary artery and umbilical artery, all arteries carry oxygenated blood.
2. **Vein:** Returns blood back to the heart. Most carry deoxygenated blood except pulmonary vein and umbilical vein.
3. **Capillaries:** Connect arteries and veins, carrying blood to tissues and cells. They are narrow with thin walls.

### C. Blood
One of three elements of circulation. Blood is fluid tissue that carries nutrients, respiratory gases, metabolic wastes, and other substances. Blood is made of 45% solid and 55% liquid called plasma. Plasma is 90% water and 10% dissolved substances like amino acids and glucose. The solid part consists of three blood cell types:

1. **Red blood cells (erythrocytes):** Disc-shaped, non-nucleated cells that transport oxygen and carbon dioxide. Have red pigment hemoglobin that carries oxygen. They are 6.2-8.2 μm in diameter.
2. **White blood cells (leucocytes):** Colorless, irregular-shaped, nucleated cells. Larger than RBC (12-17 μm). Used to prevent disease-causing pathogens.
3. **Platelets (thrombocytes):** Colorless, non-nucleated, biconvex-shaped cells (2-3 μm). Important for initiating blood clotting when blood vessels are cut or injured.

## 4.6.3 Major Diseases of Circulatory System

Different diseases or disorders affect human circulation systems:

1. **Hypertension:** Also called high blood pressure. Caused by age, obesity, high salt consumption, drug addiction, stress, kidney problems, diabetes. Treatment includes less salt and fat consumption, regular exercise, avoiding drugs, and regular checkups.

2. **Heart attack:** Occurs when blood stops flowing to part of the heart, injuring heart muscle due to lack of oxygen.

3. **Stroke:** Occurs when blood supply to part of brain is interrupted or reduced, preventing brain tissue from getting oxygen.

4. **Heart failure:** Chronic, progressive condition where heart muscle cannot pump enough blood to meet body's needs.

## What is reproduction?

Unicellular organisms have no reproductive organs as they are made of one cell. However, most multicellular organisms that reproduce sexually have distinct male and female reproductive organs. This topic covers human reproductive systems.

## 4.7.1 Male and Female Reproductive Organs

Reproductive cells are produced by male and female gonads: sperm cells and egg cells. When these unite during sexual intercourse, they produce a new organism.

### A. Male reproductive organ
The human male reproductive system consists of testes and other sex organs like penis, scrotum, vas deferens, urethra, prostate glands, and Cowper's glands.

- **Vas deferens (sperm ducts):** Long tube extending from each epididymis to urethra for transportation of sperm cells and fluid.

## 4.7.4 The Primary and Secondary Sexual Characteristics

Primary and secondary sexual characteristics are physical traits that make males and females look and behave differently from each other. Primary sexual characteristics are those present at birth (being male or female). Secondary sexual characteristics appear during puberty.

### Secondary Sexual Characteristics

When boys and girls reach puberty, follicle stimulating hormone (FSH) from the pituitary gland stimulates the testes and ovaries to produce sex hormones (testosterone in males, estrogen in females). These hormones promote the development of secondary sexual characteristics.

#### Secondary sexual characteristics of male
Puberty in boys usually begins between ages 9-15. The pituitary gland produces increasing amounts of FSH, which stimulates the testes to produce testosterone. Rising testosterone triggers many changes:

- Whole body undergoes adolescence growth spurt and becomes tall
- Growth of pubic hair, body hair, and facial hair
- Larynx enlarges, causing voice to deepen
- Shoulders and chest broaden
- Penis enlarges and skin of penis and scrotum becomes darker
- Testes begin producing sperm cells
- Development of more muscles
- Adolescents become more questioning, independent, look beyond family, feel young and insecure, confused and angry

**Key term:** Testosterone - Male sex hormone produced during puberty.

#### Secondary sexual characteristics of female
Females have gonads called ovaries located in the abdomen, associated with fallopian tubes and uterus. Girls enter puberty between ages 8-14. FSH stimulates ovaries to become active and produce the female sex hormone estrogen. When estrogen levels rise, changes occur:

- Development in height and whole body structure
- Hair grows around pubic area and armpits
- Breast development
- Widening of hips
- Voice becomes thinner
- Eggs mature and menstruation starts
- Feeling of independence and insecurity

**Key term:** Estrogen and Progesterone - Female sex hormones produced during puberty.

## 4.7.4 Menstruation

### What does menstruation mean?
The menstrual cycle is a sequence of events occurring approximately every four weeks throughout a woman's fertile life (from puberty to around age 50). At puberty, FSH from the pituitary gland stimulates ovum development and makes the ovary produce estrogen, which stimulates the uterus to build up a thick, spongy lining with blood vessels ready to support pregnancy.

About 14 days before ovulation, one ovum bursts out of its follicle. This process is called **ovulation**. After ovulation, hormone levels reduce. The remaining follicle forms the **corpus luteum** (yellow body) which secretes progesterone. Progesterone keeps the thick uterine wall ready to receive a fertilized ovum. If pregnancy does not occur about ten days after ovulation, estrogen and progesterone levels decrease, blood vessels close down, and the uterine lining discharges through the vagina as blood, which is called **menstruation**. Around age 50, women stop ovulating and menstruating. This is called **menopause**.

**Key terms:**
- Menopause: Stopping of menstruation and ovulation in females
- Ovulation: Movement of egg from ovary to fallopian tube

## 4.7.5 Reproductive Health

### What is reproductive health?
Reproductive health is the physical, mental, and social health status related to the reproductive system at all life stages. Due to lack of knowledge, reproductive health problems mainly affect women, including female genital mutilation, early marriage, rape, illegal abortion, and sexually transmitted diseases.

**Key terms:**
- Rape: Type of sexual assault usually involving sexual intercourse
- Female genital mutilation: Partial or total removal of female external genitalia

### Sexually Transmitted Diseases (STDs)
Diseases transmitted from infected to healthy person during sexual contact:

#### HIV/AIDS
HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) causes AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome). No cure or vaccine exists. HIV damages immune system white blood cells (especially T-cells). High-risk groups include homosexual men and women, intravenous drug users, sex workers, hemophiliacs, and their sexual partners.

**Transmission:**
- During breastfeeding
- Sharing contaminated needles
- Unprotected sexual intercourse
- From infected mother to baby during birth

**Prevention (ABC rules):**
- A = Abstain from sex before marriage
- B = Be faithful to one sexual partner
- C = Condomize

#### Gonorrhea
Caused by bacterium *Neisseria gonorrhea*. Found in mucus membranes (vagina, penis, throat, rectum). Transmitted during unsafe sexual intercourse.

**Symptoms:** Appear after about a week - burning sensation during urination, yellowish discharge. Untreated pregnant women can transmit to babies, causing blindness.

**Prevention:** Early treatment with antibiotics, abstinence until cured, ABC methods.

#### Chancroids
Caused by *Hemophilus ducreyi* bacteria. Most common in men. Increases HIV/AIDS risk.

**Symptoms:** Two stages:
- First: Ulceration on reproductive organs, bleeding, painful ulcers
- Second: Swollen glands filled with pus, may cause loss of penis or groin tissue

**Prevention:** Antibiotics in first stage, good sanitation, ABC rules, appropriate drug use, clinic checkups.

#### Syphilis
One of the most dangerous STDs. Caused by bacterium *Treponema pallidum*. Transmitted by unsafe sexual intercourse and from mother to fetus.

**Symptoms:** Three stages:
- First: Painless sore on penis, vagina, mouth, rectum
- Second: Tiredness, fever, sore throat, headaches, loss of appetite
- Final: Irreversible problems affecting skin, bone, brain, and other organs

**Preventions:** Same as other STDs - ABC rules, treatment, etc.
