## 5.1 Ecosystem and Interactions

### What is an ecosystem?

Different types of living organisms can interact with one another and with non-living things in their habitats for the purpose of survival. The interactions are studied by a science called ecology. Ecosystems involve all organisms in the given habitat and their interactions with their physical environments. The main physical components that are vital for all forms of living organisms are water, sunlight, air, temperatures, and land.

**Key term:**  
**Ecology:** Branch of biology that studies the interaction between organisms and their environment.

### 5.1.1 Definition and Components of Ecosystem

Environment is the sum of all living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components in a given habitat. Ecosystems can be affected by living (biotic) and non-living things or physical components (abiotic) of an environment. Therefore, an ecosystem has two major components: biotic and abiotic.

#### 1. Biotic components

These components include all living organisms living in particular ecosystems like plants, animals, bacteria, algae, etc.

##### 1.1 Types of biological interactions

Each organism is part of other organisms' environments, thus they interact in various ways. These interactions can be intra-specific or inter-specific.

- **Intra-specific interaction:** These types of interactions occur between the same species. These may be for competition for food, territory, and for finding a mate. E.g., lion and lion, birds with birds.

- **Inter-specific interaction:** These are interactions between one group of species with another group of species for various purposes. E.g., hyena and prey like cow.

- **Predators:** Predator animals like hyena feed on other animals (prey) like cow.
- **Symbiosis:** Any type of biological association between two organisms that interact. The association could be beneficial to both, beneficial to one without benefiting or harming the other, or beneficial to one and harmful to the other.

Types of symbiosis:
- **Mutualism:** Both organisms benefit. E.g., fungi and algae, cow and some birds.
- **Commensalism:** One organism benefits, the other is neither benefited nor harmed. E.g., big tree and epiphytes.
- **Parasitism:** One organism benefits (parasite), the other is harmed (host). E.g., human and tapeworm.
- **Protocooperation:** Both organisms benefit but it is not obligatory. E.g., teeth-cleaning birds and crocodiles.
- **Competition:** Two populations compete for resources. Both populations are harmed. E.g., sportsmen.

#### 2. Abiotic components

These are the physical or non-living things that affect the living condition of a given ecosystem. Some of these components are:
- **Water:** Most important component; serves as habitat, drinking water, and irrigation.
- **Sunlight:** Primary source of energy for all life on earth during photosynthesis.
- **Air:** Combination of gases like oxygen, nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and water vapor.
- **Soil:** Controls vegetation growth by fertility, pH level, etc.
- **Temperatures:** Affects organism distribution.
- **Land topography:** Arrangement of land.

### 5.1.2 Trophic (Feeding) Relationships

Based on nutrition, organisms are grouped into:
- **Autotrophs:** Synthesize their own energy.  
  - *Photoautotrophs:* Use sunlight (e.g., green plants, algae).  
  - *Chemoautotrophs:* Use chemical reactions (e.g., nitrifying bacteria).
- **Heterotrophs:** Cannot make food; feed on others (e.g., herbivores, carnivores, omnivores, decomposers).

### 5.1.3 Food Chain and Food Web

**Food Chain:** Direct and simple feeding system involving transfer of nutrients and energy in one direction:  
Producer → Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer → Tertiary Consumer  
Example: Wheat → Goat → Tiger → Lion

**Food Web:** Complex feeding interrelations among organisms consisting of many food chains.

### 5.1.4 Trophic Pyramids

- **Pyramid of Numbers:** Based on number of organisms at each trophic level.
- **Pyramid of Biomass:** Shows mass of organisms at each level.
- **Pyramid of Energy:** Shows energy flow; energy is not recycled.

### 5.1.5 Nutrient Cycles and Energy Flow

Nutrient cycles include:
- **Nitrogen Cycle:** Nitrogen fixation, ammonification, nitrification, denitrification.
- **Oxygen Cycle:** Movement of oxygen through air, biosphere, and crust.
- **Carbon Cycle:** Carbon moves among atmosphere, plants, animals, soil, and fossil fuels.
- **Hydrogen Cycle:** Exchange of hydrogen between biotic and abiotic sources.
- **Water Cycle:** Evaporation, condensation, precipitation, collection.

## 5.2 Conservation of Natural Resources

**Natural Resources:** Substances humans use, e.g., oil, coal, metals. Grouped into:
- **Renewable:** Can be replaced (e.g., vegetation, animals).
- **Non-renewable:** Cannot be easily replaced (e.g., petroleum, coal).

**Conservation:** Care and protection of natural resources for future generations.

### 5.2.1 Soil

Soil is a mixture of broken rocks, minerals, living organisms, air, water, and decaying organic matter (humus).

**Types of Soil:**
- Sandy
- Clay
- Loam (most fertile)

**Soil Horizons:** Layers (O, A, E, B, C, R) with different characteristics.

**Soil Texture:** Refers to size distribution of mineral particles (sand, silt, clay).

**Soil Fertility:** Loam is most fertile; decline caused by erosion, deforestation, overgrazing, etc.

**Soil Conservation Methods:**
- Afforestation
- Terracing
- Contour ploughing
- Crop rotation

### 5.2.2 Water

Water is essential for life, metabolic reactions, temperature regulation, and waste removal.

**Water Pollution:** Caused by industrial, agricultural, and domestic wastes.

**Impacts:** Toxic chemicals accumulate in organisms, causing diseases and birth defects.

**Water Conservation Methods:**
- Cover vegetation
- Contour ploughing
- Terracing
- Check dams
- Groundwater protection

### 5.2.3 Air

Air composition: 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.93% argon, 0.04% carbon dioxide, and trace gases.

**Air Pollution Effects:**
- Global warming
- Global dimming
- Acid rain
- Health issues (lung cancer, heart disease, etc.)

**Prevention:**
- Avoid traditional fuels
- Use renewable energy
- Reforestation
- Education and laws

### 5.2.4 Forests

Forests are complex ecosystems with trees that support biodiversity.

**Uses:**
- Clean air
- Reduce erosion
- Provide oxygen
- Habitat for wildlife

**Deforestation:** Caused by farming, furniture, construction, etc.

**Conservation:** Reforestation, education, community involvement.

### 5.2.5 Biodiversity

**Biodiversity:** Variety of life in ecosystems.

**Importance:**
- Economic value
- Ecological services
- Recreation
- Cultural and scientific value

**Conservation Strategies:**
- **In-situ:** In natural habitats (e.g., national parks, sanctuaries).
- **Ex-situ:** Outside natural habitats (e.g., zoos, seed banks, botanical gardens).

### 5.2.6 Indigenous Knowledge and Conservation

Indigenous knowledge includes local practices for resource management.

**Examples:** Terracing, crop rotation, mixed cropping, fertilization.

**Importance:** Helps adapt to climate change, supports sustainable development.

## Summary

- Ecosystems consist of biotic and abiotic components.
- Biological interactions include competition, predation, symbiosis.
- Food chains and webs show energy flow.
- Nutrients cycle; energy flows and is lost.
- Natural resources must be conserved.
- Soil, water, air, forests, and biodiversity are vital.
- Conservation methods include afforestation, pollution control, and indigenous practices.
