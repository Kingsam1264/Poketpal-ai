### Startup Question

**What is slavery and slave trade?**

A slave is a human being who is the property of another person. When Africans were captured, sold, and shipped to the New World, they were enslaved for their entire lives; their children were also enslaved. Slave trade is the capturing, selling, and buying of enslaved persons.

### Actors of Slave Trade

**Startup Question: Which countries were the main actors in the African slave trade?**

At first, the Portuguese and the Spanish were the main organizers of the trade. By the second half of the seventeenth century, countries of north-west Europe became involved. During the eighteenth century, Britain was the foremost slave-trading power, alongside the French, the Dutch, the Portuguese, and the Spanish. All had colonies in the New World.

- **Portugal:** Developed the plantation system for sugar cane in Mediterranean islands and southern Portugal/Spain. Early slave labor came from North Africa and Slavs from southern Russia.  
- **Spain:** Opened the transatlantic route to the Americas and the Caribbean.  
- **Other Europeans:** From the 1630s, the Dutch, French, and English expanded sugar, coffee, tobacco, and cotton plantations in Brazil, the Caribbean, and southern North America. The demand for slaves grew rapidly.

---

### The Origins of Slaves

**Startup Question: Why did Europeans choose Africans for slavery?**

Plantation owners first used the local indigenous Amerindian population, but they quickly died due to harsh treatment and European diseases. By the end of the first century of contact, 90% or more of the Amerindian population in the Caribbean had been wiped out.

Europeans then turned to criminals and outcasts from Europe, but their numbers were limited and they did not survive long in tropical conditions.

Eventually, Europeans turned to Africa for slave labor because:

- Africans had immunity to some tropical diseases  
- Africans had skills in metal-working, mining, and tropical agriculture

### The Nature of Slave Trade

**Startup Question: What were the natures of slave trade in Africa, the Middle Passage, and the New World?**

**In Africa:**  
Captured Africans were marched to the coast in chains, locked in wooden cages, and stripped naked for inspection. They were treated as property, not humans.

**On the Middle Passage:**  
Slaves were chained in rows on ships, forced to lie on shelves less than half a meter apart. They could not sit or move. They lay in their own waste for weeks, given minimal food and water. The dead were thrown overboard. 15–30% died during the 3–6 week journey.

**In America:**  
Plantation life was brutal. One third of enslaved people died within three years. Few survived beyond ten years due to underfeeding and overwork.

### The Triangular Trade

**Startup Question: What was triangular slave trade?**

Triangular Trade refers to the three-legged trade route between Europe, Africa, and the Americas:

1. **Europe to Africa:** Manufactured goods like glass, cotton cloth, and metalware  
2. **Africa to New World:** Enslaved Africans  
3. **New World to Europe:** Plantation goods like sugar, coffee, tobacco, and cotton

### The Abolition of the Slave Trade

**Startup Question: What were the factors that led to the abolition of slave trade?**

**Political Factors:**  
- American War of Independence (1776–83)  
- French Revolution (1789) promoted liberty and equality  
- Growing belief in universal human rights  

**Economic Factors:**  
- Factory labor in Europe was cheaper and more efficient than plantation slavery  
- Africa was seen as a market for European goods and a source of raw materials  

**Slave Resistance:**  
- Africans resisted enslavement from the beginning  
- Revolts were common, e.g., Haiti (1791), led by Toussaint L'Ouverture, resulted in the first Black republic  

**End of Slavery:**  
- 1834: British colonies  
- 1848: French colonies  
- 1860: Cuba  
- 1865: United States  
- 1888: Brazil  

**Back to Africa Movements:**  
- **Sierra Leone:** Founded in 1787 for freed Blacks from England  
- **Liberia:** Founded in 1822 by freed African Americans; became independent in 1847  

---

### Impacts of Slave Trade

**Startup Question: What were the impacts of slave trade in Africa?**

**Socially:**  
- Increased insecurity and distrust among African groups  
- Languages mixed with European ones  
- Loss of cultural beliefs and practices  

**Politically:**  
- Instability and conflict  
- Distribution of ammunition led to militarization  
- Some kings lost power  

**Economically:**  
- Human resources were lost  
- Agriculture and mining sectors declined  
- Dependency on European goods increased

### Startup Question: What is colonialism?

Colonialism is when a weaker or underdeveloped nation is controlled by a powerful one. The powerful nation has an advanced economy, strong government, and military; the weaker nation has underdeveloped resources, primitive economy, and weak governance.

**Causes:**  
- Need for raw materials  
- Markets for goods  
- Cheap labor  

---

### Partition of Africa

**Startup Question: What was the main purpose of the Berlin Conference of 1884–1885?**

The Berlin Conference was an European agreement to divide Africa. Two key principles:

1. Congo Basin recognized under Belgian King Leopold  
2. European claims required “effective occupation”  

---

### Scramble for Africa

**Startup Question: Which countries of Africa were colonized by European countries?**

By 1914:

- **Britain:** South Africa, Rhodesia, Kenya, Uganda, Sudan, Egypt  
- **France:** Algeria, Tunisia, Morocco, West Africa, Equatorial Africa, Madagascar  
- **Germany:** Tanganyika, Togoland, Cameroons, South-West Africa  
- **Italy:** Eritrea, Somaliland, Libya  
- **Portugal:** Guinea, Angola, Mozambique  
- **Spain:** Morocco, Sahara  
- **Belgium:** Congo  
- **Independent:** Ethiopia and Liberia  

**Fig 5.8: Africa under foreign rule in 1914**  
A political map showing European colonization across Africa.

---

### The Nature of Colonial Administration

**Startup Question: What were the systems of administration used by colonialist states?**

- **Direct Rule:** Used by France, Germany, Portugal, Spain, Italy. Africans were assimilated into European culture.  
- **Indirect Rule:** Used by Britain. African laws were used for local disputes, but British law for serious crimes.

---

### Consequences of Colonialism upon Africans

**Startup Question: What are the consequences of colonialism?**

- Wealth was drained  
- Local manufacturing discouraged  
- Exploitation of labor  
- Racial discrimination  
- Cultural degradation  

---

### African Independence

**Startup Question: What were the forms of colonial struggle of Africans before and after WWII?**

**Early Resistance (1900–1939):**

- **Religious:** Kimbanguism (Congo), Kitawala (East Congo), Watch Tower (Nyasaland), Ethiopianism (South Africa)  
- **Traditional Associations:** Young Baganda, Bataka (Uganda), Kikuyu Associations (Kenya)

**Post-WWII Independence:**

- **Non-violent:** West African French colonies (e.g., Ghana, Nigeria)  
- **Violent:**  
  - **Algeria:** Independent 1962, President Ahmed Ben Bella  
  - **Mozambique:** Independent 1975, President Samora Machel  
  - **Angola:** Independent 1975, President Augustino Neto  
  - **Kenya:** Independent 1963, President Jomo Kenyatta  
  - **Ghana:** Independent 1957, President Kwame Nkrumah  
  - **Nigeria:** Independent 1960, President Nnamdi Azikwe  

---

### Factors That Helped African Independence

- Lessons from WWI and WWII  
- Influence of the USA and Communist propaganda  
- Inspiration from newly independent Asian nations  
- United Nations decisions  
- Changing global attitudes toward colonialism

### Economic and Political Integration

**Startup Question: What is economic and political integration?**

- **Political integration:** Shifting loyalties and activities toward a new central authority  
- **Economic integration:** Cooperation to reduce barriers to trade, movement, and capital  

---

### The Organization of African Unity (OAU)

**Startup Question: What is the main purpose of the African Unity?**

Founded in 1963 in Addis Ababa, Ethiopia, by 32 independent African states. Eventually grew to 55 members.

**Objectives:**

- Promote unity and solidarity  
- Coordinate cooperation  
- Safeguard sovereignty  
- Eliminate colonialism and apartheid  
- Harmonize policies  

---

### Establishment of African Union (AU)

**Startup Question: Why was OAU changed to African Union?**

Shifted focus from anti-colonialism to development and integration.

**Key Summits:**

- **Sirte Summit (1999):** Called for AU creation  
- **Lomé Summit (2000):** Adopted AU Constitutive Act  
- **Lusaka Summit (2001):** Drew implementation roadmap  

**AU Launched:** 9 July 2002, Durban, South Africa

---

### Africa Moves towards Economic and Political Integration

**Startup Question: What are the core visions of African Union?**

**Vision:** An integrated, prosperous, and peaceful Africa, driven by its citizens, representing a dynamic force globally.

**Agenda 2063:** A strategic framework for a united Africa based on shared values and common destiny.

**Objectives:**

- Achieve unity and solidarity  
- Accelerate integration  
- Defend African positions globally  
- Promote sustainable development  
- Raise living standards  
- Harmonize regional policies

## Summary

- Plantation systems led to the Trans-Atlantic slave trade  
- Slave trade was brutal and dehumanizing  
- Abolition came due to political, economic, and resistance factors  
- Colonialism followed, dividing Africa among European powers  
- Africans resisted and eventually gained independence  
- OAU and AU were formed to promote unity and development  
- Africa now focuses on integration and cooperation

## Glossary

- **Amerindian:** Native American  
- **Apartheid:** Racial segregation in South Africa  
- **Assimilate:** Turn Africans into French citizens  
- **Effective occupation:** Control via treaties, flags, and administration  
- **Exploitation:** Unfair treatment for benefit  
- **Human traffic:** Forced labor  
- **Indigenous:** Original inhabitants  
- **Integration:** Breaking down barriers between African peoples  
- **Partition:** Division of Africa by European powers  
- **Slavery:** Condition of being owned by another  
- **Trans-Atlantic slave trade:** Transport of enslaved Africans to the Americas  
- **Violent:** Use of physical force to harm
