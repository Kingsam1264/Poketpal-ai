# Unit 5: Banking and Finance

## Introduction

One of the basic factors of production in economics is capital. Capital is the amount of money the producer pays for the other factors of production (labor, land, and entrepreneur) used in the production.

Banks are one of the financial institutions that are used to transfer finance from those who have in excess to those that are in need of it. People with excess money will go to finance and put it in banks; this is called saving. People who face a shortage of money will go to the bank and ask for loans. Banks will give money as a loan according to their lending procedures. This activity of banks is one example of financial intermediation.

Thus, in this unit, the issues that will be discussed concerning banks and other financial intermediaries are:

- Introduction to financial intermediaries  
- Introduction to financial markets  
- Financial institutions  
- Banking and non-banking financial institutions  
- Historical development of the banking sector in Ethiopia  
- Electronic banking (e-banking)  
- Indigenous financial institutions in Ethiopia – “ikub”, “iddir”  

Therefore, students are expected to think of banks and other financial intermediaries in their locality and their functions as savers of excess money and as lenders to those who need it.

### What is a Financial Intermediary?

A financial intermediary is an entity that acts as the middleman between two parties in a financial transaction, for instance, the Commercial Bank of Ethiopia. Financial intermediaries offer a number of benefits to the average consumer, such as safety, liquidity, and economies of scale involved in banking and asset management.

In other words, financial intermediaries mediate between the providers (with excess money) and users (with shortage of money) of financial capital. The transfer of funds from surplus spending unit to deficit spending unit through financial intermediaries is also called **Financial Intermediation**.

### Benefits of Financial Intermediaries

**For depositors**: Financial intermediaries give safety and interest earnings. Safety refers to the fact that the money of the depositors is free from any risk of loss. Banks also pay interest on the money saved at their banks by the depositors.

**For borrowers**: People who borrow money from banks and other financial intermediaries are known as investors. In the process of production, they may face a shortage of finance and go to financial intermediaries (e.g., Banks) to get money or finance for their investment. The financial intermediaries will charge them annual interest on the money they borrow. The more money they borrow, the more investment (production) they undertake, which is useful for the country.

Thus, finance (money) in the economy is like the blood circulating in the body. Any economy or country cannot function well without money (finance), just as the body cannot function without blood circulating in it. We use it to buy our day-to-day needs (demand = willingness + ability; where ability is the money or finance).

### What are Financial Markets?

Financial markets are markets that provide channels for allocation of savings to investment. They provide a variety of assets to savers as well as various forms in which the investors can raise funds and thereby decouple the acts of saving and investment. The savers and investors are constrained not by their individual abilities, but by the economy's ability to invest and save respectively. The financial markets, thus, contribute to economic development to the extent that the latter depends on the rates of savings and investment.

### Components of Financial Markets

**A. Money Market**: According to Drake & Fabozzi (2010), the money market is the sector of the financial market that includes financial instruments with a maturity date of one year or less at the time of issuance.

Money market instruments include:

- Treasury bills  
- Commercial papers  
- Negotiable certificates of deposit  
- Repurchase agreements  
- Bankers' acceptances  

**I. Treasury Bills**: Financial instruments issued by the governments with maturity dates of three weeks, one month, three months, or six months. The holder realizes a return by buying these securities for less than the maturity value and receiving the full value at maturity.

**II. Commercial Papers**: Promissory notes (written promises to pay) issued by a large, creditworthy corporation or municipality, with original maturity from 1 day to 270 days. May be interest-bearing or sold at a discount.

**III. Certificates of Deposit (CDs)**: Written promises by a bank to pay a depositor. Issued by large commercial banks with maturities between one month and one year. On maturity, the issuer repays the principal plus interest.

**B. Capital Market**: The sector of the financial market where long-term financial instruments are issued by corporations and governments. They have original maturity greater than a year and include perpetual securities (those with no maturity).

There are two types of capital market securities:

**a. Equity**: Includes common stock and preferred stock. Common stock represents ownership of a perpetual corporation. Preferred stock also represents ownership but may have a redemption date or be perpetual.

**b. Debt Obligation**: Borrower promises to repay the maturity value at a specified time beyond one year. Includes:

- Bank loans (e.g., syndicated bank loans)  
- Debt securities (Notes: ≤10 years; Bonds: >10 years)

### What are Institutions?

Institutions are norms that organize social, political, and economic relations. They are 'the underlying rules of the game' in any society or organization. There are two forms:

- **Formal institutions**: Written constitution, laws, policies, rights, and regulations enforced by official authorities.  
- **Informal institutions**: Unwritten social norms, customs, or traditions that shape thought and behavior.

### What are Financial Institutions?

Financial institutions are institutions that serve as financial intermediaries.

There are three major types:

1. **Depositary Institutions**: Accept and manage deposits and make loans (e.g., banks and credit unions).  
2. **Contractual Institutions**: Insurance companies and pension funds.  
3. **Investment Institutes**: Investment banks, underwriters, brokerage firms.

---

### 5.3.1 The Banking Financial Institution

A bank is a financial intermediary that gives money (loan) to a borrower and accepts deposits (savings) from depositors. The banking sector plays a major role in enhancing development and growth through:

- Mobilizing savings  
- Identifying good investments  
- Exerting sound corporate control  

#### Roles of Banks in an Economy

- Acquiring information about firms and managers to improve capital allocation and corporate governance  
- Managing risk and enhancing investment efficiency  

#### Efficiency of Banks

Depends on:

1. Degree of competition among banks  
2. Nature of regulation  

Competition leads to:

- Greater technical efficiency  
- Lower interest rates for borrowers  
- Higher interest rates for depositors  
- Greater variety of services  

---

### 5.3.2 The Non-Banking Financial Institution

A non-bank financial institution (NBFI) does not have a full banking license or is not supervised by a national or international banking regulatory agency.

NBFIs facilitate:

- Investment  
- Insurance  
- Risk pooling  
- Contractual savings  
- Market brokering  

Examples: insurance companies, pawn shops, cashier’s check issuers, check cashing locations, payday lending, currency exchanges, microloan organizations.

## 5.4 Historical Development of Banks in Ethiopia

By the end of this section, students will be able to:

### Key Concepts

- Socialist economy system

### Start Up Activity

How old is the oldest bank in Ethiopia?

### A Historical Note

Modern banking in Ethiopia started in 1905 with the establishment of the Abyssinian Bank, based on a fifty-year agreement with the Anglo-Egyptian National Bank.

In 1908, a new development bank (Société Nationale d'Ethiope Pour le Development de l'Agriculture et du Commerce) and two other foreign banks (Banque de l'Indochine and Compagnie de l'Afrique Orientale) were also established. These banks were criticized for being wholly foreign owned.

---

### 5.4.1 History of Banking in Ethiopia

#### I. 1905–1931

Emperor Menelik II introduced modern banking. The first modern bank, **Bank of Abyssinia**, was founded in 1906 under an agreement with the British-owned National Bank of Egypt. It was managed by foreigners and served mostly foreign businessmen and wealthy Ethiopians. It opened branches in Harar (1906), Dire Dawa (1908), Gore (1912), and Dessie and Djibouti (1920). It was liquidated in 1931.

#### II. 1931–1936

Replaced by **Bank of Ethiopia**, the first independent bank under Ethiopian government control. It took over all previous functions and branches. It was short-lived and liquidated during the Italian occupation in 1936.

#### III. 1936–1941

Italian banks introduced:

- Banca d’Italia  
- Banco di Roma  
- Banco di Napoli  
- Banca Nazionale del Lavoro  

Only Banco di Roma and Banco di Napoli continued after the occupation.

#### IV. 1941–1943

Barclays Bank was introduced by British troops and operated until 1943.

#### V. 1943–1963

**State Bank of Ethiopia** was established in 1943 as the central bank. It performed both commercial and central banking functions. It had 21 branches, including one in Khartoum, Sudan.

#### VI. 1963–1974

The State Bank was split into:

- **National Bank of Ethiopia (NBE)** – Central bank  
- **Commercial Bank of Ethiopia (CBE)** – Commercial bank  

Addis Bank was formed from nationalized foreign banks and merged into CBE in 1980.

#### VII. 1974–1991

Socialist period. All banks nationalized. Three dominant banks:

- NBE  
- CBE  
- Agricultural and Industrial Development Bank (AIDB)

#### VIII. From 1991 Onward

Ethiopia adopted a liberal economy. The NBE outlined new functions separated from total government control. The **Monetary and Banking Proclamation No. 83/1994** allowed private investment in banking.

---

### 5.4.2 Roles and Functions of Banks in Ethiopia

#### A. National Bank of Ethiopia (NBE)

- Controls interest rates and credit  
- Regulates commercial banks, insurance, and microfinance  
- Prints currency and mints coins  
- Issues bonds and treasury bills  
- Maintains low inflation  
- Manages foreign exchange and international reserves  
- Formulates monetary policy  

#### B. Commercial Bank of Ethiopia (CBE)

- Accepts deposits  
- Gives loans  
- Provides safe custody  
- Foreign currency exchange  
- Issues letters of credit and traveler’s cheques  

#### C. Development Bank of Ethiopia (DBE)

- Provides short, medium, and long-term loans  
- Focuses on agricultural and industrial development  
- Supervises financed projects

### Key Concepts

### Start Up Activity

Where do the poor go to get credit?  
Hint: Banks ask for collateral!

Mohammed Yunus (Bangladeshi economist) introduced **microfinance** and **microcredit**, leading to the Grameen Bank. He gave small loans without collateral to poor villagers.

Today, MFIs serve millions worldwide, providing:

- Small loans  
- Savings services  
- Insurance  
- Money transfers  

### 5.5.1 Microfinance and Microcredit

**Microfinance institutions (MFIs)** give access to financial services to people excluded from traditional banking.

**Microcredit** is a small loan given without collateral, often through **group lending**, where the group acts as collateral. If one defaults, others cover the payment.

**Micro-entrepreneurs** are poor but economically active individuals who use microcredit to start or expand small businesses.

### 5.5.2 Clients of Microfinance

Clients are the **economically active poor** – low-income persons with entrepreneurial skills. The **poorest of the poor** are usually excluded.

**Group lending** uses peer pressure and social ties to ensure repayment.

### 5.5.3 Microfinance and Money Lenders

Informal moneylenders charge high interest and offer no protection. However, they are popular due to:

- Simple procedures  
- Clear terms  
- Timely disbursement  
- Character-based lending  

MFIs adopt these positive features into formal systems.

### 5.5.4 Microfinance Institutions in Ethiopia

As of 2020/21, there are **41 MFIs** operational in Ethiopia. They mobilized **Birr 47 billion** in savings and provided **Birr 64.6 billion** in loans.

### What is Electronic Banking?

Also known as **Electronic Fund Transfer (EFT)**, it refers to transferring funds electronically. Examples include mobile payments, ATMs, direct deposits, and debit card purchases.

#### I. Automated Teller Machines (ATMs)

Allow:

- Cash withdrawals  
- Fund transfers  
- Deposits  
- Bill payments  
- Currency exchange  

#### II. Direct Deposit

Employers, governments, or institutions deposit funds directly into bank accounts using the **Automated Clearing House (ACH)** network.

#### III. Debit Card Purchases

Money is deducted directly from your bank account. Unlike credit cards, you cannot spend more than your balance.

#### IV. Mobile Banking

Allows banking via mobile devices. Benefits: anywhere, anytime access. Risks: security concerns, limited functionality.

## 5.7 Indigenous Financial Institutions

By the end of this section, students will be able to:

- Distinguish between different types of indigenous financial institutions in Ethiopia  
- Examine their role in improving livelihoods  

### Key Concepts

- Indigenous financial institutions  
- “Iqqub”  
- “Iddir”  
- Saving and Credit Cooperatives (SACCs)  

### Start Up Activity

Tell us about any indigenous financial institution in your area.

### 1. The “Iqqub” – Saving Club

A rotating savings club where members contribute regularly and receive the lump sum in turn. Used for weddings, funerals, house building, etc.

Preferred due to:

- Social solidarity  
- Forced savings  
- Flexibility  
- Low default risk  
- No admin costs  
- No minimum threshold  

### 2. The “Iddir”

A community-based insurance association. Members contribute monthly. Provides funeral assistance and sometimes medical or fire coverage. Has bylaws and social sanctions.

### 3. Savings and Credit Cooperatives (SACCs)

Semi-formal institutions. Established in Ethiopia in the late 1960s. Growth accelerated after 1978. Provide short-term loans for agriculture and off-farm activities.
