## 5.1 The Meaning of Federalism

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Define federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What do you think about federalism?

Federalism is a system of government where power is divided between federal and regional governments. Devolution, the principle of federalism, means transferring power to the region that constitutes the federation. The devolution of power makes neither the federal nor the regional governments supreme. Instead, power is coordinated between the center and the region. This arrangement could be one mechanism to help avoid a monopoly of power.

Different scholars define federalism in different ways. Some of them are:

> "Federalism is a political organization in which the activities of government are divided between regional governments and a central government in such a way that each kind of government has some activities on which it makes final decisions."

> "Federalism refers to the advocacy of multi-tiered government combining elements of shared-rule and regional self-rule. Within the genus of federal political systems, federations represent a particular species in which neither the federal nor the constituent units of government are constitutionally subordinate to the other."

> "Federalism is an institutional arrangement in which (a) public authority is divided between state governments and a central government, (b) each level of government has some issues on which it makes final decisions, and (c) a high federal court adjudicates disputes concerning..."

**Case Study: The Lower Omo Basin Flood**  
The Lower Omo Basin people became victims of flood some years ago due to heavy rain in the highland areas in the basin. Omo River overflowed its banks and flooded villages and farmlands. Many livestock died and thousands of people were made homeless. Confronting such challenges was beyond the capacity of the regional state. The federal government stepped in to help the victims. Ethiopians from different backgrounds responded promptly to help those affected by the flooding. Different organizations like religious institutions provided help for the victims. In this national effort, the regional governments also mobilized and channeled resources to the victims. This instance showed the role a coordinated effort of the federal and regional governments could play to solve problems. It also showed a spirit of unity and care for humans in Ethiopia.

## 5.2 Types of Federalism

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Identify types of federalism
- Explain the advantages and disadvantages of each type of federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What do you know about the major types of federalism?

Federal systems are usually known for dividing power between the federal government and the states. However, laws made by the states (regions) usually apply to the respective regions, whereas laws by federal legislatures apply to all states. In confederal arrangements, the central government has no direct authority over citizens' lives. However, in federations, the federal government has direct authority over citizens by legislating rules and laws.

Based on how federal governments execute their laws across their whole territory, federations can be classified as:
- **Dual federalism**
- **Cooperative federalism**

**Figure 5.1: Types of Federalism**  
*A diagram showing the distinction between Cooperative Federalism (based on a pragmatic mixing of authority) and Dual Federalism (based on a clear delineation of authority among national, state, and local governments).*

### A) Dual Federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What is dual federalism?

Dual federalism is a system that clearly divides government authority between state and national governments. Many refer to Dual Federalism as **Layer Cake Federalism**. State governments have their own powers where the central government could not intervene, and vice versa. Earlier, state governments controlled almost all aspects of individual citizens, whereas the national government had authority over internal improvements, international relations, and commercial regulations between the states.

**Advantages of Dual Federalism:**
1. Protects local jurisdictions from federal government overreach.
2. Allows local laws to reflect the needs and wants of local constituents (e.g., speed limits, marriage laws).

**Disadvantages of Dual Federalism:**
1. Tension between state and federal governments.
2. Reduced cooperation and efficiency.
3. Decreased unity among states as state power increases.

### B) Cooperative Federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What is cooperative federalism?

Cooperative federalism requires state and national governments to share power and collaborate on overlapping functions. It is also known as **Marble-Cake Federalism**, where federal, state, and local governments interact cooperatively to solve common problems.

**Advantages of Cooperative Federalism:**
- Efficient service delivery through collaboration.
- Practical approach to complex problems.
- Prevents excessive power concentration.
- States influence program implementation.

**Disadvantages of Cooperative Federalism:**
- Decrease in state powers due to federal intervention.

## 5.3 Key Features of Federalism

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Identify the key features of federalism
- Evaluate the features of federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
Can you mention some of the features of federalism?

Federations share certain common characteristics, including:

### 5.3.1 Division of Power

Political power is constitutionally divided between the federal government and the states. This is based on the dual principle of unity and autonomy. The federal government handles shared responsibilities, while states manage regional identity issues.

### 5.3.2 Decentralized System of Government

In decentralized systems, local governments are subordinate to the center and can be unilaterally altered or revoked. In contrast, in a federation, the division of power is constitutionally guaranteed, and both levels derive authority from the constitution.

### 5.3.3 Written and Supreme Federal Constitution

The division of power is based on a written and supreme federal constitution. Both orders of government must submit to it. The constitution is a binding agreement that establishes and limits the powers of each level.

## 5.4 Advantage and Disadvantage of Federalism

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Differentiate the advantages and disadvantages of federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What do you think about the advantages and disadvantages of federalism?

### 5.4.1 Advantages of Federalism

**Figure 5.2: Advantages of Federalism**  
*A diagram listing: Protection against tyranny, Better understanding of local issues, Increasing citizen participation, Separate government, Division of power, Rigid written constitution, Special judiciary, More efficiency.*

1. **Division of powers** – Shared responsibilities.
2. **Separate government** – Both levels have their own institutions.
3. **Written constitution** – Serves as a legal agreement.
4. **Rigid constitution** – Prevents easy violation of autonomy.
5. **Special judiciary** – Resolves constitutional disputes.
6. **Better understanding of local issues** – Local governments are closer to the people.
7. **Increasing citizen participation** – Dual levels encourage involvement.
8. **Protection against tyranny** – Power is divided and checked.
9. **More efficiency** – States can tailor solutions to local needs.

### 5.4.2 Disadvantages of Federalism

**Figure 5.3: Disadvantages of Federalism**  
*A diagram listing: Conflict of authority, Regionalism over patriotism, Lack of accountability, Inequalities between states, Blockage of nationalist policies by states.*

1. **Conflict of authority** – Power struggles between levels.
2. **Regionalism over patriotism** – Promotes local pride over national unity.
3. **Lack of accountability** – Responsibility can be shifted.
4. **Inequalities between states** – Disparities in services and development.
5. **Blockage of nationalist policies** – States may resist or obstruct national laws.

## 5.5 The Roles of Federalism in Accommodating Diversity in Ethiopia

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Explain the role of federalism in Ethiopia
- Identify the nature of Ethiopian federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What do you think federalism in Ethiopia accommodates diversity?

The rationale of federalism in contemporary Ethiopia is primarily the reconciliation of ethnic diversity with political unity. Ethiopia has historically been a centralized unitary state, ignoring its multiethnic diversity. This proved ineffective. Federalism was introduced to accommodate the rights and demands of diverse ethnic groups.

The current federal arrangement, the **Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia**, aims to accommodate the interests of distinct ethnic groups. Federalism is a mechanism to manage Ethiopia’s multi-faceted diversity.

### 5.5.1 Features of Ethiopian Federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What are the unique features of the Ethiopian federal system?

- **Power Distribution:** The constitution lists federal, state, and concurrent powers. Residual powers belong to the states. Article 50(8) mandates mutual respect of powers.
- **Bi-cameral Legislature:**  
  - **House of People's Representatives:** The highest federal authority; legislates on federal matters; can question federal officials.  
  - **House of Federation:** Represents nations, nationalities, and peoples; members elected directly or indirectly; ensures constitutional interpretation and ethnic representation.

### 5.5.2 Challenges to Ethiopian Federalism

**Brainstorming:**  
What do you think about the challenges of Ethiopian federalism?

Ethiopia has practiced federalism since 1995 with both success and challenges. Key challenges include:
- Balancing unity and diversity
- Institutionalizing national self-determination
- Managing regional governments that lack viability or resources
- Addressing disparities in development and representation

Some states have not evolved into viable regional governments due to lack of experience, resources, or historical marginalization. This calls for an **asymmetric federal system** in some areas, where the federal government may need more extensive powers temporarily.

## Unit Summary

- Federalism is a system where the same territory is controlled by two levels of government.
- It contrasts with unitary and confederal systems.
- Advantages include protection from tyranny, increased participation, and efficiency.
- Disadvantages include conflict of authority, regionalism, and inequality.

- **Diversity:** Differences that shape perspectives and approaches.
- **Executive:** Implements laws.
- **Legislative:** Formulates laws.
- **Judiciary:** Interprets laws.
- **Jurisdictions:** Authority to hear and determine cases.
- **Rigid constitution:** Difficult to amend.
