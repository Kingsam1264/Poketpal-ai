Environmental chemistry is a branch of chemical science that deals with the production, transport, reactions, effects, and fates of chemical species in the water, air, terrestrial, and biological environment and the effects of human activities thereon.

A common pollutant species is used to illustrate the definition of environmental chemistry. Sulfur in coal is oxidized to sulfur dioxide gas, which is then released into the atmosphere. Sulfur dioxide gas can be oxidized to sulfur trioxide and eventually converted to sulfuric acid in the atmosphere, then fall back to earth as acid rain, affect a receptor like plants, and end up in a "sink" like a body of water or soil.

### **5.1.3 Concepts Related to Environmental Chemistry**

#### **Activity 5.4**
Discuss the relationship between pollutant and environmental pollution. What criteria should be considered to label the environment as polluted?

**Pollutant**: A substance whose concentration has increased due to human activity, ultimately having detrimental effects on the environment. Examples include sulfur dioxide (SOâ‚‚), carbon dioxide (COâ‚‚), ozone (Oâ‚ƒ), Pb, Hg, excess heat, light, and noise.

**Contaminant**: A substance that does not occur in nature but is introduced into the environment through human activity. A contaminant is called a pollutant when it exerts detrimental effects on human health or the environment. Receptors include bodies of water, humans, trees, animals, and materials.

**Sink**: The medium that interacts with and retains pollutants or converts them chemically.

**Dissolved Oxygen (DO)**: Oxygen dissolved in water, vital for aquatic life. Optimum levels in good quality water are 4-8 mg/L. Water with levels <4 mg/L is considered polluted and unfit for human or animal consumption.

**Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD)**: Indicates the capacity of dissolved organic matter (DOM) in a water sample to consume oxygen. Determined experimentally by measuring DO at the beginning and end of a 5-day period in a sealed sample. It gives a measure of oxygen utilized due to oxidation of DOM present in the water sample.

**Threshold Limit Value (TLV)**: Indicates the permissible level of a toxic pollutant in the atmosphere to which a healthy individual can be exposed during an 8-hour day without adverse effects. TLV is found by experimentation on animals, medical knowledge, and environmental studies.

**Water pollution** is the degradation of water quality caused by discharge of untreated sewage, industrial and agricultural waste, and oil spillage. Water is polluted if it contains matter affecting health of living things or causing property damage.

**Major water pollutants:**
- Nitrate and phosphate fertilizers washed from soil
- Phosphate detergents
- Untreated sewage
- Insecticides and herbicides
- Heavy metal ions
- Acidic and/or basic residues from industrial processes

Phosphate and nitrate fertilizers enter water systems, accelerating growth of surface-water plants like algae. This reduces light for bottom plants, causing die-off. Bacteria decomposing dead plants consume dissolved oxygen faster than natural aeration can replace it, decreasing oxygen levels and killing aquatic animals like fish. This sequence is called **eutrophication**.

**Examples of water pollutants:**
- **Domestic sewage:** Major source of plant nutrients (nitrates, phosphates), causing algal blooms. When algae die, oxygen declines as microorganisms digest them.
- **Solid waste:** Improper disposal of garbage, rubbish, e-waste, construction waste pollutes water.
- **Fertilizers:** Excessive nitrates/phosphates from food/animal waste and inorganic fertilizers.
- **Insecticides (pesticides) and herbicides:** Applied in agriculture, washed into water bodies. Some persist and don't decompose easily.
- **Lead:** Dangerous toxin that readily dissolves in water, causing devastating health effects.

#### **Methods of Reducing Water Pollution**
- Treat water before discharge into rivers and lakes
- Avoid discharging heat-laden water into natural systems
- Recycle industrial and agricultural wastes
- Use moderate amounts of agricultural chemicals and increase organic fertilizers/biological control methods

### **5.4.1 Principles of Green Chemistry**

**"Green chemistry"** is a movement to make chemical industries more environmentally friendly and sustainable. It asks: "Why generate pollution if there's a greener alternative?"

Developing green alternatives begins by considering chemical hazards and properties. Chemists develop manufacturing processes where every stage is environmentally safeâ€”from raw materials to end-of-life product disposal. The process is **"benign by design."**

A green chemistry solution may use safer chemicals (e.g., liquid COâ‚‚ replacing toxic dry-cleaning solvents). It can also improve efficiency. Ibuprofen production was reduced from six steps (40% atom economy) to three steps (~80% atom economy), generating less waste, using less energy, and being more profitable.

The **"12 Principles of Green Chemistry"** involve modern pollution control and cleaner production:

---

#### **1. Prevention**
Better to prevent waste than treat or clean up waste after generation. This involves updating chemical transformations to limit hazardous waste generation, reducing risks from storage, transportation, and treatment.

*Example:* Pulp and paper industry used chlorine compounds producing toxic chlorinated waste. Green chemistry converts wood pulp using oxygen, water, and polyoxometalate salts, producing only water and COâ‚‚ as by-products.

**Simply: "Less Waste = Less Pollution"**

---

#### **2. Atom Economy**
A primary criterion for green chemistry. The idea is to improve processes by avoiding atom waste from reactants to products. Atom economy is the ratio of "mass of desired product" to "total mass of products," expressed as percentage:

For a reaction: A + B â†’ C (desired) + D (by-product)

**Atom Economy = [Mass of C / (Mass of C + D)] أ— 100**

For optimal processes, atom economy should be near or equal to 100%.

**Figure 5.8: Simple diagram showing atom economic reaction**
The figure illustrates greenness across process components:
- Greenness of raw material: nontoxic, sustainable feedstocks
- Greenness of product: nontoxic product
- Greenness of catalyst: nontoxic catalyst
- Greenness of solvent: nontoxic solvent
- Greenness of chemical reaction: atom economic reaction improves selectivity

**Benefits:** Minimizes waste, reduces waste management costs, simplifies storage/transportation, minimizes pollution, prevents illegal pursuits, reduces emissions.

---

#### **3. Less Hazardous Chemical Synthesis**
Synthetic methods should use or generate chemicals with little/no toxicity to environment and human health. Avoid reactions that produce hazardous byproducts.

---

#### **4. Design of Safer Chemicals**
Design chemicals that fulfill their intended purpose but are benign and harmless to ecosystems. Modifications should reflect at the molecular level.

---

#### **5. Safer Solvents and Auxiliaries**
Most industries (polymer, pharmaceutical, chemical) use solvents. While avoidance is ideal, replacement with safer, innocuous solvents is practical.

---

#### **6. Design for Energy Efficiency**
Recognize energy requirements, environmental/economic impacts, and minimize them. Run reactions at ambient conditions (room temperature/pressure) when possible. Achieve through well-maintained equipment, waste heat recovery, solvent removal, proper catalyst use, and combined heat/power systems.

---

#### **7. Use of Renewable Feedstock**
Use starting materials that are renewable rather than depletable. Renewable sources are agricultural products or process wastes; depletable sources are fossil fuels (petroleum, natural gas, coal) or mining operations.

---

#### **8. Reduce Derivatives**
Reactions should occur at particular functional groups, blocking unnecessary waste generation by reducing process steps using enzymes, catalysts, or solvents. This reduces feedstock and utility demand, increasing overall economy and efficiency.

*Example:* Ibuprofen synthesis originally required 514 g reactants per mole (206 g) of ibuprofen (40% atom economy). The 1990s BHC process (now BASF) uses three steps with ~80% atom economy, generating less waste, using less-hazardous recyclable materials, providing cost savings, and earning EPA's Greener Synthetic Pathways Award.

---

#### **9. Catalysis**
Catalysis is a key pillar of Green Chemistry. "Stoichiometric" technologies are primary waste sources, while "Catalytic Processes" achieve environmental and economic goals. Benefits include lower energy consumption, increased selectivity, and decreased use of harmful/toxic agents.

**Examples:**
- **Zeolites and Clays:** Promising benign heterogeneous catalysts replacing harmful catalysts
- **Enzymes:** Biocatalysts from biological sources, renewable and biodegradable

Catalysis helps build a sustainable world and mitigates environmental impacts of unsustainable chemical processes.

---

#### **10. Design for Degradation**
Chemical products should break down into innocuous degradation products at end-of-life, not persisting in the environment. Chemicals should be stable during use but degrade into small, harmless molecules after use.

---

#### **11. Real-time Analysis for Pollution Prevention**
Like live news, real-time analysis in chemical industries enables production, transportation, and pollution prevention. It allows online process control, preventing threats, increasing profit, and ensuring environmental safety. Includes in-process monitoring and control to minimize or eliminate hazardous byproduct formation.

---

#### **12. Inherently Safer Chemistry for Accident Prevention**
Choose safe/safer chemicals to mitigate accident chances. Benefits industry and environment by safeguarding against calamitous industrial/laboratory accidents. **Avoidance of hazard is the goal.**

---

Environmental chemistry encompasses chemistry fields and processes in soil, water, air, and living systems. It's the branch dealing with production, transport, reactions, effects, and fates of chemical species in water, air, terrestrial, and biological environments, and human activity effects.
