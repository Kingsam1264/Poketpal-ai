### Definition

A polygon is a simple closed plane figure formed by three or more line segments joined end to end, no two of which in succession are collinear. The line segments forming the polygon are called sides. The common end point of any two sides is called a vertex of the polygon. The angles formed inside the polygon are called interior angles.

For instance: Triangle (three sides) is the simplest polygon. Other polygons are quadrilaterals (four sides), pentagons (five sides), and so on.

### Definition

ii. A polygon is said to be a **concave polygon** when there is at least one interior angle whose measure is more than 180 degrees. The vertices of a concave polygon present inwards and also outwards.

This indicates as the number of sides of a polygon increases, the number of triangles also increases. Furthermore, the sum of measure of interior angles of the polygon increases.

To summarize the above procedure, if n is the number of sides of a polygon, then n−2 non-overlapping triangles are formed. Hence, we conclude the following statement:

**Sum of the measure of interior angles of n-sided polygon is equal to (n−2) × 180°.**

**Example 1:** Find the sum of the measure of interior angles of hexagon.

**Solution:** Hexagon is a 6-sided polygon. We use n = 6 in the above formula. Hence, the sum of measure of interior angles of a hexagon is equal to (n−2) × 180° = (6−2) × 180° = 4 × 180° = 720°.

**Example 2:** The sum of interior angle of a polygon is 1,080°. How many sides does the polygon have?

**Solution:** We need the value of n which satisfies (n−2) × 180° = 1,080°.
n−2 = 1,080° / 180° = 6. So, n = 8. The polygon is octagon.

### Sum of the Exterior Angles of a Polygon

What can you say about the sum of exterior angles of a polygon? Does the sum depend on the number of sides of the polygon?

Let us see how to find sum of exterior angles of a polygon.

**Example 1:** Determine the sum of the exterior angles of a triangle.

**Solution:**

**Figure 6.7: Triangle with Interior and Exterior Angles**  
The image shows triangle ABC with interior angles a, b, c and corresponding exterior angles α, β, γ.

Consider a triangle ABC as shown in figure 6.7. You will observe that angles α, β, and γ are exterior angles, and angles a, b, and c are interior angles of ∆ABC.

The sum of the interior angles of a triangle is 180°: a + b + c = 180°. Also, we note that (a + b + c) + (α + β + γ) = 3 × 180° (measure of three straight lines). This leads to α + β + γ = 360°.

Hence, the sum of the measure of exterior angles of a triangle is 360°.

**Example 2:** Find the sum of the measure of exterior angles of a quadrilateral.

**Solution:**

**Figure 6.8: Quadrilateral with Exterior Angles**  
The image shows quadrilateral ABCD with exterior angles α, β, γ, δ and interior angles a, b, c, d.

Consider a quadrilateral ABCD as shown in figure 6.8 with exterior angles α, β, γ, and δ. Its interior angles are a, b, c, and d. The sum of interior angles of a quadrilateral is (n−2) × 180° = 2 × 180° = 360° where n is the number of sides of the polygon. For our case, we know that n = 4.

Hence, a + b + c + d = 360°. From the figure we conclude that (a + b + c + d) + (α + β + γ + δ) = 4 × 180° = 720° and α + β + γ + δ = 360°.

Hence, the measure of the exterior angles of a quadrilateral is 360°.

### Sum of the Exterior Angles of n-Sided Polygon

Similarly, if we take an n-sided polygon, the sum of the interior angles of a polygon is (n−2) × 180°.

For such a polygon, we also have n straight lines. Each one is the sum of interior and exterior angle. Therefore, we have:

The sum of interior and exterior angles of n-sided polygon is n × 180°.

Using this concept, we can derive the sum of exterior angles of a polygon as follows.

For an n-sided polygon,
Sum of interior angles of a polygon + sum of exterior angles of a polygon = n × 180°.

Using (n−2) × 180° in place of sum of interior angles of a polygon, the equation becomes:
(n−2) × 180° + sum of exterior angles of a polygon = n × 180°.

Using distribution we get:
n × 180° − 360° + sum of exterior angles of a polygon = n × 180°.

Collecting like terms and rearranging gives us:
Sum of the measure of exterior angles of a polygon = 360°

Hence, we can conclude that:

**For any n-sided polygon, the sum of the measure of exterior angles of the polygon is 360°.**

**Note:** At each vertex of a polygon, the sum of interior and exterior angles is 180°.

a. the remaining interior angle of the triangle.
b. the measure of exterior angle at each vertex.

**Solution:**

Assume the degree measure of the remaining interior angle of the triangle be x. Since the sum of the measure of interior angles of a triangle is 180°, then x + 60° + 40° = 180°. x = 180° − (60° + 40°) = 80°.

A polygon is said to be **regular** if it is equiangular (having equal angles) and equilateral (having equal sides).

What is the measure of each interior and exterior angle of n-sided regular polygon?

For n-sided polygon, recall that:
i. The sum of the measure of interior angles is (n−2) × 180°.
ii. The sum of the measure of exterior angles is 360°.

Using this and the definition of regular polygon, the measure of each interior angle of n-sided regular polygon is determined by:

$$\frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n}$$ (Since a regular polygon is equiangular)

And the measure of each exterior angle is $$\frac{360°}{n}$$ (since there are n exterior angles with same measure).

**Example:** Find the measure of each interior and each exterior angle of a regular pentagon.

**Solution:** Given a regular pentagon which is five-sided (n = 5). So each interior angle degree measure is:

$$\frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n} = \frac{(5-2) \times 180°}{5} = \frac{3 \times 180°}{5} = 108°$$

The measure of each exterior angle is $$\frac{360°}{5} = 72°$$.

Now, let us discuss symmetry of regular polygons:

- If two parts of a figure are identical after folding or flipping, then it is said to be symmetric. To be symmetrical, one half must be the mirror image of the other. If the figure is not symmetrical then it is said to be asymmetrical.
- Line of symmetry is a line through which the two halves of the figure match exactly. It is also called mirror line. A figure can have more than one line of symmetry. An asymmetrical figure has no line of symmetry.

**Note:** All regular polygons are symmetrical shapes so that we have lines of symmetry for regular polygons.

### Measure of Central Angles

The central angle for a regular polygon is an angle formed at the center of a circle by two consecutive radii from the vertex of a polygon (see figure 6.16, ∠BOC is a central angle). For n-sided regular polygon, we can obtain n isosceles triangles (see also figure 6.16, ΔBOC is one of such isosceles triangles). Recall that the sum of measures of angles at a point is 360°. Therefore, the sum of the central angles is 360°. Hence, the measure of each central angle of n-sided regular polygon is:

$$\frac{360°}{n}$$

(each interior angle of a regular polygon has the same measure).

**Example:** Find the measure of central angle of a regular polygon inscribed in a circle which has the given number of sides: a) 5, b) 8.

**Solution:**
a. The measure of the central angle depends on the number of sides of a regular polygon. For n = 5, the measure of each central angle is $$\frac{360°}{5} = 72°$$.

b. For n = 8, the measure of each central angle is $$\frac{360°}{8} = 45°$$.

### Perimeter, Area and Apothem of Regular Polygons Inscribed in a Circle

There is a close relationship between the perimeter, area, side length and apothem of an n-sided regular polygon which is inscribed in a circle. We discuss a particular case as an illustrative example and we develop the formula through the process for the general n-sided regular polygon.

**Example:** In figure 6.17, the regular hexagon is inscribed in a circle with center O and radius r. Express the side length s, perimeter P, apothem a, and area A of the regular hexagon using r.

**Solution:**

**Figure 6.17: Regular Hexagon Inscribed in Circle**  
The image shows regular hexagon ABCDEF inscribed in a circle with center O and radius r. Triangle AOB is highlighted as one of six equilateral triangles formed by connecting the center to two adjacent vertices.

Given that ABCDEF is a regular hexagon. It has 6 sides. Triangle AOB is one of the equilateral triangles as shown in figure 6.17 (since ∠AOB = 360° ÷ 6 = 60°, ∠OAB = ∠OBA = (180° − 60°) ÷ 2 = 60°). There are 6 congruent equilateral triangles inside this regular hexagon. So that s = r, P = 6s = 6r.

Draw a perpendicular bisector from O to AB at G. OG is the apothem a of the hexagon.

Here ΔAOG ≅ ΔBOG (SSS). So m(∠AOG) = m(∠BOG) = 60° ÷ 2 = 30°. Then, a = r cos 30° = $$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$r.

The area of ΔAOB = $$\frac{1}{2}$$AB × OG = $$\frac{1}{2}$$ar = $$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}$$r².

The area of the hexagon ABCDEF = A = 6 × ΔAOB = 6 × $$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}$$r² = $$\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}$$r².

**Example 1:** Suppose a regular polygon (n-gon) is inscribed in a circle with center O and radius r. Find the side length s, perimeter P, apothem a, and area A of the regular n-gon. (In this case, n-gon is not necessarily a hexagon, but Fig 6.17 may help our understanding).

**Solution:**

A regular n-gon is given in the figure. It has n sides. Triangle AOB is one of such isosceles triangles as shown in figure 6.16. Moreover, there are n congruent isosceles triangles inside this regular n-gon.

Draw a perpendicular bisector from O to AB at G. OG is the apothem a of the n-gon. Here, ΔAOG ≅ ΔBOG (SSS).

And m(∠AOB) = 360° ÷ n = $$\frac{360°}{n}$$.

So that, m(∠AOG) = m(∠BOG) = $$\frac{360°}{n} ÷ 2 = \frac{180°}{n}$$.

Then, $$\frac{s}{2} = r \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$ which implies $$s = 2r \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$.

$$P = n × s = 2nr \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$ and $$a = r \cos(\frac{180°}{n})$$.

The area of ΔAOG = $$\frac{1}{2}$$AB × OG = $$\frac{1}{2}$$sa. So, the area of the n-gon is A = n × area of isosceles triangle. This is $$A = \frac{1}{2}nas$$, but P = ns so that $$A = \frac{1}{2}aP$$.

The above example leads us to state the following theorem.

### Theorem 6.1

For n-sided regular polygon inscribed in a circle of radius r, length of side s, apothem a, perimeter P and area A are determined by:

$$s = 2r \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$
$$a = r \cos(\frac{180°}{n})$$
$$P = 2nr \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$
$$A = \frac{1}{2}aP$$

**Example 2:**
a. Find the length of side of a square inscribed in a circle of radius 5cm.
b. Find the apothem of a regular pentagon inscribed in a circle of radius 6cm.
c. Find the area of a regular nonagon inscribed in a circle whose radius is 10cm. (Use a calculator or a trigonometric table whenever necessary)

**Solution:**
a. By the formula in Theorem 6.1, $$s = 2r \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$. Using r = 5cm and n = 4, the length of the side is:
$$s = 2(5cm) \sin(\frac{180°}{4}) = 10cm × \sin(45°) = 10cm × \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = 5\sqrt{2}cm$$

b. Given r = 6cm and n = 5, we can determine the apothem (a) by using the formula $$a = r \cos(\frac{180°}{n})$$.
$$a = 6cm × \cos(\frac{180°}{5}) = 6cm × \cos(36°)$$. Using scientific calculator or trigonometric table, cos(36°) ≈ 0.8090. Hence, a ≈ 4.854cm.

c. The area of a regular polygon is determined by:
$$A = \frac{1}{2}aP = \frac{1}{2}(r \cos(\frac{180°}{n})) × 2nr \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$

Using n = 9 and r = 10cm,
$$A = nr^2 \cos(\frac{180°}{n}) \sin(\frac{180°}{n}) = 9(100cm^2) \cos(20°) \sin(20°)$$

Using trigonometric table/Scientific calculator: cos(20°) ≈ 0.9397 and sin(20°) ≈ 0.3420, hence, A = 289.240cm².

## Summary

1. A polygon is a simple closed plane figure formed by three or more line segments joined end to end, no two of which in succession are collinear. The line segments forming the polygon are called sides. The common end point of any two sides is called a vertex of the polygon. The angles formed inside the polygon are called interior angles.

2. i) A polygon is said to be a convex polygon when the measure of each interior angle is less than 180°.
   ii) A polygon is said to be a concave polygon when there is at least one interior angle whose measure is more than 180 degrees.

3. The sum of the measure of interior angles of n-sided polygon is determined by (n−2) × 180°.

4. For any n-sided polygon, the sum of the measure of exterior angles of the polygon is 360°.

5. At each vertex of a polygon, the external and internal angle add up to 180°.

6. A regular polygon is a convex polygon with all sides equal and all interior angles equal.

7. i) The measure of each interior angle of a regular n-sided polygon is $$\frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n}$$.
   ii) The measure of each exterior angle of a regular n-sided polygon is $$\frac{360°}{n}$$.
   iii) The measure of each central angle of a regular n-sided polygon is $$\frac{360°}{n}$$.

8. If two parts of a figure are identical after folding through the line of symmetry, then it is said to be symmetric. A symmetrical figure has at least one line of symmetry. An asymmetrical figure has no line of symmetry.

9. An n-sided regular polygon has n lines of symmetry.

10. An inscribed polygon is a polygon in which all vertices lie on a circle. The polygon is inscribed in the circle and the circle is circumscribed about the polygon.

11. A circumscribed polygon is a polygon in which each side touches a circle. The circle is inscribed in the polygon and the polygon is circumscribed about the circle.

12. A circle can always be inscribed in or circumscribed about any regular polygon.

13. An apothem of a regular polygon is a perpendicular segment from a midpoint of a side of a regular polygon to the center of the circle circumscribed about the polygon.

14. Formula for the length of side s, apothem a, perimeter P and area A of the regular polygon with n-sides and radius r are given by:

$$s = 2r \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$
$$a = r \cos(\frac{180°}{n})$$
$$P = 2nr \sin(\frac{180°}{n})$$
$$A = \frac{1}{2}aP$$
