## Introduction
You can add, subtract or multiply polynomial expressions using the basic rules defined on the set of real numbers. Then, in these computational processes the result gives another polynomial expression. On the other hand, if you divide a polynomial expression by another polynomial expression, the result may not be a polynomial expression. In this unit, you will learn about quotients of polynomial expressions which are called rational expressions.

- An expression that can be written in the form aₙxⁿ + aₙ₋₁xⁿ⁻¹ + ... + a₁x + a₀, where n is a non-negative integer and a₀,...,aₙ are real numbers, is called a polynomial expression.

Rational expressions are defined as quotients of polynomials.

**Definition 2.1**
Given two polynomials p(x) and q(x) with q(x)≠0, an expression that can be expressed in the form p(x)/q(x) is called a rational expression. In a rational expression p(x)/q(x), the polynomial p(x) is called numerator and q(x) is called denominator.

**Example**
Which of the following are rational expressions?
a. (2x+3)/(5x²+9x-4)
b. (6x²+4x-7)/(x²+1)
c. 2/(x³-1)
d. x²+3x-1
e. 2/(√(x²+1))

**Solution**
a. (2x+3)/(5x²+9x-4) is a rational expression, because both 2x+3 and 5x²+9x-4 are polynomial expressions.
b. (6x²+4x-7)/(x²+1) is a rational expression, because both 6x²+4x-7 and x²+1 are polynomial expressions.
c. 2/(x³-1) is a rational expression, because 2 is a constant polynomial and x³-1 is a polynomial expression.
d. x²+3x-1 is a rational expression, because the expression x²+3x-1 can be expressed as (x²+3x-1)/1 and both x²+3x-1 and 1 are polynomial expressions.
e. 2/(√(x²+1)) is not a rational expression, because the denominator is not a polynomial expression.

**Definition 2.2**
Given a rational expression p(x)/q(x), the set of all real numbers x such that q(x) ≠ 0 is called the domain of p(x)/q(x). That is, the domain of p(x)/q(x) is {x∈R: q(x)≠0}.

**Example**

a. (2x²+x)/(6x+3)
b. 3/(x²+5x+6)
c. (5x+9)/(x²+1)

b. The denominator of the given rational expression is x²+5x+6 and x²+5x+6=0 implies (x+2)(x+3)=0 ⇒ x=-2 or x=-3. Thus, the domain of 3/(x²+5x+6) is {x∈R| x≠-2 and x≠-3}.
c. The denominator of the given rational expression is x²+1 and x²+1≠0 for all x∈R. Therefore, domain of (5x+9)/(x²+1) is R, the set of all real numbers.

### 2.1.3 Simplifications of Rational Expressions

**Activity 2.3**
Write each one of the following rational numbers in its lowest term.
a. 15/28
b. 4/6
c. 10/100
d. 18/3

From your responses in Activity 2.3, observe the following two important points to write a given rational number in its lowest form:
1. First factorize both the numerator and the denominator; and
2. Cancel out the common factors of both the numerator and the denominator.

Recall from your knowledge about rational numbers that:
i. a rational number a/b, where a and b are integers and b≠0, is in its lowest term if the greatest common factor of a and b is 1;
ii. given three integers a, b, c, with b≠0 and c≠0, you have: (ac)/(bc) = a/b.

**Definition 2.3**
A rational expression p(x)/q(x) is said to be in lowest term or in its simplest form, if only common factor of the numerator p(x) and the denominator q(x) is 1.

**Example 1**
Simplify the rational expression (x+1)/(x²-1).

**Solution**
(x+1)/(x²-1) = (x+1)×1/(x+1)(x-1) (Factorize both the numerator and the denominator.)
= 1/(x-1) for x≠-1 (Cancel out the common factor, x+1, of both the numerator and denominator.)

Observe that for x=-1, the expression (x+1)/(x²-1) is not defined, but the expression 1/(x-1) is defined at x=-1, and its value is 1/(-1-1) = -1/2.

Therefore, (x+1)/(x²-1) = 1/(x-1) for x≠-1.

To simplify a given rational expression, first you have to determine the domain of the given expression.

**Steps to simplify a given rational expression p(x)/q(x)**
1. Step 1: Determine the domain of the expression p(x)/q(x)
2. Step 2: Factorize both p(x) and q(x) (if possible); and
3. Step 3: Cancel out the common factors of both p(x) and q(x) to obtain the simplified form of the given rational expression

**Example 2**
Simplify each of the following rational expressions.
a. (3x+6)/(x²+4x+4)
b. (x³+2x²+x)/(x²-1)
c. (x-1)/(x²-4x+3)

**Solution**
a. x²+4x+4=0 implies x=-2. Then, the domain of (3x+6)/(x²+4x+4) is {x∈R| x≠-2}.
(3x+6)/(x²+4x+4) = 3(x+2)/(x+2)² (Factorize both 3x+6 and x²+4x+4.)
= 3/(x+2) for x≠-2 (Cancel out x+2 from both the numerator and the denominator.)

Therefore, (3x+6)/(x²+4x+4) = 3/(x+2) for x≠-2 in a simplified form as 1 is the only common factor of 3 and x+2.

b. x²-1=0 implies x=1 or x=-1. Then domain of (x³+2x²+x)/(x²-1) is {x∈R| x≠-1,1}.
(x³+2x²+x)/(x²-1) = x(x+1)(x+1)/(x+1)(x-1) (Factorize both x³+2x²+x and x²-1.)
= x(x+1)/(x-1) for x≠-1 (Cancel out x+1 from the numerator and the denominator.)

Therefore, (x³+2x²+x)/(x²-1) = (x²+x)/(x-1) for x≠-1.

c. x²-4x+3=0 implies x=1 and x=3. Then the domain of (x-1)/(x²-4x+3) is {x∈R| x≠1,3}.
(x-1)/(x²-4x+3) = (x-1)/(x-1)(x-3) (Factorize x²-4x+3.)
= 1/(x-3) for x≠1 (Cancel out x-1 from both the numerator and the denominator.)

Therefore, (x-1)/(x²-4x+3) = 1/(x-3) for x≠1.

Let a/b and c/d be rational numbers with b≠0 and d≠0. The basic four operations - addition, subtraction, multiplication and division on rational numbers are given as follows:
i. (a/b) + (c/d) = (ad+bc)/(bd)
ii. (a/b) - (c/d) = (ad-bc)/(bd)
iii. (a/b) × (c/d) = ac/(bd)
iv. (a/b) ÷ (c/d) = ad/(bc) (c≠0)

**Addition and Subtraction of Rational Expressions**

Addition and subtraction of rational expressions are extensions of the same operations on the set of rational numbers and they are defined as follows:
Consider two rational expressions p(x)/q(x) and r(x)/s(x) with q(x)≠0 and s(x)≠0. Then
a. p(x)/q(x) + r(x)/s(x) = [p(x)s(x) + r(x)q(x)]/[q(x)s(x)]
b. p(x)/q(x) - r(x)/s(x) = [p(x)s(x) - r(x)q(x)]/[q(x)s(x)]

**Example 1**
Perform each of the following operations:
a. (x+1)/(x²+2x) + 3x/(x+5)
b. (x+1)/(x²+2x) - 3x/(x+5)

**Solution**
a. (x+1)/(x²+2x) + 3x/(x+5) = [(x+1)(x+5) + 3x(x²+2x)]/[(x²+2x)(x+5)]
= [x²+6x+5 + 3x³+6x²]/[x³+7x²+10x]
= [3x³+7x²+6x+5]/[x³+7x²+10x]

Therefore, (x+1)/(x²+2x) + 3x/(x+5) = (3x³+7x²+6x+5)/(x³+7x²+10x).

b. (x+1)/(x²+2x) - 3x/(x+5) = [(x+1)(x+5) - 3x(x²+2x)]/[(x²+2x)(x+5)]
= [x²+6x+5 - 3x³-6x²]/[x³+7x²+10x]
= [-3x³-5x²+6x+5]/[x³+7x²+10x]

Therefore, (x+1)/(x²+2x) - 3x/(x+5) = (-3x³-5x²+6x+5)/(x³+7x²+10x).

**Multiplication and Division of Rational Expressions**

Multiplication and division of rational expressions are extensions of the same operations on the set of rational numbers and they are defined as follows:
Consider two rational expressions p(x)/q(x) and r(x)/s(x) with q(x)≠0 and s(x)≠0. Then,
i. p(x)/q(x) × r(x)/s(x) = p(x)r(x)/q(x)s(x)
ii. p(x)/q(x) ÷ r(x)/s(x) = p(x)s(x)/q(x)r(x) (r(x)≠0)

**Example 2**
Perform each of the following operations:
a. (x+1)/(x-1) × (x+2)/(x²+3x)
b. (x+1)/(x-1) ÷ (x+2)/(x²+3x)

**Solution**
a. (x+1)/(x-1) × (x+2)/(x²+3x) = (x+1)(x+2)/[(x-1)(x²+3x)] = (x²+3x+2)/(x³+2x²-3x)

Therefore, (x+1)/(x-1) × (x+2)/(x²+3x) = (x²+3x+2)/(x³+2x²-3x).

b. (x+1)/(x-1) ÷ (x+2)/(x²+3x) = (x+1)(x²+3x)/[(x-1)(x+2)] = (x³+4x²+3x)/(x²+x-2)

Therefore, (x+1)/(x-1) ÷ (x+2)/(x²+3x) = (x³+4x²+3x)/(x²+x-2).

### 2.1.5 Decomposition of Rational Expressions into Partial Fractions

**Activity 2.5**
Consider the rational expression (x+1)/(x²-3x+2). Then,
a. factorize x²-3x+2
b. show that (x+1)/(x²-3x+2) = 2/(x-2) - 1/(x-1)

In Activity 2.5, the fractional expressions 1/(x-1) and 2/(x-2) are called partial fractions and writing (x+1)/(x²-3x+2) = 2/(x-1) + 3/(x-2) is decomposing the given expression as a sum of partial fractions.

The main objective in this discussion is to determine how to write a given rational expression as a sum of partial fractions. The idea of writing a rational expression as a sum of partial fractions is important, especially in a branch of Mathematics called Calculus and you will be introduced to the topic calculus in Grade 12.

**Definition 2.4**
Two polynomials p(x) and q(x) are equal if both polynomials have the same degree and the terms of the same degree in both polynomials have equal coefficients.

**Example 1**
Let p(x) = 3x³+5x²-7x+c and q(x) = ax⁴+bx³+5x²-7x+4. Find the values of the constants a, b and c if p(x) = q(x) for all x∈R.

**Solution**
p(x) = q(x) for all x∈R if and only if terms of the same degree in both polynomials have equal coefficients.
i. The coefficient of the term with degree 4 in p(x) is 0 and the coefficient of the term with degree 4 in q(x) is a. This implies, a=0.
ii. The coefficient of the term with degree 3 in p(x) is 3 and the coefficient of the term with degree 3 in q(x) is b. This implies, b=3.
iii. The constant term in p(x) is c and the constant term in q(x) is 4. This implies, c=4.

**Definition 2.5**
A rational expression p/q(x) is called a proper rational expression if the degree of p(x) is less than degree of q(x), otherwise it is called an improper rational expression.

**Note**
Note that, given an improper rational expression p(x)/q(x), using long division of polynomial expressions, we can write it as f(x) + r(x)/q(x) where f(x) and r(x) are polynomial expressions and the degree of r(x) is less than the degree of q(x).

**Example 2**
Write the rational expression (5x³+7x²+5x+9)/(x²+1) in the form of f(x) + r(x)/q(x) where f(x), r(x) and q(x) are polynomial expressions and the degree of r(x) is less than the degree of q(x).

**Solution**
Using long division on polynomial expressions, the polynomial expression 5x³+7x²+5x+9 can be written as 5x³+7x²+5x+9 = (x²+1)(5x+7) + (-2x+2).

Then (5x³+7x²+5x+9)/(x²+1) = (5x+7) + (-2x+2)/(x²+1).

In writing a given rational expression as a sum of partial fractions, it is always important to factorize a polynomial expression into a product of polynomial expressions of smaller degree whenever possible.

The following Theorem is important in factorizing a polynomial into a product of polynomials of smaller degrees.

**Theorem 2.1**
Any non-constant polynomial expression with real coefficients can be factorized as a product of linear and/or irreducible quadratic factors with the possibility of some powers.

**Steps in decomposing a given rational expression as a sum of partial fractions:**

I. If ax + b, for a ≠ 0, is a factor in the denominator of the given rational expression and (ax + b)ⁿ is not a factor, then the partial fraction that corresponds to this factor is A/(ax+b) where A is a constant that we have to determine.

**Example 4**
Decompose (6x+2)/(x²+6x+5) as a sum of partial fractions.

**Solution**
The given rational expression is a proper rational expression because the degree of 6x+2 is 1, which is less than the degree of x²+6x+5, which is 2.

First let us factorize x²+6x+5.
That is, x²+6x+5 = (x+5)(x+1).

Then, (6x+2)/(x²+6x+5) = A/(x+5) + B/(x+1) for some constants A and B.

Now, let us determine A and B and to do so write:
A(x+5) + B(x+1) = (A+B)x + (5A+B)

Then, for x ≠ -1 and x ≠ -5, the polynomial expressions, 6x+2 and (A+B)x + (5A+B) are equal.

That is, A+B = 6 and 5A+B = 2, the coefficients of terms of the same degree are equal.

Then we can solve the two equations simultaneously as follows:
A + B = 6
5A + B = 2
-4A = 4

This implies A = -1 and from the equation A+B = 6, we have B = 6-(-1) = 7.

Hence, (6x+2)/(x²+6x+5) = -1/(x+5) + 7/(x+1) (written as a sum of partial fractions).

II. If (ax + b)ᵏ, for a ≠ 0, is a factor in the denominator, for some integer k > 1 and (ax + b)ᵏ⁺¹ is not a factor, then the corresponding partial fractions that correspond to this factor are:
A₁/(ax+b) + A₂/(ax+b)² + ... + Aₖ/(ax+b)ᵏ
where A₁,...,Aₖ are constants that we have to determine.

**Example 5**
Decompose (x+5)/(x²+4x+4) as a sum of partial fractions.

**Solution**
First let us factorize x²+4x+4.
That is, x²+4x+4 = (x+2)².

Then, (x+5)/(x²+4x+4) = A₁/(x+2) + A₂/(x+2)² = [A₁(x+2) + A₂]/(x+2)² = [A₁x + (2A₁+A₂)]/(x+2)².

For all x∈R and x ≠ -2, we have x+5 = A₁x + (2A₁+A₂).

This implies A₁ = 1 and 2A₁+A₂ = 5 (Coefficients of terms of the same degree are equal). Then, solving for A₂ gives you A₂ = 5-2A₁ = 5-2(1) = 3.

Therefore, (x+5)/(x²+4x+4) = 1/(x+2) + 3/(x+2)² (written as a sum of partial fractions).

III. If ax² + bx + c, for a ≠ 0, is a factor in the denominator with b²-4ac < 0 and (ax² + bx + c)ⁿ is not a factor, then the partial fraction that corresponds to this factor is (Ax+B)/(ax²+bx+c), where A and B are constants that we have to determine.

**Example 6**
Write (x²+3x+5)/[(x+2)(x²+x+1)] as a sum of partial fractions.

**Solution**
The quadratic expression x² + x + 1 is irreducible, because the discriminant: 1²-4(1)(1) = -3 and -3<0.

Then, (x²+3x+5)/[(x+2)(x²+x+1)] = A/(x+2) + (A₁x+B₁)/(x²+x+1).

This implies:
x²+3x+5 = A(x²+x+1) + (A₁x+B₁)(x+2)
= (A+A₁)x² + (A+2A₁+B₁)x + (A+2B₁)

Thus, for x≠-2, x²+3x+5 = (A+A₁)x² + (A+2A₁+B₁)x + (A+2B₁).

Thus, from the equality of the two polynomial expressions, we have:
A + A₁ = 1
A + 2A₁ + B₁ = 3
A + 2B₁ = 5

Solving the three equations simultaneously gives us A=1, A₁=0 and B₁=2.

Therefore, (x²+3x+5)/[(x+2)(x²+x+1)] = 1/(x+2) + 2/(x²+x+1) (written as a sum of partial fractions).

IV. If (ax²+bx+c)ᵏ, for a≠0 and b²-4ac<0, is a factor in the denominator for k > 1 and (ax²+bx+c)ᵏ⁺¹ is not a factor, then the corresponding sum of partial fractions to this factor is:
(A₁x+B₁)/(ax²+bx+c) + (A₂x+B₂)/(ax²+bx+c)² + ... + (Aₖx+Bₖ)/(ax²+bx+c)ᵏ
where A₁,...,Aₖ and B₁,...,Bₖ are constants that we have to determine.

**Example 7**
Write (6x²+5x+2)/(x²+2x+3)² as a sum of partial fractions.

**Solution**
The quadratic expression x²+2x+3 is irreducible, because 2²-4(1)(3) = 4-12 = -8 and -8<0.

Then, (6x²+5x+2)/(x²+2x+3)² = (A₁x+B₁)/(x²+2x+3) + (A₂x+B₂)/(x²+2x+3)².

Thus:
6x²+5x+2 = A₁x³ + (2A₁+B₁)x² + (3A₁+2B₁+A₂)x + (3B₁+B₂) for all x∈R.

This implies:
A₁ = 0
2A₁+B₁ = 6
3A₁+2B₁+A₂ = 5
3B₁+B₂ = 2 (the coefficients of terms of the same degree are equal).

Then, solving four equations simultaneously gives you:
A₁=0, B₁=6, A₂=-7, B₂=-16.

Therefore, (6x²+5x+2)/(x²+2x+3)² = 6/(x²+2x+3) + (-7x-16)/(x²+2x+3)² (written as a sum of partial fractions).

**Note**
To decompose an improper fraction of the form p(x)/q(x), first write p(x)/q(x) = f(x) + r(x)/q(x) where the degree of r(x) is less than the degree of q(x) and then write r(x)/q(x) as a sum of partial fractions using any one of the methods discussed above.

**Example 8**
Write (x²+7x+1)/(x²+3x+2) as a sum of partial fractions.

**Solution**
Since the degree of the numerator is not less than degree of the denominator, perform polynomial long division.

(x²+7x+1)/(x²+3x+2) = 1 + (4x-1)/(x²+3x+2).

Then write (4x-1)/(x²+3x+2) as a sum of partial fractions.

That is, (4x-1)/(x²+3x+2) = (4x-1)/[(x+1)(x+2)] = A/(x+1) + B/(x+2).

(4x-1)/[(x+1)(x+2)] = [A(x+2) + B(x+1)]/[(x+1)(x+2)] = [(A+B)x + (2A+B)]/[(x+1)(x+2)]

The denominators of the two expressions are equal, so we require the equality of the numerators:
4x-1 = (A+B)x + (2A+B)

Thus, solve the following system of two linear equations:
A + B = 4
2A + B = -1

Solving these two equations gives you: A = -5 and B = 9.

Therefore, (x²+7x+1)/(x²+3x+2) = 1 + 9/(x+1) - 5/(x+2).

### 2.2.1 Rational Equations

In this section, you will learn how to solve equations involving rational expressions.

**Definition 2.6**
Any equation that involves only rational expressions is called a rational equation. That is, a rational equation is an equation that can be reduced to the form p(x)/q(x) = 0, where p(x) and q(x) are polynomial expressions and q(x)≠0.

**Example 1**
Write each of the following equations as p(x)/q(x) = 0, where p(x) and q(x) are polynomial expressions and q(x)≠0.
a. 1/(x+1) = 2x/(x-1)
b. 3x = (2x+5)/(x²-2)

**Solution**
a. 1/(x+1) = 2x/(x-1) implies 1/(x+1) - 2x/(x-1) = 0 (Subtracting 2x/(x-1) from both sides.)
= [1(x-1) - 2x(x+1)]/[(x+1)(x-1)] = 0 (Simplifying the rational expression.)
= (-2x²-x-1)/(x²-1) = 0 is the required rational equation.

b. 3x = (2x+5)/(x²-2) implies 3x - (2x+5)/(x²-2) = 0 (Subtracting (2x+5)/(x²-2) from both sides.)
= [3x(x²-2) - 1(2x+5)]/(x²-2) = 0 (Simplifying the rational expression)
= (3x³-8x-5)/(x²-2) = 0 is the required rational equation.

**Steps in Solving Rational Equations**
Given a rational equation, solving the rational equation is finding all the possible numbers in the domain of the given rational expression that satisfy the given rational equation.

The following are steps that you can follow to solve rational equations.
1. Step 1: Reduce the given rational equation to the form p(x)/q(x) = 0 (without simplifying the given expression)
2. Step 2: Solve the equation q(x) = 0
3. Step 3: Solve the equation p(x) = 0

Therefore, the solution set is the set of all real numbers in Step 3 without the set of numbers obtained in Step 2.

**Example 2**
Solve each of the following rational equations:
a. 1/3 + 2/x = 1/2
b. 2/(x+1) = (x+3)/(x+1) + 3/(x+1)
c. x/(x²-9) = x/(x+3)

**Solution**
a. 1/3 + 2/x = 1/2 implies 1/3 + 2/x - 1/2 = 0 (Subtracting 1/2 from both sides.)
= [2x + 6 - 3x]/(6x) = 0 (Simplifying the rational expression.)
= (4x-3)/(6x) = 0

Then, solving the linear equation 4x-3 = 0 gives us x = 3/4.

Therefore, the solution set of the given equation is {3/4}.

b. 2/(x+1) = (x+3)/(x+1) + 3/(x+1) implies 2/(x+1) - (x+3)/(x+1) - 3/(x+1) = 0 (Subtracting from both sides.)
= [2 - (x+3) - 3]/(x+1) = 0
= (x-1)/(x+1) = 0

Then, determine the value(s) of x that make the denominator zero.
That is, x+1 = 0 ⇒ x = -1.
Thus, x = -1 will be excluded from the solution set of the given rational equation.

Then, solving the equation x-1 = 0 gives us x = 1.

Therefore, the solution set of the given equation is {1}.

c. x/(x²-9) = x/(x+3) implies x/(x²-9) - x/(x+3) = 0 (Subtracting x/(x+3) from both sides.)
= [x - x(x-3)]/[(x-3)(x+3)] = 0
= [x - x² + 3x]/[(x-3)(x+3)] = 0
= (-x²+4x)/[(x-3)(x+3)] = 0

Find the value(s) of x that make the denominator zero.
That is, (x-3)(x+3) = 0 ⇒ x-3 = 0 or x+3 = 0.
That is, x = 3 or x = -3 and both x = 3 and x = -3 are excluded from the solution set of the given rational equation.

Then, -x²+4x = 0 and this is a quadratic equation. Thus, using the quadratic formula:
x = [-4 ± √(16-4(-1)(0))]/[2(-1)] = [-4 ± √16]/(-2) = [-4 ± 4]/(-2).

That is, x = 0 or x = 4.

Therefore, the solution set of the given equation is {0, 4}.

### 2.2.2 Rational Inequalities

**Definition 2.7**
Any inequality that involves only rational expressions is called a rational inequality. That is, a rational inequality is an inequality that can be reduced to the form p(x)/q(x) > 0, p(x)/q(x) ≥ 0, p(x)/q(x) < 0, or p(x)/q(x) ≤ 0, where p(x) and q(x) are polynomial expressions and q(x)≠0.

**Example 1**
Which of the following are rational inequalities?
a. 1 > 2/(3x)
b. 2x < √(x+5)
c. 1/x² ≥ 1/(x²+1)

**Solution**
a. Since both 1 and 2/(3x) are rational expressions, 1 > 2/(3x) is a rational inequality.
b. √(x+5) is not a rational expression. Thus, 2x < √(x+5) is not a rational inequality.
c. 1/x² and 1/(x²+1) are rational expressions. Thus, 1/x² ≥ 1/(x²+1) is a rational inequality.

The solution set of a rational inequality is the set of all real numbers in the domain of the given rational expression that satisfy the given inequality.

**Steps in Solving Rational Inequalities**
1. Step 1: Determine the domain of the rational inequality
2. Step 2: Write the given inequality to the form p(x)/q(x) > 0 or p(x)/q(x) ≥ 0 or p(x)/q(x) < 0 or p(x)/q(x) ≤ 0
3. Step 3: Find the zeros of both p(x) and q(x)
4. Step 4: Divide the number line into different intervals using the numbers that are not in the domain of the inequality in Step 1 and numbers in Step 3. The method is called the sign chart method.
5. Step 5: Find the interval that satisfies the given inequality using the product rule

**Example 2**
Solve the inequality (x+1)/(x-1) > 0.

**Solution**
To determine the domain of the given inequality, solve the equation x-1 = 0.
This implies, x = 1 and then the domain of the given inequality is {x∈R| x≠1}.

Solve the equation x+1 = 0. This implies x = -1.

Using the numbers -1 and 1, divide the number line into three intervals as in the table below.

| Interval | x+1 | x-1 | (x+1)/(x-1) |
|----------|-----|-----|-------------|
| x<-1     | -   | -   | +           |
| -1<x<1   | +   | -   | -           |
| x>1      | +   | +   | +           |

Then, from the given sign chart, observe that:
i. (x+1)/(x-1) is positive on (-∞, -1)∪(1, ∞)
ii. (x+1)/(x-1) is negative on (-1, 1)
iii. (x+1)/(x-1) is zero at x = -1 and
iv. (x+1)/(x-1) is undefined at x = 1.

Therefore, the solution set of the given inequality is (-∞, -1)∪(1, ∞).

**Example 3**
Solve the inequality 1/x² ≥ 5/(3x).

**Solution**
The given rational inequality is not defined when x=0.
Then, the domain of the given inequality is {x∈R| x≠0}.

1/x² ≥ 5/(3x) ⇒ 1/x² - 5/(3x) ≥ 0 ⇒ (3-5x)/(3x²) ≥ 0.

Hence, the given inequality 1/x² ≥ 5/(3x) is equivalent to the inequality (3-5x)/(3x²) ≥ 0, that is, both inequalities have the same solution set.

3-5x = 0 ⇒ x = 3/5.

Then, divide the number line into four different intervals using the numbers 0 and 3/5 as in the table below.

| Interval | 3-5x | 3x² | (3-5x)/(3x²) |
|----------|------|-----|--------------|
| x<0      | +    | +   | +            |
| 0<x<3/5  | +    | +   | +            |
| x>3/5    | -    | +   | -            |

Then, from the given sign chart, observe that:
i. (3-5x)/(3x²) is positive on (-∞,0)∪(0,3/5)
ii. (3-5x)/(3x²) is zero at x = 3/5
iii. (3-5x)/(3x²) is undefined at x = 0

Therefore, the solution set of the inequality (3-5x)/(3x²) ≥ 0 is (-∞,0)∪(0,3/5], which is also the solution set of the inequality 1/x² ≥ 5/(3x).

**Definition 2.8**
A function f that can be expressed in the form f(x) = p(x)/q(x), where both p(x) and q(x) are polynomial expressions and q(x)≠0, is called a rational function.

**Example 1**

**Solution**

b. g(x) = (3x-5)/(√(x+1))² is not a rational function, because (√(x+1))² is not a polynomial expression.
c. p(x) = 3x²+4x-5 is a polynomial function and any polynomial function, p(x), is a rational function, because p(x) can be expressed as p(x) = p(x)/1 and the denominator 1 is constant, which is a polynomial expression.

The domain of a rational function f(x) = p(x)/q(x) is the set of all real numbers such that q(x) is not zero. That is, Dom(f) = {x∈R | q(x)≠0}.

**Definition 2.10**
Given a rational function f(x) = p(x)/q(x):
1. If f(x) → ∞ or f(x) → -∞ as x → a from the left or x → a from the right, then the line x = a is called a vertical asymptote to the graph of f(x).
2. If f(x) → b as x → ∞ or as x → -∞, then the line y = b is called a horizontal asymptote to the graph of f(x).

Consider a rational function f(x) = p(x)/q(x) where:
p(x) = aₙxⁿ + aₙ₋₁xⁿ⁻¹ + ... + a₀, with aₙ≠0
and q(x) = bₘxᵐ + bₘ₋₁xᵐ⁻¹ + ... + b₀ with bₘ≠0

1. If p(a) ≠ 0 and q(a) = 0, then the line x = a is a vertical asymptote to the graph of f(x).
2. If both p(a) = 0 and q(a) = 0, then the graph of f(x) has a "hole" at x = a or we have to make further simplification to decide.
3. If n < m, then the line y = 0 is a horizontal asymptote to the graph of f(x).
4. If n = m, then the line y = aₙ/bₘ is a horizontal asymptote to the graph of f(x).
5. If n = m + 1, then the graph of f(x) has an asymptote y = ax + b, a ≠ 0, and because the line is an oblique line, y = ax + b is called an oblique asymptote to the graph of f(x).
6. If n > m + 1, the graph of f(x) has no horizontal asymptote and it has no oblique asymptote.

**Example 1**
Find the vertical, horizontal or oblique asymptotes to the graph of each of the following functions (if any):
a. f(x) = 1/(x-2)
b. g(x) = x/(x²+3x+2)
c. h(x) = (3x²+1)/(x²+4x+4)
d. l(x) = (x³+3x+1)/(x²+1)
e. m(x) = (x³+x²+x+1)/(x+2)

**Solution**
a. x-2 = 0 implies x = 2. Then, Dom(f) = {x∈R | x≠2}.
Let p(x) = 1 and q(x) = x-2. Then f(x) = p(x)/q(x).
p(2) = 1≠0 and q(2) = 0.
Therefore, the line x = 2 is a vertical asymptote to the graph of f(x).
The degree of p(x) is 0 and the degree of q(x) is 1. The degree of p(x) is less than the degree of q(x). Here, the line y = 0 is a horizontal asymptote to the graph of f(x).

b. x²+3x+2 = 0 implies x=-1 or x=-2.
Thus, Dom(g) = {x∈R | x≠-1,-2}.
g(x) = x/(x²+3x+2) = x/[(x+2)(x+1)].

For x≠-1, g(x) simplifies to g(x) = 1/(x+2).
Therefore, x = -2 is the only vertical asymptote to the graph of g(x) and the line y = 0 is a horizontal asymptote to the graph of g(x), because the degree of x is 1 and the degree of x²+3x+2 is 2. That is, the degree of x is less than the degree of x²+3x+2.

c. x²+4x+4 = 0 implies x = -2. Then the domain of h(x) is {x∈R | x≠-2}.
h(x) = (3x²+1)/(x²+4x+4) = (3x²+1)/(x+2)².
h(-2) = (3(-2)²+1)/((-2)²+4(-2)+4) = 13/0.

This implies the line x = -2 is the vertical asymptote to the graph of h(x).
Since the degree of 3x²+1 is 2 and the degree of x²+4x+4 is also 2, then the line y = 3/1 = 3 is the horizontal asymptote to the graph of h(x).

d. Since x²+1 ≠ 0 for any real number x, the graph of l(x) has no vertical asymptote.
Since the degree of x³+3x+1 is 3 and the degree of x²+1 is 2. That is, the degree of x³+3x+1 is one more than the degree of x²+1, then using long division, you can write:
l(x) = (x³+3x+1)/(x²+1) = x + (2x+1)/(x²+1).

Therefore, the line y = x is an oblique asymptote to the graph of l(x).

e. x+2 = 0 implies x = -2. Thus the domain of m(x) is {x∈R | x≠-2}.
m(-2) = [(-2)³+(-2)²+(-2)+1]/(-2+2) = -5/0 ≠ 0.
This implies x = -2 is a vertical asymptote to the graph of m(x).
The degree of x³+x²+x+1 is 3, the degree of x+2 is 1 and 3 > 1+1. Thus, the graph of m(x) = (x³+x²+x+1)/(x+2) has no horizontal asymptote and it also has no oblique asymptote.

4. Determine the vertical asymptote(s) if any. That is, find a such that p(a)≠0 and q(a)=0. Then the vertical line x = a is a vertical asymptote to the graph of f(x).
5. Determine the horizontal or oblique asymptotes (if there is any).

## 2.4 Applications

Rational formulas can be useful tools for representing real-life situations and for finding answers to real problems. Equations representing direct, inverse, and joint variation are examples of rational formulas that can model many real-life situations.

The following steps are used in solving word problems:
1. Step 1: Understanding the problem
2. Step 2: Setting up the equation(s) or expressions using variables
3. Step 3: Solving the given equation(s) for the given variables.
4. Step 4: Interpreting the results.

**Example 1**
Person A takes 2 hours to plant 50 flower bulbs and person B takes 3 hours to plant 45 flower bulbs. If they are working together, how long should it take them to plant 160 bulbs?

**Solution**
To solve this problem, consider the planting rate of the two persons; that is, how many bulbs each person can plant in one hour.

A: 50 Bulbs/2 Hours = 25 Bulbs per hour
B: 45 Bulbs/3 Hours = 15 Bulbs per hour

Now, combine the hourly rates of the two persons to determine the rate of the work when they work together.

A and B together: 25 + 15 = 40 Bulbs per hour

That is, when A and B work together, they plant 40 flower bulbs in an hour.

From the work formula, W = rt, we have r = W/t, where r is the combined work rate, W is the amount of work that must be done and t is the amount of time it will take to complete the work.

The required information is, how much time it will take to do the required work at the given rate. To do so we have to solve the rational equation:
40 = 160/t

Solving this equation for t gives us that t = 4 hours.

Thus, if A and B work together, it will take 4 hours for them to plant 160 bulbs together.

**Example 2**
A sample of 80g of a particular ice cream contains 15 gram of fat and 20g of carbohydrate.
a. How much fat does 350g of the same ice cream contain?
b. How much carbohydrate does 350g of the same ice cream contain?

**Solution**
a. If the amount of fat in 350g is x g, then x/350 = 15/80.
Solving for x gives us, x = (15/80) × 350g = 65.625g.

b. If the amount of carbohydrate in 350g is y g, then y/350 = 20/80.
Solving for y gives us, y = (20/80) × 350g = 87.5g.

**Example 3**
A large mixing tank contains 100 gallons of water into which 5 pounds of sugar have been mixed. A tap will open pouring 10 gallons per minute of water into the tank. At the same time sugar is poured into the tank at a rate of 1 pound per minute. Find the concentration (pounds per gallon) of sugar in the tank after 10 minutes. Compare the concentration after 10 minutes with the concentration at the beginning.

**Solution**
Let t be the number of minutes since the tap opened. Since the water increases at 10 gallons per minute, and the sugar increases at 1 pound per minute, these are constant rates of change, that is,
- W(t) = 100 + 10t gallons (the amount of water in the tank after t minutes)
- S(t) = 5 + t pounds (the amount of sugar in the tank after t minutes)

The concentration C will be the ratio of pounds of sugar to gallons of water.
That is, C(t) = (5 + t)/(100 + 10t).

Thus, the concentration after 10 minutes is given by:
C(10) = (5+10)/(100+10(10)) = 15/200 = 0.075.

The concentration at the beginning was C(0) = 5/100 = 0.05.

Since 0.075 > 0.05, there is high concentrations after 10 minutes than at the beginning.

## Summary
1. Let p(x) and q(x) be two polynomial expressions. An expression of the form p(x)/q(x), where q(x)≠0, is a rational expression.

2. Given two rational expressions p(x)/q(x) and r(x)/s(x) with q(x)≠0 and s(x)≠0, we define:
   - p(x)/q(x) + r(x)/s(x) = [p(x)s(x) + r(x)q(x)]/[q(x)s(x)]
   - p(x)/q(x) - r(x)/s(x) = [p(x)s(x) - r(x)q(x)]/[q(x)s(x)]
   - p(x)/q(x) × r(x)/s(x) = p(x)r(x)/[q(x)s(x)]
   - p(x)/q(x) ÷ r(x)/s(x) = p(x)s(x)/[q(x)r(x)] (r(x)≠0)

3. A function f that can be expressed in the form f(x) = p(x)/q(x), where both p(x) and q(x) are polynomials and q(x)≠0, is called a rational function.

4. The domain of a rational function f(x) = p(x)/q(x) is the set of all real numbers such that q(x) is not zero.

5. Given a rational function f(x) = p(x)/q(x):
   a. If f(x) → ∞ or f(x) → -∞ as x → a from the left or x → a from the right, then the line x = a is called a vertical asymptote to the graph of f(x).
   b. If f(x) → b as x → ∞ or as x → -∞, then the line y = b is called a horizontal asymptote to the graph of f(x).
