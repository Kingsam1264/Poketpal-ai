### Newton's Second Law of Motion
"When an unbalanced force acts on an object of mass m, the object accelerates in the direction of the applied force with the magnitude of acceleration directly proportional to the net applied force and inversely proportional to the mass of the object."

$$a \sim \vec{F}_{net} \quad \text{and} \quad a \sim \frac{1}{m}$$

$$a = k \frac{\vec{F}_{net}}{m}$$

When k = 1, we write:
$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$$

### Key terms
- **Net force**: The sum of two or more forces (Resultant force)
- **Acceleration**: The time rate of change of velocity. It could happen when a body changes its speed or direction of motion or both
- **System**: A collection of objects, elements or components that are organized for a common purpose. Anything outside the system is the surrounding

## Weight and the Gravitational Force

The gravitational pull on an object is expressed as:
$$\vec{F}_g = \vec{W} = m\vec{g}$$

**Question:** Is weight of a body the same everywhere? Discuss your answer.

### Example 4.1
What is the weight of a 1kg block on the surface of the moon where acceleration due to gravity is only one-sixth of its value on the surface of the Earth?

$$g_m = \frac{1}{6}g = \frac{1}{6}(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2) = 1.6 \text{ m/s}^2$$

An object on the moon weighs one-sixth as much as it does on the surface of the Earth.

### Key terms
- **Friction**: A force that opposes the motion of one surface over another
- **Static friction**: Friction between two contacting surfaces at rest
- **Kinetic friction**: Friction between two surfaces while one slides over the other

The coefficient of friction is defined as:
$$\mu = \frac{F_f}{F_N}$$

The equations for friction are:
$$F_s = \mu_s F_N \quad \text{(static friction)}$$
$$F_k = \mu_k F_N \quad \text{(kinetic friction)}$$

**Note:** $F_k \leq F_s$ and $\mu_s \leq \mu_k$

**Figure 4.19:** Static friction and kinetic friction

### Example 4.3
An 8kg block is placed on a horizontal surface. The coefficient of static friction and that of kinetic friction between the block and surface are 0.4 and 0.35, respectively.

**Solution:**
A) What is the horizontal force just enough to start moving the block?
$$F_s = \mu_s F_N = \mu_s mg = (0.4)(8\text{ kg})(9.8\text{ m/s}^2) = 31.4\text{ N}$$

B) What horizontal force must be applied on the block to keep it uniformly accelerating at 4 m/s²?
$$F_{net} = ma = (8\text{ kg})(4\text{ m/s}^2) = 32\text{ N}$$
$$F_k = \mu_k mg = (0.35)(8\text{ kg})(9.8\text{ m/s}^2) = 27.4\text{ N}$$
$$F_{app} = F_{net} + F_k = 32\text{ N} + 27.4\text{ N} = 59.4\text{ N}$$

### Free-Body Diagram
The most important step in solving a problem by means of Newton's second law. It is a drawing that includes only those forces that act directly on the object of interest.

### Example 4.6
Two blocks of identical materials are connected by a light string on a level surface (Figure 4.25). Assuming no friction between the blocks and the level surface, find the acceleration of the masses and the tension in the connecting string when the string attached to m₁ is pulled to the right by a 36N force. Use m₁ = 4 kg and m₂ = 8 kg.

**Solution:**
$$a = \frac{F}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{36\text{ N}}{4\text{ kg} + 8\text{ kg}} = 3\text{ m/s}^2$$
$$T = m_2 a = (8\text{ kg})(3\text{ m/s}^2) = 24\text{ N}$$

### Example 4.7
For the system of masses in Example 4.6, find the acceleration of the masses if the coefficient of kinetic friction between each of the blocks and the level surface is 0.25.

**Solution:**
$$a = \frac{F - F_{f1} - F_{f2}}{m_1 + m_2} = 0.55\text{ m/s}^2$$

### Example 4.8: Atwood's Machine
Two masses (m₁ = 4 kg and m₂ = 6 kg) are connected by a light cord that passes over a massless, frictionless pulley. Find the magnitude of acceleration of the two masses and the tension in the cord.

**Solution:**
$$a = g\frac{m_2 - m_1}{m_1 + m_2} = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2\left(\frac{6\text{ kg} - 4\text{ kg}}{6\text{ kg} + 4\text{ kg}}\right) = 1.96\text{ m/s}^2$$
$$T = m_1(g + a) = 4\text{ kg}(9.8 + 1.96)\text{ m/s}^2 = 47\text{ N}$$

For a static object: $T - mg = 0$

### Kinetic Energy
$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

### Example 4.14
A 1200kg car traveling at 30m/s has kinetic energy:
$$KE = \frac{1}{2}(1200\text{ kg})(30\text{ m/s})^2 = 540\text{ kJ}$$

### Work-Energy Theorem
$$W_{net} = \Delta KE = KE_f - KE_i$$

### Potential Energy
$$PE = mgh$$

### Conservative and Non-conservative Forces
**Conservative forces** have these properties:
1. Work done is independent of the path
2. Work done through any closed path is zero

**Non-conservative forces**:
- Work done depends on the path (e.g., friction)

When dissipative forces act:
$$W_d = \Delta ME = \Delta KE + \Delta PE$$

### Power
Average power:
$$P_{avg} = \frac{W}{t}$$

Another expression:
$$P = Fv$$

### Example 4.15
A 1000kg elevator car carries a maximum load of 800kg. As the elevator moves upward, a constant frictional force of 4000N retards its motion. What minimum power must the motor deliver to lift the loaded elevator car at a constant speed of 2.5m/s?

**Solution:**
$$T = mg + F_f = (1000 + 800)(9.8) + 4000 = 21640\text{ N}$$
$$P = Tv = (21640\text{ N})(2.5\text{ m/s}) = 54100\text{ W} = 54.1\text{ kW}$$

**Solution:**

## Summary of Unit 4

Newton's laws of motion can be applied in numerous situations to solve problems related to motion.

- **Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)**: In absence of unbalanced force, a body at rest remains at rest and a body in motion keeps moving with same velocity
- **Newton's Second Law**: Acceleration is directly proportional to net force and inversely proportional to mass: $\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$
- **Newton's Third Law**: Forces are in pairs; action and reaction are equal and opposite
- **Friction**: Contact force that opposes motion; depends on surfaces and normal force
- **Static friction > Kinetic friction**
- **Normal Force**: Not always equal to weight; depends on surface orientation
- **Equilibrium**: Net force is zero; body at rest or moving at constant velocity
- **Free-Body Diagrams**: Essential for solving problems with multiple forces
- **Net Work**: $W_{net}$ is work done by net force
- **Work-Energy Theorem**: $W_{net} = \Delta KE$
- **Kinetic Energy**: $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
- **Potential Energy**: $PE = mgh$
- **Work by Variable Force**: $W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{x}$
- **Elastic Potential Energy**: $PE = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$
- **Linear Momentum**: $\vec{P} = m\vec{v}$
- **Impulse**: $\vec{J} = \vec{F}\Delta t = \Delta\vec{P}$
- **Center of Mass**: Point where total mass is assumed concentrated
- **Power**: Rate of doing work, $P = \frac{W}{t}$ or $P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}$
- **Positive Power**: Energy transferred to object
- **Negative Power**: Energy transferred from object
