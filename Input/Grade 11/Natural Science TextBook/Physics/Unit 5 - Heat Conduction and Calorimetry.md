**What is temperature?**

Temperature is a physical quantity that expresses quantitatively the perceptions of hotness and coldness. It is measured with a thermometer. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules of a substance.

**What is heat?**

Heat is the flow of energy from an object at a higher temperature to an object at a lower temperature. Heat is a form of energy and is measured in joules (J). Heat is not a property of a system, but rather energy in transit.

## Heat, internal energy and thermodynamic work

### Key terms:

- **Internal energy** is equal to the sum of internal kinetic energy due to molecular motion (translational, rotational, and energy of vibration) and internal potential energy due to molecular attractive forces.
- **Heat** is energy which is in transit from one body to another as the result of a difference in temperature.
- **Thermodynamic work** is the quantity of energy transferred from one system to another.

## Heat capacity

The heat capacity C of an object is defined as:

$$C=\frac{Q}{\Delta T}$$

where:
- Q is the amount of heat energy transferred to the object
- ΔT is the change in temperature of the object

The SI unit of heat capacity is J/°C or J/K.

### Key terms:

- **Heat capacity** is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of an object by 1°C or 1K.
- **Specific heat capacity** of a substance is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kilogram of the substance by 1°C or 1K.

## Specific heat capacity

The specific heat capacity c is given by:

$$c = \frac{C}{m} \quad \text{or} \quad Q = c m \Delta T$$

where m is the mass of the substance.

## Example 5.1:

Calculate the heat energy transferred if the temperature of 600g of lead is raised from 20°C to 100°C.

## Key term:

- **Thermal expansion** is the change in size of matter in response to a change in temperature.

## Linear expansion

When the temperature of a rod changes from T₀ to T = T₀ + ΔT, its length changes from L₀ to L = L₀ + ΔL.

The change in length is given by:
$$\Delta L = L_0 \alpha \Delta T$$

where:
- ΔT = T - T₀ is the change in temperature
- ΔL = L - L₀ is the change in length
- α is the linear coefficient of thermal expansion (unit: °C⁻¹ or K⁻¹)
- L₀ is the original length

**Table 5.2: Linear expansion coefficients of some solids**

| Material | α × 10⁶ (°C⁻¹) |
|----------|----------------|
| Aluminum | 24 |
| Brass and Bronze | 19 |
| Copper | 17 |
| Glass | 9 |
| Lead | 29 |
| Steel | 11 |
| Concrete | 12 |

## Example 5.2:

A steel bridge is built in several segments, each 20m long. The bridge was constructed when the temperature was 20°C. If a gap of 4cm is left between neighboring segments, what would be the maximum temperature that the bridge can manage before buckling?

**Solution:**

Given:
- T₀ = 20°C
- L₀ = 20.0 m
- ΔL = 4 cm = 4 × 10⁻² m
- α = 1.1 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹

The gap is filled from the two sides, so each side expands by 2 cm.

The temperature change required:
$$\Delta T = \frac{\Delta L}{L_0 \alpha} = \frac{4.0 \times 10^{-2} \, \text{m}}{1.1 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{K}^{-1} \times 20.0 \, \text{m}} = 180 \, \text{K} = 180°C$$

The temperature at which the bridge may buckle is:
$$T = T_0 + \Delta T = 20°C + 180°C = 200°C$$

Such a big change in temperature may occur if there are exceptional moments such as flowing volcano over the bridge.

## Area expansion

When a plate with initial area A₀ at temperature T₀ is heated to temperature T = T₀ + ΔT, its area changes to A = A₀ + ΔA.

The change in area is given by:
$$\Delta A = A_0 \beta \Delta T$$

where β is called the coefficient of surface (area) expansion (unit: °C⁻¹ or K⁻¹).

**Figure 5.9: Area expansion**

The image shows a rectangular plate expanding from initial area A₀ to final area A₀ + ΔA when temperature increases from T to T + ΔT.

### Relationship between α and β

For isotropic materials:
$$\beta = 2\alpha$$

## Example 5.3:

The surface area of one side of a circular brass plate at temperature 100°C is 3850 cm². To put this plate inside a cylinder, you need to cool it to 0°C. What is the base area of the cylinder?

**Solution:**

Given:
- T₀ = 100°C, T = 0°C
- A₀ = 3850 cm²
- α for brass = 1.9 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹

The area of the plate at T = 0°C is:
$$A = A_0 (1 + 2\alpha \Delta T) = 3850 \, \text{cm}^2 \left[1 + 2 \times 1.9 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{K}^{-1} \times (0°C - 100°C)\right]$$
$$A = 3835.37 \, \text{cm}^2$$

In order for the plate to pass into the cylinder, either side of its surface area should be reduced to below 3835.37 cm².

## Volume Expansion

When the volume of an object changes from V₀ to V when the temperature changes from T₀ to T, we have:
$$\Delta V = V_0 \gamma \Delta T$$
where γ is the coefficient of volume expansion.

The final volume is:
$$V = V_0 (1 + \gamma \Delta T)$$

**Figure 5.10: Volume expansion**

The image shows a three-dimensional object expanding from initial volume V₀ to final volume V₀ + ΔV when heated.

### Relationship of γ with α and β

For isotropic materials:
$$\gamma = 3\alpha$$

### Peculiarity of water expansion

The peculiarity of water in its response to temperature changes is that its volume decreases as its temperature increases between 0°C to 4°C. The density of water has maximum value at 4°C.

## Example 5.4:

1. A 200 liter aluminum cylindrical reservoir is filled with Ethyl alcohol (γ = 112 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹) at the temperature of 20°C. What volume of alcohol will overflow if the system is heated to 100°C.

**Solution:**

Given:
- α_Al = 2.31 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹
- T₀ = 20°C, T = 100°C
- V₀ = 200 L

The coefficient of volume expansion for aluminum:
$$\gamma_{Al} = 3\alpha_{Al} = 3 \times 2.31 \times 10^{-5} = 6.93 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{K}^{-1}$$

The difference in volumes:
$$\Delta V_{overflow} = V_0 \Delta T (\gamma_{alcohol} - \gamma_{Al})$$
$$\Delta V_{overflow} = 200 \, \text{L} \times 80 \, \text{K} \times (112 \times 10^{-5} - 6.93 \times 10^{-5})$$
$$\Delta V_{overflow} = 1.681 \, \text{L}$$

2. While working in the laboratory, you fill water in a glass flask that holds exactly one liter at temperature 10°C. You heat the flask, raising the temperature of the water and flask to 30°C. Which material expands more; the glass or the water?

**Solution:**

Given:
- γ_water = 207 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹
- γ_glass = 3α_glass = 3 × 9 × 10⁻⁶ = 2.7 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹

The water expands more, as the result some water spills out.

Let ΔV_s be the volume of water spilled:
$$\Delta V_s = (\gamma_{water} - \gamma_{glass}) V \Delta T$$
$$\Delta V_s = (207 \times 10^{-6} - 27 \times 10^{-6}) \times 1.0 \, \text{L} \times 20°C$$
$$\Delta V_s = 36 \, \text{mL}$$

### Real and apparent coefficients of cubic expansion

When measuring the coefficient of cubic expansion of a liquid in a container, we get an apparent value since the container itself experiences the change in volume. The real value of coefficient of volume expansion of the liquid is given by:
$$\gamma_r = \gamma_a + \gamma_c$$

where:
- γ_r is the real value
- γ_a is the apparent value
- γ_c is the coefficient of cubic expansion of the container

## Example 5.5:

Mercury of volume 10000 cm³, which is filled in a brass container, is heated from 10°C to 80°C. What will be the real and apparent volume expansions of mercury in the brass container?

**Solution:**

Given:
- T₀ = 10°C, T = 80°C
- γ_mercury = 18 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹
- γ_brass = 5.7 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹

The real expansion of mercury:
$$\Delta V_{real} = V_0 \gamma_{mercury} \Delta T = 10000 \, \text{cm}^3 \times 18 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{K}^{-1} \times 70 \, \text{K} = 126 \, \text{cm}^3$$

The apparent coefficient:
$$\gamma_{apparent} = 18 \times 10^{-5} - 5.7 \times 10^{-5} = 12.3 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{K}^{-1}$$

The apparent volume expansion:
$$\Delta V_{apparent} = 10000 \, \text{cm}^3 \times 12.3 \times 10^{-5} \times 70 \, \text{K} = 86.1 \, \text{cm}^3$$

### Thermal expansion of gases

Gases expand much more than solids and liquids with temperature increase, following the ideal gas law.

## What is phase change?

Phase change is the transition of matter from one state to another. The common phases are solid, liquid, and gas. During phase change, temperature remains constant while heat is absorbed or released.

**Figure 5.14: Temperature versus heat diagram**

The image shows a graph with temperature on the y-axis and heat energy on the x-axis. It illustrates heating of ice from -50°C to steam at 100°C, showing plateaus at 0°C (melting) and 100°C (boiling) where temperature remains constant despite heat addition.

## Latent heat

The energy absorbed or released during phase change is called latent heat (L).

$$Q = mL$$

where:
- Q is the heat energy
- m is the mass
- L is the specific latent heat

- L = L_f for latent heat of fusion (solid to liquid)
- L = L_v for latent heat of vaporization (liquid to gas)

### Key terms

- **Latent heat of fusion** is the amount of heat absorbed by 1kg of a substance from solid to liquid - leaving the temperature of the system unaltered.
- **Latent heat of vaporization** is the amount of heat absorbed by 1kg of the substance to change its phase from liquid to gas.

## Example 5.6

Suppose the temperature of 4kg of water is increased from -50°C to steam at 100°C, at standard pressure. Assume the specific capacity of ice is constant, 2100 J/kg·K. How much energy is absorbed by the water? Draw the temperature versus heat added graph.

**Solution:**

Given:
- m = 4 kg
- c_ice = 2100 J/kg·K
- L_f = 3.36 × 10⁵ J/kg
- c_water = 4200 J/kg·K
- L_v = 2.26 × 10⁶ J/kg

1. Heat to raise ice from -50°C to 0°C:
$$Q_1 = m c_{ice} \Delta T = 4 \, \text{kg} \times 2100 \, \text{J/kg·K} \times 50 \, \text{K} = 420,000 \, \text{J}$$

2. Heat to melt ice at 0°C:
$$Q_2 = m L_f = 4 \, \text{kg} \times 3.36 \times 10^5 \, \text{J/kg} = 1,344,000 \, \text{J}$$

3. Heat to raise water from 0°C to 100°C:
$$Q_3 = m c_{water} \Delta T = 4 \, \text{kg} \times 4200 \, \text{J/kg·K} \times 100 \, \text{K} = 1,680,000 \, \text{J}$$

4. Heat to vaporize water at 100°C:
$$Q_4 = m L_v = 4 \, \text{kg} \times 2.26 \times 10^6 \, \text{J/kg} = 9,040,000 \, \text{J}$$

Total energy:
$$Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 + Q_4 = 12,484,000 \, \text{J}$$

## Phase diagram

A phase diagram is a graphical representation of the physical states of a substance under different conditions of temperature and pressure.

### Key terms

- **Triple point** represents the combination of pressure and temperature that facilitates all phases of matter at equilibrium.
- **Critical Point** is the point in temperature and pressure on a phase diagram where the liquid and gaseous phases of a substance merge together into a single phase.

- **Sublimation** is the term for a phase change that proceeds directly from the solid phase to the vapor phase without first making the transition to the liquid state.

## 5.6.1 Measuring specific heat capacity

For any material, we need to know:
- the mass of the substance
- the amount of heat energy supplied
- its starting temperature
- the final temperature

The law of calorimetry states that for an isolated system, the heat energy lost from the hot body will equal the heat gained by the cold body.

### Example 5.7

A 0.05kg bar of unknown metal is heated to 200.0°C and then immersed into a container with 0.40kg of water initially at 20.0°C. If the final equilibrium temperature of the system is 22.4°C, find the specific heat of the metal.

**Solution:**

Using heat exchange method: Q_water = -Q_metal

$$m_w c_w (T_f - T_{c}) = -m_s c_s (T_f - T_h)$$

$$0.4 \, \text{kg} \times 4186 \, \text{J/kg·°C} \times (22.4°C - 20.0°C) = -0.05 \, \text{kg} \times c_s \times (22.4°C - 200.0°C)$$

Solving for c_s:
$$c_s = \frac{0.4 \times 4186 \times 2.4}{0.05 \times 177.6} = 453 \, \text{J/kg·°C}$$

### Electrical method

The electrical method can be used to find the specific heat capacity of a solid or a liquid. Energy is supplied for a known time by an electrical heater of known power.

**Figure 5.17: Finding the specific heat capacity of a solid by electric method**

The image shows an experimental setup with a thermometer, electrical heater of known power, insulation, and solid sample of known mass.

**Keyterm:**

- **Calorimetry** is the measurement of the quantity of heat exchanged between substances.

The energy supplied by the heater:
$$P t = m_s c_s \Delta T$$

where P is power, t is time, m_s is mass, c_s is specific heat, and ΔT is temperature change.

### Example 5.8

A 100W electrical heater, running for 5 minutes, warmed a 0.50kg block of an unknown metal. The initial temperature of the metal block was 20.0°C and its final temperature was 86.6°C. Determine the type of the metal.

**Solution:**

Given:
- P = 100 W
- t = 5 min = 300 s
- m = 0.50 kg
- ΔT = 86.6°C - 20.0°C = 66.6°C

Total energy supplied:
$$E = P t = 100 \, \text{W} \times 300 \, \text{s} = 30,000 \, \text{J}$$

Heat gained by the block:
$$Q = m c_s \Delta T = 0.5 \, \text{kg} \times c_s \times 66.6°C$$

Since heat supplied equals heat gained:
$$0.5 \, \text{kg} \times c_s \times 66.6°C = 30,000 \, \text{J}$$

$$c_s = \frac{30,000}{0.5 \times 66.6} = 900.9 \, \text{J/kg·°C}$$

Comparing with Table 5.1, the metal is aluminum.

### I. Electrical heating to determine the specific heat capacity of a liquid

**Figure 5.18: Determining the specific heat capacity of a liquid**

The image shows a calorimeter setup with thermometer, stirrer, heater, water, insulated base, and calorimeter.

The energy supplied heats both the liquid and the calorimeter:
$$P t = m_l c_l \Delta T + m_c c_c \Delta T$$

$$c_l = \frac{P t}{m_l \Delta T} - \frac{m_c c_c}{m_l}$$

where:
- m_l, c_l = mass and specific heat of liquid
- m_c, c_c = mass and specific heat of calorimeter

### Example 5.9

A copper calorimeter and stirrer of mass 350g contains 227g of a liquid. A 1000W heater running for 1 minute and 15 seconds heated this combination from 20°C to 88°C. Calculate the specific heat capacity of the liquid given that the specific heat capacity of copper is 420 J/kg·K.

**Solution:**

Given:
- t = 75 s
- P = 1000 W
- ΔT = 88°C - 20°C = 68°C
- c_copper = 420 J/kg·°C
- m_c = 0.35 kg
- m_l = 0.227 kg

Energy supplied:
$$P t = 1000 \, \text{W} \times 75 \, \text{s} = 75,000 \, \text{J}$$

Heat gained:
$$Q = m_l c_l \Delta T + m_c c_c \Delta T = (0.227 \, \text{kg}) c_l \times 68°C + 0.35 \, \text{kg} \times 420 \, \text{J/kg·°C} \times 68°C$$

$$75,000 = 15.436 \, \text{kg·°C} \, c_l + 9,996 \, \text{J}$$

$$c_l = \frac{75,000 - 9,996}{15.436} = 4,248.6 \, \text{J/kg·°C}$$

The liquid is water.

### Mixture method

**Figure 5.19: Determining the specific heat capacity of a solid (bolt) using the method of mixtures**

The image shows a calorimeter setup with cotton, thermometer, stirrer, cover, beaker with water, outer jacket, steel bolt, and calorimeter on a tripod stand.

The heat lost by the hot solid equals heat gained by water and calorimeter:
$$Q_{lost} = Q_{gained}$$
$$m_s c_s (T_h - T_f) = m_w c_w (T_f - T_c) + m_c c_c (T_f - T_c)$$

### Example 5.10:

In the above activity, suppose the temperature and mass of the bolt is 100°C and 125g, respectively. Mass of water is 200g, and mass of copper calorimeter and stirrer is 100g. Initial temperature of water and calorimeter is 20°C. If the equilibrium temperature is 25°C, what is the specific heat capacity of the bolt?

**Solution:**

Given:
- m_s = 0.125 kg
- T_h = 100°C
- m_w = 0.2 kg
- m_c = 0.1 kg
- T_c = 20°C
- T_f = 25°C

Heat lost by bolt:
$$Q_{lost} = 0.125 \, \text{kg} \times c_s \times (100°C - 25°C) = 9.375 \, \text{kg·°C} \times c_s$$

Heat gained by water and calorimeter:
$$Q_{gained} = 0.2 \, \text{kg} \times 4200 \, \text{J/kg·°C} \times 5°C + 0.1 \, \text{kg} \times 385 \, \text{J/kg·°C} \times 5°C = 4,200 + 192.5 = 4,392.5 \, \text{J}$$

Setting equal:
$$9.375 \, c_s = 4,392.5$$
$$c_s = 468.5 \, \text{J/kg·°C}$$

## 5.6.2 Measuring specific latent heat

Methods include:
- Electrical method: Energy supplied for known time by electrical heater
- Method of mixture: Mixing water and ice in a calorimeter

### Measurement of specific latent heat of ice using mixture method

**Figure 5.20: A simple experiment to determine the specific latent heat of fusion of ice**

The image shows two setups: (a) ice in calorimeter, and (b) steam condensing into calorimeter.

The heat lost by warm water and calorimeter equals heat gained by ice to melt plus heat to warm melted ice:
$$m_w c_w (T_{0w} - T_f) + m_c c_c (T_{0w} - T_f) = m_i L_f + m_i c_w (T_f - 0°C)$$

### Example 5.11:

The following experimental data are obtained to determine L_f of ice:
- Mass of calorimeter = 0.15 kg
- Mass of water = 0.20 kg
- Mass of ice added = 0.60 kg
- Initial temperature of ice = -10°C
- Initial temperature of water = 49°C
- Final temperature of mixture = 20°C
- Specific heat of water = 4186 J/kg·K
- Specific heat of ice = 2100 J/kg·K
- Specific heat of calorimeter = 400 J/kg·K

**Solution:**

$$0.60 \, \text{kg} \times L_f + 0.60 \times 2100 \times 10 + 0.60 \times 4186 \times 20 = 0.20 \times 4186 \times 29 + 0.15 \times 400 \times 29$$

Solving gives:
$$L_f = 346,045 \, \text{J/kg}$$

### Measurement of specific latent heat of steam

$$m_s L_v + m_s c_w (T_s - T) = m_c c_c (T - T_c) + m_w c_w (T - T_w)$$

where m_s is mass of steam, T_s is steam temperature, T is final temperature.

# UNIT SUMMARY

- **Heat** is energy in transit due to temperature differences.
- Two objects are in **thermal equilibrium** if they have equal temperature and do not exchange heat energy.
- **Internal energy** is the total kinetic energy of molecules plus potential energy from molecular forces. It increases with temperature and phase changes.
- **Heat and work** are two ways to transfer energy. Heat is thermal energy transfer; work is mechanical energy transfer. Both can change internal energy (ΔU = Q - W).
- **Conduction** is heat transfer through collisions between neighboring atoms/molecules.
- **Convection** is heat transfer through fluid movement.
- **Radiation** is heat transfer via electromagnetic waves, requiring no medium.
- **Heat capacity** (C) is heat needed to raise an object's temperature by 1°C: $$C = \frac{Q}{\Delta T}$$
- **Specific heat capacity** (c) is heat needed per unit mass: $$c = \frac{Q}{m \Delta T}$$ (units: J/kg·°C)
- **Thermal expansion** is change in size with temperature change. For solids: linear (α), area (β = 2α), and volume (γ = 3α) expansion.
- **Real vs apparent expansion**: $$\gamma_r = \gamma_a + \gamma_c$$
- **Latent heat** (Q = mL) is energy absorbed/released during phase change at constant temperature.
  - L_f: fusion (solid ↔ liquid)
  - L_v: vaporization (liquid ↔ gas)
- **Phase diagram** shows states of matter vs temperature and pressure.
  - **Triple point**: where solid, liquid, and gas coexist.
  - **Critical point**: where liquid and gas phases merge.
- **Calorimetry** measures heat exchange. The law states: Heat gained by cold object = Heat lost by hot object.
