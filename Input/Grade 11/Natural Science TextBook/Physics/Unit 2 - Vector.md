### Types of vectors

Vectors can be classified based on their direction, magnitude, and orientation:

- **Parallel vectors**: Vectors that have the same or parallel line of action and the same direction
- **Antiparallel vectors**: Vectors that are parallel but point in opposite directions
- **Equal vectors**: Vectors that have the same magnitude and direction
- **Negative of a vector**: A vector with the same magnitude but opposite direction

**Figure 2.4:**  
(a) Parallel vectors - two vectors pointing in the same direction  
(b) Antiparallel vectors - two vectors on the same line but opposite directions  
(c) Equal vectors - identical in magnitude and direction  
(d) Negative of a vector - same size but opposite direction

### Triangle Law of Vector Addition

If two vectors $\vec{S}_1$ and $\vec{S}_2$ are represented by two sides of a triangle in order (head-to-tail), then the third side represents the resultant vector $\vec{S} = \vec{S}_1 + \vec{S}_2$.

### Parallelogram Law of Vector Addition

If two vectors are represented as adjacent sides of a parallelogram, then the diagonal drawn from the common point represents the resultant vector.

### Polygon Law of Vector Addition

For more than two vectors, the resultant can be found by joining all vectors head-to-tail in any order. The resultant is the vector drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector.

$$\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} + \vec{C} + \vec{D}$$

### Example 2.2

A man rows his boat along the direction of flow of a river (downstream). If the boat can sail in still water at 0.50 m/s, and the river flows at 0.30 m/s, what is the resultant velocity of the boat? Let the magnitude of velocity of the river be $A$ and that of the boat be $B$.

$A = 0.30$ m/s

$$R = A + B = 0.30\ \text{m/s} + 0.50\ \text{m/s} = 0.80\ \text{m/s}$$

The direction of the resultant is along the direction of the river flow. Therefore, the resultant velocity is:

$$\vec{R} = 0.8\ \text{m/s, downstream}$$

### Example 2.3

Two men are pushing a block along a horizontal surface by exerting oppositely directed forces of magnitudes 200 N and 100 N respectively, as shown in Figure 2.13. What is the resultant force applied on the block by the two men?

**Solution**

The direction of the resultant is along the direction of the 200 N (larger) force.

$$\vec{R} = 100\ \text{N, toward the right}$$

### Adding Two Perpendicular Vectors

When vectors are perpendicular, the magnitude of the resultant is found using the Pythagorean theorem, and the direction is found using trigonometry.

**Recall**  
Pythagorean theorem: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$  
Trigonometric ratios: $\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$, $\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$, $\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$

---

### Example 2.4

An ant, starting at a point, walked through a distance of 40 cm due South and then 50 cm due West. What is the resultant displacement of the ant from its starting point?

**Solution**  
Placing the tail of the vectors at the origin of coordinates, the vector diagram of the motion is shown in Figure 2.15.

$\vec{S}_1 = 40\ \text{cm, South}$  
$\vec{S}_2 = 50\ \text{cm, West}$

Using Pythagorean theorem, the magnitude of the resultant displacement is:

$$S = \sqrt{S_1^2 + S_2^2} = \sqrt{(40\ \text{cm})^2 + (50\ \text{cm})^2} = \sqrt{4100\ \text{cm}^2} = 64\ \text{cm}$$

Direction of the resultant displacement is shown by the angle $\theta$, where:

$$\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} = \frac{40\ \text{cm}}{50\ \text{cm}} = 0.8$$
$$\theta = 38.6°$$

The resultant is: $\vec{S} = 64\ \text{cm, } 38.6°\ \text{South of West}$

### Components of a Vector

A vector can be resolved into its components along coordinate axes. In two dimensions, any vector $\vec{A}$ can be expressed as the sum of its x-component $\vec{A}_x$ and y-component $\vec{A}_y$.

**Figure 2.16: Vector $\vec{A}$ in terms of its vector components $\vec{A}_x$ and $\vec{A}_y$**  
Shows a vector $\vec{A}$ broken down into its horizontal and vertical components. The x-component is $A_x = A\cos\theta$ and the y-component is $A_y = A\sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle measured from the positive x-axis.

$$\vec{A} = \vec{A}_x + \vec{A}_y$$

The scalar components are:
$$A_x = A\cos\theta$$
$$A_y = A\sin\theta$$

---

### Key Terms

**Component of a vector**: The projection of a vector on the coordinate axes.  
**Resolution of a vector**: Breaking a vector into its components.

### Finding the Resultant of Vectors by Component Method

For vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ with components:
$$\vec{R} = \vec{R}_x + \vec{R}_y$$

where:
$$\vec{R}_x = \vec{A}_x + \vec{B}_x$$
$$\vec{R}_y = \vec{A}_y + \vec{B}_y$$

The magnitude is:
$$R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}$$

The direction is:
$$\tan\theta = \frac{R_y}{R_x}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{R_y}{R_x}\right)$$

---

### Attention

- If both $R_x$ and $R_y$ are positive numbers, angle $\theta$ is above the +x axis
- If $R_x$ is negative and $R_y$ is positive, angle $\theta$ is above the -x axis
- If both $R_x$ and $R_y$ are negative, angle $\theta$ is below the -x axis
- If $R_x$ is positive and $R_y$ is negative, angle $\theta$ is below the +x axis

### Example 2.6

Consider two displacement vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ of magnitudes 100 m and 200 m respectively, with angles as shown in Figure 2.21. Find the magnitude and direction of the resultant displacement vector.

**Solution**  
We first resolve the vectors into their x and y components:

For $\vec{A}$ (50° from x-axis):
$$A_x = A\cos 50° = 100(0.643) = 64.3\ \text{m}$$
$$A_y = A\sin 50° = 100(0.766) = 76.6\ \text{m}$$

For $\vec{B}$ (150° from x-axis, or 30° above negative x-axis):
$$B_x = -B\cos 30° = -200(0.866) = -173.2\ \text{m}$$
$$B_y = B\sin 30° = 200(0.50) = 100\ \text{m}$$

The vector components of the resultant are:
$$R_x = A_x + B_x = 64.3\ \text{m} + (-173.2\ \text{m}) = -108.9\ \text{m}$$
$$R_y = A_y + B_y = 76.6\ \text{m} + 100\ \text{m} = 176.6\ \text{m}$$

The magnitude of the resultant is:
$$R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} = \sqrt{(-108.9)^2 + (176.6)^2} = 207.5\ \text{m}$$

The direction is:
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{176.6}{108.9}\right) = \tan^{-1}(1.62) = 58.3°\ \text{above the negative x-axis}$$

Therefore: $\vec{R} = 207.5\ \text{m, } 58.3°\ \text{above the negative x-axis}$

### Unit Vectors

A unit vector has a magnitude of 1 and points in a specific direction. The unit vector along the x-axis is denoted by $\hat{i}$ and that along the y-axis is denoted by $\hat{j}$.

**Figure 2.21: Unit vectors on the xy-plane**  
Shows the standard unit vectors $\hat{i}$ pointing along the positive x-axis and $\hat{j}$ pointing along the positive y-axis.

Any vector can be expressed in terms of unit vectors:
$$\vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j}$$

For vector addition:
$$\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B} = (A_x + B_x)\hat{i} + (A_y + B_y)\hat{j}$$

---

### Example 2.8

Given force vector $\vec{F} = (12\hat{i} - 16\hat{j})$ N, what is a unit vector along $\vec{F}$?

**Solution**  
$$\vec{U}_F = \frac{\vec{F}}{F} = \frac{12\hat{i} - 16\hat{j}}{\sqrt{12^2 + (-16)^2}} = \frac{12\hat{i} - 16\hat{j}}{20} = (0.6\hat{i} - 0.8\hat{j})\ \text{N}$$

The magnitude of $\vec{U}_F = \sqrt{(0.6)^2 + (0.8)^2} = 1$

### Example 2.9

Vector $\vec{A}$ has x and y components of 4 units and 2 units, respectively, and vector $\vec{B}$ has the corresponding components of -9 units and 3 units respectively. Find:
(a) the vector components of their resultant
(b) the magnitude and direction of their resultant

**Solution**  
Given:
$$\vec{A} = 4\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}$$
$$\vec{B} = -9\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}$$

The resultant:
$$\vec{R} = (4 - 9)\hat{i} + (2 + 3)\hat{j} = -5\hat{i} + 5\hat{j}$$

(a) The x and y components are: $R_x = -5$ units, $R_y = 5$ units

(b) The magnitude is:
$$R = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}\ \text{units}$$

The direction is:
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{5}{-5}\right) = -45°$$

Since $R_x$ is negative and $R_y$ is positive, the vector is in the second quadrant, making an angle of 45° above the negative x-axis.

### Example 2.10

An unknown vector $\vec{D}$ is added to vector $\vec{C} = (-4\hat{i} + 5\hat{j})$ units and the resultant $\vec{R} = \vec{C} + \vec{D}$ has x and y components of -1 and 1 units, respectively. Find the magnitude of the unknown vector.

**Solution**  
Given: $\vec{C} = (-4\hat{i} + 5\hat{j})$, $\vec{R} = (-\hat{i} + \hat{j})$

From $\vec{R} = \vec{C} + \vec{D}$:
$$R_x = C_x + D_x \Rightarrow -1 = -4 + D_x \Rightarrow D_x = 3$$
$$R_y = C_y + D_y \Rightarrow 1 = 5 + D_y \Rightarrow D_y = -4$$

Therefore: $\vec{D} = (3\hat{i} - 4\hat{j})$ units

### Example 2.11

**Traditional Maresha**

A farmer is ploughing the field using traditional Maresha (plow) pulled by two oxen as shown in Figure 2.24a. The two animals are pulling the beam (Mofer) with a force $\vec{F}_2$ of 1200 N at an angle of 40° from the horizontal and the farmer's force $\vec{F}_1$ on the handle (Erf) is 150 N at 60° above the horizontal as shown in Figure 2.24b. Find the resultant horizontal pulling force exerted by the farmer and the oxen on the Maresha. At this point assume all the forces are acting at point K.

**Solution**  
The forces acting on the Maresha are the force exerted by the farmer on the Erf, $\vec{F}_1$, and the pulling force on the Mofer, $\vec{F}_2$.

Both $\vec{F}_1$ and $\vec{F}_2$ have x-components and the sum of these components gives the resultant horizontal force:

$$F_x = F_{1x} + F_{2x} = F_1\cos\theta_1 + F_2\cos\theta_2$$

$$F_x = 150\ \text{N} \cos 60° + 1200\ \text{N} \cos 40° = 75\ \text{N} + 919\ \text{N} = 994\ \text{N}$$

For the whole system to work, the resultant downward vertical force must be slightly greater than the upward forces. If the upward forces on the Maresha exceed the downward forces (which is the sum of weight of the plow and the downward component of the force exerted on the Erf), the Maresha will be lifted above the ground and ploughing will not be possible.

### Multiplying or Dividing a Vector by a Scalar

When a vector is multiplied by a scalar $c$, the result is a new vector whose magnitude is $|c|$ times the original magnitude, and whose direction is the same as the original if $c > 0$, or opposite if $c < 0$.

### Dot Product

The dot product (scalar product) of two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ is defined as:

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the vectors when placed tail to tail.

**Figure 2.25: Vectors placed tail to tail to form angle $\theta$**  
Shows two vectors with their tails at the same point, forming an angle $\theta$ between them.

---

### Key Terms

**Scalar (dot) product**: Product of vectors that yields a scalar.  
**Angle between vectors**: The angle two vectors form when placed tail to tail.

**Figure 2.26: Alternative ways of finding the dot product**  
Shows that $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A(B\cos\theta) = B(A\cos\theta)$, illustrating that the dot product can be interpreted as the magnitude of one vector times the component of the other vector in its direction.

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB\cos\theta$$
$$\hat{i} \cdot \hat{i} = \hat{j} \cdot \hat{j} = 1$$
$$\hat{i} \cdot \hat{j} = 0$$

### Example.2.14: Force and Displacement

Consider a block placed on a horizontal surface and that force $\vec{F}$ is applied on the block to move the block through displacement $\vec{S}$. The work done by the force is defined as the dot product of the force and the displacement. If $\vec{F} = (5\hat{i} + 3\hat{j})$ N and $\vec{S} = (-2\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})$ m, what is the work done by the applied force?

**Solution**  
$$W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{S} = (5\hat{i} + 3\hat{j})\ \text{N} \cdot (-2\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\ \text{m}$$
$$W = 5(-2) + 3(4) = -10 + 12 = 2\ \text{N·m} = 2\ \text{J}$$

### Example 2.15: Force and Velocity

Consider force $\vec{F}$ of a certain machine applied on a body to move the body with an average velocity $\vec{V}$. The power developed by the machine is defined as the dot product of the force and the average velocity. If $\vec{F} = (50\hat{i} + 30\hat{j})$ N and $\vec{V} = (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})$ m/s, what is the power developed by the machine?

**Solution**  
$$P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{V} = (50\hat{i} + 30\hat{j})\ \text{N} \cdot (3\hat{i} + 4\hat{j})\ \text{m/s}$$
$$P = 50(3) + 30(4) = 150 + 120 = 270\ \text{N·m/s} = 270\ \text{W}$$

### Properties of Dot Product

1. **Commutative property**: $\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}$
2. **Distributive property**: $\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = (\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}) + (\vec{A} \cdot \vec{C})$

## UNIT 2 SUMMARY

When a physical quantity is described by a single number, we call it a **scalar quantity**. In contrast, a quantity described by both a magnitude (the "how much" or "how big" part) and direction in space is a **vector**.

Depending on their direction, magnitude and orientation we can categorize vectors as:
- Collinear vectors
- Coplanar vectors
- Parallel vectors
- Antiparallel vectors
- Equal vectors
- Null vectors
- Unit vectors

**Graphical methods of vector addition** make use of translation of vectors:
- **Triangle law**: Vectors are connected head to tail to form a triangle
- **Parallelogram law**: Vectors are connected tail to tail and a parallelogram is constructed using the vectors as its sides
- **Polygon law**: Used to find the resultant of more than two vectors. The vectors are joined head to tail without regard to the order they are taken. The vector drawn from the tail of the first vector to the head of the final vector represents the resultant.

**Analytic methods** of vector addition/subtraction allow us to find resultant of sums or differences of vectors without having to draw them. They are exact, contrary to graphical methods which are approximate.

- The resultant of two vectors in the same direction has a magnitude equal to the sum of the magnitude of each of the vectors and it is directed along the direction of any one of the vectors.
- The resultant of two vectors at right angles has a magnitude equal to the square root of the sum of the squares of the magnitude of each of the vectors, and the direction of the resultant is determined by the angle it makes with respect to a reference line (commonly the x-axis).

A **unit vector** along the x-axis is denoted by $\hat{i}$ and that along the y-axis is denoted by $\hat{j}$.

A vector represented by its components as $\vec{A} = \vec{A}_x + \vec{A}_y$ can also be represented as $\vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j}$.

If $\vec{R}$ is the resultant of $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$, then:
$$\vec{R} = R_x\hat{i} + R_y\hat{j}$$
where $R_x = A_x + B_x$ and $R_y = A_y + B_y$.

The angle that $\vec{R}$ makes with the x-axis is:
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{R_y}{R_x}\right)$$

The **scalar product** of two vectors $\vec{A}$ and $\vec{B}$ is defined as:
$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = AB\cos\theta$$
and also:
$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_xB_x + A_yB_y$$

The scalar product can be used to determine the angle between vectors and to find the component of one vector in the direction of another.
