From Activity 6.1, you have observed that some mappings of objects preserve shape, size or distance between any two points and some other mappings of objects do not preserve shape, size or distance between any two points. Due to this reason, transformations are classified as either rigid motion or non-rigid motion. But in this section, you will focus only on rigid motions.

### Definition 6.1
A rigid motion is a motion which preserves distance. That means, for A and B, AB = A'B' where A' and B' are the images of A and B respectively as shown in Figure 6.1.

**Figure 6.1:** A diagram showing points A and B and their images A' and B' under a rigid motion, demonstrating that the distance between points is preserved.

**Note:** Rigid motion carries any plane figures to a congruent plane figure. That means rigid motion carries triangles to congruent triangles, parallelograms to congruent parallelograms, etc.

Within the rigid motion, there are three main types of transformations that you will learn in this unit. These are translation, reflection and rotation.

### Definition 6.2
A translation is a transformation that occurs when every point of a figure is moved from one location to another location along the same direction through the same distance.

If point P is translated to point P', then the vector PP' is called the translation vector.

You can state a translation formula in terms of coordinates as follows:

Let T = (a, b) be a translation vector, then:

a. The origin is translated to (a, b). That means (0, 0) → (a, b)

b. The image of the point P (x, y) under the translation vector T will be the point P' (x + a, y + b).

## Line Translation

### Example 3
If a translation T takes the point (-2, 3) to the point (1, 2), then what are the images of the following lines:
a. L: 3x - y + 4 = 0
b. L: 4y + 2x + 1 = 0

**Solution:**
Let T be the translation vector, A = (-2, 3) and B = (1, 2). Then:
T = AB = B - A = (1 - (-2), 2 - 3) = (3, -1). Thus, T: (3, -1)

The point P (x, y) is translated to the point P' = (x', y').
Here, x' = x + 3, y' = y - 1.
Therefore, x = x' - 3, y = y' + 1

A translation maps lines onto parallel lines. Let L' be the image of L under T. Then:

a. L: 3x - y + 4 = 0
3(x' - 3) - (y' + 1) + 4 = 0
3x' - 9 - y' - 1 + 4 = 0
→ 3x' - y' - 6 = 0
= L': 3x - y - 6 = 0

b. L: 4y + 2x + 1 = 0
4(y' + 1) + 2(x' - 3) + 1 = 0
4y' + 4 + 2x' - 6 + 1 = 0
→ 4y' + 2x' - 1 = 0
= L': 4y + 2x - 1 = 0

### Note
Generally, a translation vector T: (a, b) translates point P (x, y) to point P'(x', y'). The equation of P'(x', y') can be got by P → P', x' = x - a, y' = y - b.

### Definition 6.3
Let L be a fixed line in the plane. A reflection M about a line L is a transformation of the plane onto itself which carries each point A of the plane into the point A' of the plane such that L is the perpendicular bisector of AA'.

The line L is called the line of reflection or the axis of reflection.

**Figure 6.7:** A diagram showing point A, its image A' after reflection across line L, where L is the perpendicular bisector of segment AA'.

**Notation:** The reflection of a point A about the line L is denoted by M(A); i.e., A' = M(A).

### Basic Properties of Reflection
1. A reflection about a line L has the property that, for two points A and B in the plane, if A = B, then M(A) = M(B). Hence, reflection is a function mapping the set of points in the plane into the set of points in the plane except reflection of points about the axis of reflection.

2. A reflection about a line L maps distinct points to distinct points, i.e., if A ≠ B, then M(A) ≠ M(B). Equivalently, it has the property that, for points A and B in the plane, M(A) = M(B), then A = B. Thus, reflection is a one-to-one mapping.

3. For every point A' in the plane, there exists a point A such that M(A) = A'.

4. If the point A' is on L, then there exists A = A' such that M(A) = A'. Thus reflection is an onto mapping.

Now, consider reflections with respect to the line y = mx and the line y = mx + b for b ≠ 0.

2. Cosine of the sum and difference:
   cos(α + β) = cos α cos β - sin α sin β
   cos(α - β) = cos α cos β + sin α sin β

Using these trigonometric identities, you obtain:
x' = r[cos 2θ cos α + sin 2θ sin α] = (r cos α) cos 2θ + (r sin α) sin 2θ

y' = r[sin 2θ cos α - cos 2θ sin α] = (r cos α) sin 2θ - (r sin α) cos 2θ
= x sin 2θ - y cos 2θ

#### 1. Reflection of a point in the line

### 2. Reflection of a circle in the line

**Note:**
- If the centre of a circle C is on the line of reflection L, then the image of C is itself.
- If the centre O of a circle C has image O' when reflected about a line L, then the image circle has centre O' and radius the same as C.

#### Example 9
Find image of the circle x² + y² - 4x - 2y + 4 = 0 after reflection in the line L: y = x - 1.

**Solution:**
By applying completing square method on x² + y² - 4x - 2y + 4 = 0, you find (x - 2)² + (y - 1)² = 1.

Clearly, the centre of the circle is (2, 1) and it lies on the line of reflection y = x - 1. Therefore, the circle is its own image.

### Definition 6.4
A rotation R about a point O through an angle θ is a transformation of the plane onto itself which carries every point A of the plane into the point A' of the plane such that OA = OA' and m(∠AOA') = θ. O is called the centre of rotation and θ is called the angle of rotation.

### A. Rotation when the center of rotation is about the origin

**Note:**
1. The rotation is in the counter clockwise direction if θ > 0 and in the clockwise direction if θ < 0.
2. Rotation is a rigid motion but the figures may be turned in different directions.

**Figure 6.18:** Diagram illustrating rotation about the origin, showing the direction of rotation based on the sign of angle θ.

#### Example 1
Find the image of point A (2, 0) when it is rotated through 30° about the origin.

**Solution:**
Let the image of the point A (2, 0) be A' (a, b) as shown in Figure 6.19.

**Figure 6.19:** Point A (2, 0) rotated 30° counterclockwise about the origin to position A' (√3, 1).

From trigonometry, you have A' (a, b) = (r cos θ, r sin θ) where r = 2 and θ = 30°.

Therefore, A' (a, b) = (2 cos 30°, 2 sin 30°) = (2 × (√3/2), 2 × (1/2)) = (√3, 1)

Thus, the image of A (2, 0) is A' (√3, 1).

**Notation:** If R is rotation through an angle θ, then the image of A (x, y) is denoted by R(x, y). For example, in the above example, R₃₀(2, 0) = (√3, 1).

### Theorem 6.1
Let R be a rotation through angle θ about the origin. If R(x, y) = (x', y'), then:
- x' = x cos θ - y sin θ
- y' = x sin θ + y cos θ

**Proof:**
From trigonometry, you have:
(x, y) = (r cos α, r sin α) and (x', y') = (r cos(α + θ), r sin(α + θ))

where r cos(α + θ) = r cos α cos θ - r sin α sin θ ... (cosine of the sum formula)
= x cos θ - y sin θ ... because x = r cos α and y = r sin α

and r sin(α + θ) = r sin α cos θ + r cos α sin θ ... (sine of the sum formula)
= y cos θ + x sin θ ... because x = r cos α and y = r sin α

∴ R₀(x, y) = (x cos θ - y sin θ, y cos θ + x sin θ)

#### Example 2

**Solution:**

   = 1 cos 30° - 3 × sin 30° = 1 × (√3/2) - 3 × (1/2) = (√3 - 3)/2

   y' = x sin θ + y cos θ = 1 sin 30° + 3 × cos 30° = 1 × (1/2) + 3 × (√3/2) = (1 + 3√3)/2

   Thus, R₃₀(1, 3) = ((√3 - 3)/2, (1 + 3√3)/2)

b. A rotation of 810° is the same as two consecutive rotations by 360° followed by a rotation by 90° (because 810° = 2 × 360° + 90°).

x' = x cos θ - y sin θ; x = 3, y = -4; θ = 90°
= 3 × cos(90°) - (-4) × sin(90°) = 3 × 0 + 4 × 1 = 4

y' = x sin θ + y cos θ = 3 × sin(90°) + (-4) × cos(90°) = 3 × 1 - 4 × 0 = 3

Thus, R₈₁₀(3, -4) = R₉₀(3, -4) = (4, 3).

### Corollary 6.1
Rotation about the point (a, b) can be divided into the following three transformations:
1. Translation by T = (-a, -b): (x₁', y₁') = (x - a, y - b) → The centre of rotation is moved to the origin.
2. Rotation through an angle θ about the origin:
   x₂' = x₁' cos θ - y₁' sin θ
   y₂' = x₁' sin θ + y₁' cos θ
3. Translation by T = (a, b):
   x₃' = a + x₂' = a + x₁' cos θ - y₁' sin θ = a + (x - a) cos θ - (y - b) sin θ
   y₃' = b + y₂' = b + x₁' sin θ + y₁' cos θ = b + (x - a) sin θ + (y - b) cos θ

Find the image of the point A (3, 4) when it is rotated through θ = π about (6, 5).

x' = a + (x - a) cos θ - (y - b) sin θ
y' = b + (x - a) sin θ + (y - b) cos θ

where (x, y) = (3, 4), (a, b) = (6, 5), θ = π

x' = 6 + (3 - 6) cos π - (4 - 5) sin π = 6 + (-3)(-1) - (-1)(0) = 9

## Summary and Review Exercise

### Summary

#### 1. Transformation of the plane
a. Transformation can be classified as rigid motion and non-rigid motion.
b. Rigid motion is a motion that preserves distance. Otherwise, it is non-rigid.
c. Identity transformation is a transformation in which image of every point is itself.

#### 2. Translation
A translation is a transformation that occurs when every point of a figure is moved from one location to another location along the same direction through the same distance:
- If point A is translated to point A', then the vector AA' is called the translation vector.
- If T = (a, b) is a translation vector, then:
  i. The origin is translated to (a, b). That means (0, 0) → (a, b)
  ii. The image of the point A (x, y) under the translation T = A' (x + a, y + b).

#### 3. Reflection
Let L be a fixed line in the plane. A reflection M about a line L is a transformation of the plane onto itself which carries each point A of the plane into the point A' of the plane such that L is the perpendicular bisector of AA'.
- Reflection in the x-axis, M(x, y) = (x, -y).
- Reflection in the y-axis, M(x, y) = (-x, y).
- Reflection in the line y = x, M(x, y) = (y, x).
- Reflection in the line y = -x, M(x, y) = (-y, -x).
- Reflection in the line y = mx, M(x, y) = (x', y') where:
  x' = x cos 2θ + y sin 2θ
  y' = x sin 2θ - y cos 2θ and m = tan θ

#### 4. Rotation
A rotation R about a point O through an angle θ is a transformation of the plane onto itself which carries every point A of the plane into the point A' of the plane such that OA = OA' and m(∠AOA') = θ. O is called the centre of rotation and θ is called the angle of rotation.
- If R is a rotation through angle θ about the origin, then:
  R(x, y) = (x', y') = (x cos θ - y sin θ, y cos θ + x sin θ)
- Let R be a counter-clockwise rotation through an angle θ about the origin. Then:
  a. If θ = π/2, then R₀(x, y) = (-y, x)
  b. If θ = π, then R₀(x, y) = (-x, -y)
  c. If θ = 3π/2, then R₀(x, y) = (y, -x).
  d. If θ = 2nπ, then R(x, y) is the identity transformation.
  e. Every circle with centre at the centre of rotation is fixed.
