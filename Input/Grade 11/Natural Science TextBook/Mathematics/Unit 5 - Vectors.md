### 5.1.1 Scalars
**Definition 5.1**  
Physical quantities expressed by a certain number associated with a suitable unit without any mention of direction in space is known as scalar. The number describing the quantity of a particular scalar is known as its magnitude. For example, time (15 seconds), mass (20 kg), length (5 km), area (7 m²), volume (10 m³), temperature (37°C).

### 5.1.2 Vectors
**Definition 5.2**  
A vector is a physical quantity which can be expressed completely by stating both its magnitude with particular unit and direction. For example, car movement is usually described by giving the speed and the direction, say 60 km/h northeast. The car speed and car direction together form a vector quantity, velocity of the car.

From Activity 5.3, you can add two vectors geometrically by joining tail to head of the resultant vector **r** = **u** + **v**, which describes addition of vectors and can be found by placing **v** such that its tail is at the same point as the head of **u**. The resultant vector **r**, or addition of vectors **u** + **v**, has its tail at the tail of **u** and its head at the head of **v**, as shown in Figure 5.6, indicating triangular law of vector addition.

**Figure 5.6: Triangle Law of Vector Addition**  
The image shows vectors **u** and **v** placed head-to-tail, with resultant vector **r** = **u** + **v** drawn from the tail of **u** to the head of **v**.

**Definition 5.4** (Parallelogram method of addition of vectors (head-to-tail method))

If **u** and **v** are two vectors, the sum **u** + **v** is the vector determined by translating vector **v** until its tail coincides with the head of **u**. Then, the directed line segment from the tail of **u** to the head of **v** is the vector **u** + **v**.

### Theorem 5.1 (Commutative property of vector addition)
If **u** and **v** are any two vectors, then **u** + **v** = **v** + **u**.

**Figure 5.7: Commutative Property**  
The image shows a parallelogram demonstrating that **u** + **v** = **v** + **u**, with both resultant vectors having the same magnitude and direction.

### Theorem 5.2 (Associative Property of Vector Addition)
If **u**, **v** and **w** are any three vectors, then (**u** + **v**) + **w** = **u** + (**v** + **w**).

**Figure 5.8: Associative Property**  
The image shows three vectors **u**, **v**, and **w** demonstrating that (**u** + **v**) + **w** = **u** + (**v** + **w**).

### Note
- For any three vectors **u**, **v** and **w**, if **u** = **v** and **v** = **w**, then **u** = **w**

- If **u** = (x₁, y₁) and **v** = (x₂, y₂) then **u** + **v** = (x₁ + x₂, y₁ + y₂)

### Note
The product k**v** of a vector **v** and a scalar k is a vector with a magnitude that is |k| times the magnitude of **v**, and with a direction that is the same as the direction of **v** if k > 0, and opposite to the direction of **v** if k < 0. This is called scalar multiplication. If k = 0 or **v** = **0**, then k**v** = **0**.

### Theorem 5.3 (Properties of Scalar Multiplication)
Let **u** and **v** be vectors, let k₁ and k₂ be scalars. Then the following properties are true.

1. **The magnitude of the vector which is multiplied by a scalar is equal to the absolute value of the scalar times the magnitude of the vector.**  
   |k**u**| = |k| |**u**|

2. **Associative Property:**  
   k₁(k₂**u**) = (k₁k₂)**u**

3. **Distributive Property:**  
   i. (k₁ + k₂)**u** = k₁**u** + k₂**u**  
   ii. k₁(**u** + **v**) = k₁**u** + k₁**v**

4. **Identity Property:**  
   1 · **u** = **u**

5. **Multiplicative Property of 0:**  
   0 · **u** = **0**

## 5.2.5 Norm of Vectors

If AB is a vector with initial point (x₁,y₁) and terminal point (x₂,y₂) as shown in Figure 5.20, then its position vector **u** is determined as:
**u** = (x₂ - x₁, y₂ - y₁) = (x₂ - x₁)**i** + (y₂ - y₁)**j**

Thus, (x₂ - x₁) and (y₂ - y₁) are the coordinates of **u** with respect to the base (**i**, **j**).

**Figure 5.20: Vector Norm**  
The image shows vector AB from A(x₁,y₁) to B(x₂,y₂), with its position vector **u** shown, illustrating the components.

The norm or the magnitude of a vector **u** = (x₂ - x₁)**i** + (y₂ - y₁)**j** is given by:
|**u**| = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

### Example 1
Find the norms of the following vectors:
a. (3,-3)  
b. (4,6)  
c. (-4,0)  
d. (0,6)

**Solution**
a. The norm or magnitude of vector **u** = (3,-3) is:
|**u**| = √(3² + (-3)²) = √(9 + 9) = 3√2

b. The norm or magnitude of vector **u** = (4,6) is:
|**u**| = √(4² + 6²) = √(16 + 36) = 2√13

c. The norm or magnitude of vector **u** = (-4,0) is:
|**u**| = √((-4)² + 0²) = √(16 + 0) = 4

d. The norm or magnitude of vector **u** = (0,6) is:
|**u**| = √(0² + 6²) = √(0 + 36) = 6

### Definition 5.5
Any vector that has magnitude which equals to 1 is called unit vectors and denoted by **v̂** (read as v-hat). Unit vectors are generally used to denote the direction of a vector.

If **u** is any non-zero vector then the unit vector in the direction of **u** is given by:
**û** = **u** / |**u**|

For vectors in two dimensions, in general **u** = x**i** + y**j** where x and y are scalars and its magnitude is given by the length or norm of vector OA where |**u**| = √(x² + y²).

In this case, we say that **u** is expressed as a linear combination of **i** and **j**.

### Example 2
If **u** = (1,2) and **v** = (3,4), then find:
a. the unit vector in the direction of **v**  
b. the unit vector in the direction of **u** + **v**  
c. the unit vector in the direction of **u** - **v**

**Solution**
a. |**v**| = √(x² + y²) = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5

   Substitute the given values of **v** and |**v**| into **v̂** = **v** / |**v**|

   Hence, **v̂** = **v** / |**v**| = (3,4) × (1/5) = (3/5, 4/5) = (3/5)**i** + (4/5)**j**

b. **u** + **v** = (1,2) + (3,4) = (4,6)

   The norm of |**u** + **v**| = √(x² + y²) = √(4² + 6²) = √(16 + 36) = √52 = 2√13

   Substitute the given values of **u** + **v** and |**u** + **v**| into (**u** + **v**)̂ = (**u** + **v**) × 1/|**u** + **v**|

   Hence, (**u** + **v**)̂ = (4,6) × 1/(2√13) = (4/(2√13), 6/(2√13)) = (2/√13, 3/√13) = (2√13/13, 3√13/13)

c. **u** - **v** = (1,2) - (3,4) = (-2,-2)

   The norm of |**u** - **v**| = √(x² + y²) = √((-2)² + (-2)²) = √(4 + 4) = √8 = 2√2

   Substitute the given values of **u** - **v** and |**u** - **v**| into (**u** - **v**)̂ = (**u** - **v**) × 1/|**u** - **v**|

   Hence, (**u** - **v**)̂ = (-2,-2) × 1/(2√2) = (-2/(2√2), -2/(2√2)) = (-1/√2, -1/√2) = -**i**/√2 - **j**/√2

### Theorem 5.5 (Properties of the dot product)
If **u**, **v** and **w** are vectors and k is a scalar then:
1. k(**u**·**v**) = (k**u**)·**v** = **u**·(k**v**)
2. **Distributive property**  
   i) **u**·(**v** + **w**) = **u**·**v** + **u**·**w**  
   ii) (**v** + **w**)·**u** = **v**·**u** + **w**·**u**

### Corollary 5.1
If **u** = (u₁, u₂) and **v** = (v₁, v₂) are vectors, then **u**·**v** = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂.

**Proof**  
**u**·**v** = (u₁**i** + u₂**j**)·(v₁**i** + v₂**j**)  
= u₁**i**·(v₁**i** + v₂**j**) + u₂**j**·(v₁**i** + v₂**j**)  
= u₁v₁**i**·**i** + u₁v₂**i**·**j** + u₂v₁**j**·**i** + u₂v₂**j**·**j**  
= u₁v₁ + u₂v₂

From Activity 5.10, recall 2 by 2 and 3 by 3 matrix that you have already seen in previous unit.

The determinant of 2 by 2 matrix 
```
|u₁ u₂|
|v₁ v₂|
```
is defined by u₁v₂ - u₂v₁.

Again, a determinant of 3 by 3 matrix can be defined in terms of 2 by 2 determinants as follows:
```
|u₁ u₂ u₃|
|v₁ v₂ v₃|
|w₁ w₂ w₃|
```
= u₁|v₂ v₃| - u₂|v₁ v₃| + u₃|v₁ v₂|
      |w₂ w₃|     |w₁ w₃|     |w₂ w₃|

Observe that each term on the right side of the above equation involves a number uᵢ in the first row of the determinant, and uᵢ is multiplied by a 2 by 2 determinant obtained from the left side by deleting the row and column in which uᵢ appears. This implies that 2 by 2 determinants and the standard basis vectors **i** = (1,0,0), **j** = (0,1,0) and **k** = (0,0,1) define the cross product of the vectors **u** = u₁**i** + u₂**j** + u₃**k** and **v** = v₁**i** + v₂**j** + v₃**k** as:

**u** × **v** = 
```
|i   j   k |
|u₁ u₂ u₃|
|v₁ v₂ v₃|
```
= (u₂v₃ - u₃v₂)**i** - (u₁v₃ - u₃v₁)**j** + (u₁v₂ - u₂v₁)**k**

Thus, the cross product of two vectors, say **u** × **v**, is equal to another vector at right angles to both vectors, and it happens in the three-dimensions.

### Definition 5.7
Given two non-zero vectors, **u** = u₁**i** + u₂**j** + u₃**k** and **v** = v₁**i** + v₂**j** + v₃**k** then the cross product of **u** and **v** is the vector:

**u** × **v** = 
```
|i   j   k |
|u₁ u₂ u₃|
|v₁ v₂ v₃|
```
= (u₂v₃ - u₃v₂)**i** - (u₁v₃ - u₃v₁)**j** + (u₁v₂ - u₂v₁)**k**

### Example 1
If **u** = **i** + 3**j** + 4**k** and **v** = 2**i** + 7**j** - 5**k**, then find the cross product of **u** × **v**.

**Solution**  
**u** × **v** = 
```
|i   j   k |
|1   3   4 |
|2   7  -5|
```
= (3(-5) - 4(7))**i** - (1(-5) - 4(2))**j** + (1(7) - 3(2))**k**  
= (-15 - 28)**i** - (-5 - 8)**j** + (7 - 6)**k**  
= -43**i** + 13**j** + **k**

Thus, the cross product of **u** × **v** is -43**i** + 13**j** + **k**.

### Example 2
Find |**u** × **v**| where **u** = 3**i** + 4**j** + 5**k** and **v** = 7**i** + 8**j** + 9**k**.

**Solution**  
**u** × **v** = 
```
|i   j   k |
|3   4   5 |
|7   8   9 |
```
= ((4×9) - (5×8))**i** - ((3×9) - (5×7))**j** + ((3×8) - (4×7))**k**  
= (36 - 40)**i** - (27 - 35)**j** + (24 - 28)**k**  
= -4**i** + 8**j** - 4**k**

Therefore, |**u** × **v**| = √[(-4)² + 8² + (-4)²] = 4√6.

### Theorem 5.6 (Properties of the cross product)
If **u**, **v** and **w** are vectors and r is a scalar:
1. **Anticommutativity:** **u** × **v** = -(**v** × **u**)
2. **Multiplication by scalars:** (r**u**) × **v** = r(**u** × **v**) = **u** × (r**v**)
3. **Distributivity:** **u** × (**v** + **w**) = **u** × **v** + **u** × **w**
4. **Orthogonal properties:** (**u** × **v**)·**u** = (**u** × **v**)·**v** = 0
5. **Cross product of the zero vector:** **u** × **0** = **0**
6. **Cross product of the vector with itself:** **u** × **u** = **0**
7. **The cross product of the unit vectors:** **i** × **i** = **j** × **j** = **k** × **k** = **0**
8. **Triple scalar product:** **u**·(**v** × **w**) = (**u** × **v**)·**w**

#### Activity 5.13
Consider a circle with center R₀(x₀,y₀) and a radius of r units as shown in Figure 5.42. Discuss how to show that the equation of the tangent line is (x - x₀)(x₁ - x₀) + (y - y₀)(y₁ - y₀) = r² where (x₁,y₁) is a point of tangency to the circle at R and (x,y) is any point on line L.

**Figure 5.42: Tangent Line to Circle**  
The image shows a circle with center C(x₀,y₀), radius r, a point of tangency R₁(x₁,y₁), and the tangent line L at that point.

Let R₁(x₁,y₁) be a point of tangency to the circle and it fulfills the following equation because it is on the point of the circle: (x₁ - x₀)² + (y₁ - y₀)² = r², r > 0.

A tangent line is a straight line that touches the circumference of a circle at the point of tangency. The radius of the circle r is perpendicular (orthogonal) to the tangent L at the point of contact R₁(x₁,y₁).

If R(x,y) is an arbitrary point on L, RR₁ · CR₁ = 0.

Thus, the equation of the tangent line is:
((x - x₁), (y - y₁))·((x₁ - x₀), (y₁ - y₀)) = 0  
→ (x - x₁)(x₁ - x₀) + (y - y₁)(y₁ - y₀) = 0 ... by corollary 5.1.

Adding both sides of the equation above and (x₁ - x₀)² + (y₁ - y₀)² = r² you obtain:
(x - x₁)(x₁ - x₀) + (y - y₁)(y₁ - y₀) + (x₁ - x₀)² + (y₁ - y₀)² = r²

Factorizing (x₁ - x₀) and (y₁ - y₀) from respective terms, you have:
(x - x₁ + x₁ - x₀)(x₁ - x₀) + (y - y₁ + y₁ - y₀)(y₁ - y₀) = r²  
→ (x - x₀)(x₁ - x₀) + (y - y₀)(y₁ - y₀) = r²

### Note
If the circle is centred at the origin, then the above equation becomes:
xx₁ + yy₁ = r²

### Example 1
Prove the diagonals of a parallelogram meet at right angles if and only if it is a rhombus.

**Proof**  
**Figure 5.45: Parallelogram Diagonals**  
The image shows parallelogram OABC with vectors OA = **u** and OB = **v**, and diagonal vectors OC = **u** + **v** and AB = **v** - **u**.

Let OA = **u** and OB = **v**, as shown in Figure 5.45.  
Therefore, BC = OA = **u** and AC = OB = **v**  
OC = **u** + **v**  
AB = **v** - **u**

OC·AB = (**u** + **v**)·(**v** - **u**)  
= **u**·**v** - **u**·**u** + **v**·**v** - **u**·**v** ... distributive property  
= **v**·**v** - **u**·**u** = |**v**|² - |**u**|²

Now for vectors to be perpendicular, the scalar product must be 0.  
Therefore, 0 = |**v**|² - |**u**|²  
→ |**v**|² = |**u**|² → |**v**| = |**u**|

Hence, when the length of **u** is the same as **v** (i.e., a rhombus) the angle is 90°.

### Example 2

**Solution**

**v** = 70 cos 35°**i** + 70 sin 35°**j**

Simplify the scalars, you get:
**v** ≈ 57.34**i** + 40.15**j** (since the scalars are the horizontal and vertical components of **v**)

Therefore, the horizontal component is 57.34 m/s and the vertical component is 40.15 m/s.

## Summary and Review Exercise

### Summary
1. **Vector**
   a. A scalar quantity is a physical quantity which can be expressed completely by its magnitude and particular units alone.
   b. A vector quantity is a physical quantity which can be expressed completely by stating both its magnitude with particular unit and directions.
   c. Two vectors are said to be equal, if they have the same magnitude and direction.
   d. A zero vector or null vector is a vector whose magnitude is zero and whose direction is indeterminate.
   e. A unit vector is a vector whose magnitude is one.

2. **Addition and subtraction of vectors**  
   Let **u** and **v** be vectors, then the sum **u** + **v** is a vector given by the parallelogram law or triangle law satisfying the following properties:
   a. Vector addition is commutative. **u** + **v** = **v** + **u**.
   b. Vector addition is associative. (**u** + **v**) + **w** = **u** + (**v** + **w**).
   c. **u** + **0** = **u**.
   d. **u** + (-**u**) = **0**.
   e. For any three vectors **u**, **v** and **w**, if **u** = **v** and **v** = **w**, then **u** = **w**.

3. **Multiplication of a vector by a scalar**  
   Let **u** and **v** be vectors, let k₁ and k₂ be scalars, then the following properties are true:
   a. |k**u**| = |k| |**u**|
   b. Associative Property: k₁(k₂**u**) = (k₁k₂)**u**
   c. Distributive Property: (k₁ + k₂)**u** = k₁**u** + k₂**u** or k₁(**u** + **v**) = k₁**u** + k₁**v**.
   d. Identity Property: 1 · **u** = **u**.
   e. Multiplicative Property of 0: 0 · **u** = **0**.

4. **Scalar product or dot product**  
   If **u**, **v** and **w** are vectors, k is a scalar and θ is an angle between two vectors then the dot product of two vectors is defined as **u**·**v** = |**u**| |**v**| cos θ satisfying the following properties:
   a. The scalar product of vectors is commutative. **u**·**v** = **v**·**u**
   b. k(**u**·**v**) = (k**u**)·**v** = **u**·(k**v**).
   c. The scalar product of vectors is distributive: **u**·(**v** + **w**) = **u**·**v** + **u**·**w**.
   d. If **u** = **0** or **v** = **0**, then **u**·**v** = 0.
   e. Two vectors **u** and **v** are orthogonal if **u**·**v** = 0.
   f. If **u** = (u₁,u₂) and **v** = (v₁,v₂) are vectors, then **u**·**v** = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂.

5. **Cross product**  
   If **u**, **v** and **w** are vectors and r is a scalar the cross product of vectors has the following properties:
   a. Anticommutativity: **u** × **v** = -**v** × **u**.
   b. Multiplication by scalars: (r**u**) × **v** = r(**u** × **v**) = **u** × (r**v**).
   c. Distributivity: **u** × (**v** + **w**) = **u** × **v** + **u** × **w**.
   d. Orthogonal properties: (**u** × **v**)·**u** = (**u** × **v**)·**v** = 0.
   e. Cross product of the zero vector: **u** × **0** = **0**.
   f. Cross product of the vector with itself: **u** × **u** = **0**.
   g. The cross product of the unit vectors: **i** × **i** = **j** × **j** = **k** × **k** = **0**.
   h. Triple scalar product: **u**·(**v** × **w**) = (**u** × **v**)·**w**.
