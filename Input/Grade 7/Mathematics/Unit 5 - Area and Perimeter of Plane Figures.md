## Definition 5.1: A Triangle

A triangle is a simple closed plane figure made of three line segments.

<figure>
<span>B</span>
</figure>

**Components of a triangle:**
- The line segments forming the triangle are **sides** of the triangle. AC, AB and BC are the sides of the above triangle.
- The point where the sides of the triangle meet is called **vertex** (plural form vertices). A, B and C are the vertices of the triangle.
- Angles of a triangle are formed by two sides of the triangle meeting at a point (vertex). The interior angles of the above triangle are:
  - The angle at vertex A, ∠BAC or ∠A
  - The angle at vertex B, ∠ABC or ∠B
  - The angle at vertex C, ∠BCA or ∠C

**Key Property:** The sum of interior angles of a triangle is 180°
```
∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
```

A triangle is named by using its vertices. The above triangle is named as triangle ABC, symbolically, ΔABC. It can also be named using any other sequence of vertices like triangle BCA, symbolically, ΔBCA.

**Note:**
1. A triangle has three sides, three angles, and three vertices.
2. The sum of all interior angles of a triangle is always equal to 180°

## Definition: Quadrilateral

A quadrilateral is a simple closed plane figure bounded by four line segments.

A quadrilateral is named by using its four vertices in clockwise or anti-clockwise direction. The above quadrilateral is named as quadrilateral ACDB or BACD or CDBA and so on, but it cannot be named as ABCD or ADCB.

**Properties of quadrilateral ABDC:**
- **Sides**: AB, AC, CD, and BD
  - Opposite side to AB is CD
  - Adjacent sides to AB are AC and BD
- **Vertices**: A, B, D and C
  - Opposite vertex to A is D
  - Vertices adjacent to A are C and B
- **Angles**: ∠A, ∠B, ∠D and ∠C (interior angles)
  - Opposite angle to ∠A is ∠D
  - Angles adjacent to ∠A are ∠C and ∠B
- **Diagonals**: A line segment that connects two opposite vertices. The diagonals are AD and CB.

## Special Parallelograms

Rectangle, Rhombus, and Square are special parallelograms. They all satisfy the properties of parallelogram.

### A. Rectangle

**Definition:** A rectangle is a parallelogram with four right angles.

<figure>
<span>B C A D</span>
</figure>

**Construction of Rectangle:**
Construct a rectangle PQRS using the information given below:
Given: PQ ∥ RS, PQ = 8cm, QR = 4cm, m(∠P) = 90°

**Solution:**
1. Construct line segment PQ = 8cm
2. Construct ∠P = 90° and ∠Q = 90°
3. Mark PS = QR = 4cm and complete the rectangle

**Properties of Rectangle:**
1. Rectangle satisfies all properties of parallelogram
2. The diagonals of rectangle are equal in length and bisect each other
3. All angles of rectangle are right angle (90°)

**Note:**
- A right angle is an angle that measures 90°
- All rectangles are parallelogram, but all parallelograms are not rectangles
- A quadrilateral with congruent diagonals is not necessarily a rectangle
- A parallelogram with congruent diagonals is a rectangle

### B. Rhombus

**Definition:** A rhombus is a parallelogram in which all sides are congruent.

<figure>
<span>R S Q P</span>
</figure>

**Properties of Rhombus:**
1. All properties of parallelogram are properties of rhombus
2. All sides of rhombus are congruent (i.e., AB = BC = CD = AD)
3. The diagonals of rhombus are perpendicular to each other (i.e., AC ⊥ DB)
4. The diagonals of rhombus bisect the angles at the vertices. For example, ∠CDA is bisected by diagonal DB, so that m(∠CDB) = m(∠ADB)

**Note:**
1. All rhombuses are parallelogram, but all parallelograms are not rhombus
2. The diagonals of rhombus are perpendicular and bisect each other

### C. Square

**Definition:** A square is a parallelogram with four congruent sides and four right angles.

<figure>
<span>B C A D</span>
</figure>

**Properties of Square:**
1. Square satisfies all properties of parallelogram, rectangle and rhombus
2. The diagonals of square are:
   - Perpendicular to each other
   - Congruent
   - Bisect each other
3. The diagonals bisect the angles at the vertices. Hence, the diagonal forms 45° with its side.

### D. Kite

**Definition 5.8:** A kite is a quadrilateral that has two pairs of consecutive congruent sides, but opposite sides are not congruent.

<figure>
<span>Kite shape with vertices A, B, C, D</span>
</figure>

**Properties of Kite:**
1. The diagonals of a kite are perpendicular to each other, but they do not bisect each other
2. One pair of opposite angles are congruent (∠A ≅ ∠C, but ∠B ≠ ∠D)

## Definition 5.9: Area and Perimeter

**Area** is the number of square units needed to cover a closed region of a plane figure.

**Perimeter** is the total length of the boundary of a closed plane figure.

### 1. Area and Perimeter of Rectangle

- **Base (b)**: The length of the rectangle
- **Height (h)**: The width of the rectangle

**Area of rectangle** is the product of its base and height:
```
Area = base × height
A = b × h
```

**Perimeter of rectangle** is calculated as:
```
Perimeter = b + h + b + h
P = 2b + 2h
P = 2(b + h)
```

### 2. Area and Perimeter of Square

Since all sides are equal (S):
```
Area = side × side = S²
Perimeter = 4S
```

### 3. Area of Triangle

#### a. Area of Right-Angled Triangle

The formula is derived from area of rectangle. The diagonal of a rectangle divides it into two equal right-angled triangles.

<figure>
<span>h (height) b (base)</span>
</figure>

The area of triangle is half of the area of rectangle:
```
A = ½ × b × h
```

#### b. Area of Acute/Obtuse Angled Triangle

To calculate the area of any triangle, draw a perpendicular line from one vertex to the opposite side (or its extension). This perpendicular is called **altitude** or **height (h)**, and the opposite side is the **base (b)**.

<figure>
<span>B D C A</span>
</figure>

The area of ΔABC is:
```
A = ½ × b × h
```

**Note:** If the lengths of the sides of a triangle are a, b and c, then the perimeter p of the triangle is:
```
p = a + b + c
```

# Circumference and Area of a Circle

**Definition 5.10:** A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are equidistant from a fixed point called the center of the circle.

<figure>
<span>D B O C Circle</span>
</figure>

**Components:**
- **Center**: Point O (circle is named by its center: "circle O")
- **Chord**: Line segment with endpoints on the circle (e.g., CD and AB)
- **Diameter**: Any chord passing through the center (AB is diameter). It's the longest chord.
- **Radius**: Line segment from center to any point on the circle (denoted by r)
- **Diameter is twice the radius**: d = 2r
- **Circumference**: Complete path around the circle (perimeter), denoted by C

## Formula for Circumference of a Circle

### Activity 5.5.1

1. Put a thread slowly and carefully around a circle until both ends join (do not overlap)
2. Stretch this thread against a ruler and measure its length which gives the circumference
3. Calculate the ratio C/d

**Result:** The ratio of circumference to diameter is constant, approximately 3.14159..., represented by π (pi).

```
C/d = π
Therefore, C = π × d
or C = 2πr (since d = 2r)
```

### Example 1:

Find the circumference of a circle with diameter 5cm (use π = 3.14)

**Solution:**
```
C = π × d = 3.14 × 5cm = 15.7cm
```

### Example 2:

Find the circumference of a circle with radius 7cm (use π = 22/7)

**Solution:**
```
C = 2πr = 2 × (22/7) × 7cm = 44cm
```

## Formula for Area of a Circle

### Activity 5.5.2

Draw a circle of radius 10cm and divide it into 16 equal parts. Try to fill a rectangle as shown:

<figure>
<span>Circle divided into sectors rearranged as rectangle with length = half circumference and width = radius</span>
</figure>

**Observations:**
1. The length of rectangle is equal to half the circumference (πr)
2. The width of rectangle is equal to the radius (r)
3. The area of rectangle equals the area of circle

**Formula:**
```
Area = length × width = πr × r = πr²
```

**Alternative form:**
```
A = π(d/2)² = πd²/4 (since r = d/2)
```

### Example 3:

Find the area of a circle with radius 10cm (use π = 3.14)

**Solution:**
```
A = πr² = 3.14 × (10cm)² = 314cm²
```

### Example 4:

Find the area of a circle with diameter 8m (leave answer in terms of π)

**Solution:**
```
A = πd²/4 = π(8)²/4 = 64π/4 = 16π m²
```

### Example 5:

Find the area of a semicircle with diameter 20m.

**Solution:**
```
Radius = 10m
Area = ½ × πr² = ½ × π × (10)² = 50π m²
```

# Applications

At the end of this section you will be able to:
- Apply the concept of perimeter and area of plane figures in real life problems

**Applications include:**
- Decorating walls and estimating painting costs
- Estimating fencing material for gardens
- Estimating roofing material area
- Making various furniture
- Estimating carpet size for floors

### Example 1:

The diagram shows the floor plan of a hotel. Carpet costs Birr 100 per square meter. How much will it cost to carpet the hotel?

<figure>
<span>Hotel floor plan with two trapeziums: dimensions 4m, 6m, 3m</span>
</figure>

**Solution:**
```
Area of one trapezium = ½ × 3 × (4 + 6) = 15m²
Total area = 15 + 15 = 30m²
Cost = 30 × 100 = Birr 3000
```

### Example 2:

A plot of land is shown below. Fencing material costs Birr 250 per meter.

<figure>
<span>Land shape with dimensions: 40m, 50m, 20m, 60m</span>
</figure>

**Find:**
a. Length of fencing material required
b. Total cost

**Solution:**
```
BC = 50 - 20 = 30m
CD = 60 - 40 = 20m
Perimeter = 40 + 30 + 20 + 50 + 60 + 20 = 220m
Cost = 250 × 220 = Birr 55,000
```

### Example 3:

A wall with window and door needs painting. Painting costs Birr 200 per m².

<figure>
<span>Wall: 8m × 3m, Window: 1m × 1.5m, Door: 2m × 1.7m</span>
</figure>

**Find:**
a. Area to be painted
b. Total cost

**Solution:**
```
Painted area = (8 × 3) - (1 × 1.5) - (2 × 1.7) = 24 - 1.5 - 3.4 = 19.1m²
Cost = 200 × 19.1 = Birr 3820
```

### Example 4:

A classroom (9m × 6m) needs flooring with tiles (30cm × 30cm). How many tiles are needed?

**Solution:**
```
Classroom area = 9 × 6 = 54m² = 540,000cm²
Tile area = 30 × 30 = 900cm²
Number of tiles = 540,000 ÷ 900 = 600 tiles
```

# Summary for Unit 5

## Key Formulas:

| Shape | Area Formula | Perimeter Formula |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Rectangle | A = b × h | P = 2(b + h) |
| Square | A = s² | P = 4s |
| Triangle | A = ½ × b × h | P = a + b + c |
| Parallelogram | A = b × h | P = 2(a + b) |
| Trapezium | A = ½h(b₁ + b₂) | P = sum of all sides |
| Rhombus/Kite | A = ½ × d₁ × d₂ | P = sum of all sides |
| Circle | A = πr² | C = 2πr or C = πd |

## Properties Summary:

- **Trapezium**: Exactly one pair of opposite sides parallel
- **Parallelogram**: Both pairs of opposite sides parallel; opposite sides equal; opposite angles equal; diagonals bisect each other
- **Rectangle**: All parallelogram properties + all angles 90° + diagonals equal
- **Rhombus**: All parallelogram properties + all sides equal + diagonals perpendicular
- **Square**: All properties of parallelogram, rectangle, and rhombus
- **Kite**: Two pairs of consecutive congruent sides; diagonals perpendicular but not bisecting
